## Findings

### Finding 1: Legal AI — Access-Trust Gap Is Now Quantified (Today's Data)
**Source:** [Legal AI Access at 83%, But Trust Issues Remain](https://www.artificiallawyer.com/2026/03/23/legal-ai-access-at-83-but-trust-issues-remain/) — [domain trade publication]
**Date:** March 23, 2026 (published today)
**Agent level:** Industry
**What:** Factor's survey (reported by Artificial Lawyer) quantifies a critical gap: 83% of legal teams now have access to AI tools, but only 22.1% report high trust in outputs. High-trust teams are 3x more likely to report positive ROI. Factor CEO Varun Mehta: "In 2026, the market has largely solved for access. The question is whether legal teams can translate genAI into defensible, repeatable impact in day-to-day work." This is the clearest quantification of the access-trust gap in any domain.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (domain trade publication reporting named company survey, with specific metrics)
**Key claims:** 83% access, 22.1% high trust, 3x ROI multiplier for high-trust teams

### Finding 2: Legal AI — Client-Side Revolt Signal from Stockholm
**Source:** ['We Get Nothing From Law Firm AI Use' – Client](https://www.artificiallawyer.com/2026/02/12/we-get-nothing-from-law-firm-ai-use-client/) — [domain trade publication]; [Why Legal AI Adoption Slows After Pilots](https://www.artificiallawyer.com/2026/02/25/why-legal-ai-adoption-slows-after-pilots/) — [domain trade publication]
**Date:** February 2026
**Agent level:** Industry
**What:** At the Pocketlaw/Miramis in-house conference in Stockholm, a senior GC told 500+ other GCs: "We get nothing" from law firms' AI use, predicting costs will only increase. The room implicitly agreed. Meanwhile, in-house teams are moving ahead — every in-house lawyer Artificial Lawyer spoke to was using AI meaningfully, with some already leveraging agents. Separately, Artificial Lawyer reports law firms have reached "a point many don't want to get beyond" — they want AI as a helper for non-billable work but resist AI that handles billable work, because it threatens their business model. This creates a structural tension: clients want efficiency, firms want revenue.
**Evidence level:** Level 2-3 (multiple independent domain trade sources converging on same pattern: business model prevents law firm AI adoption, while in-house teams advance)
**Key claims:** Zero client benefit from law firm AI; in-house teams adopting faster; business model is the blocker, not technology
**Nordic signal:** Stockholm conference, Nordic in-house counsel community

### Finding 4: 95% AI Pilot Failure Rate — Counter-Evidence Convergence
**Source:** [Why Enterprise AI Pilots Fail](https://www.ciodive.com/news/why-enterprise-ai-pilots-fail/808751/) — [domain trade publication]; [Why 95% of AI Pilots Fail](https://www.salesforce.com/news/stories/why-ai-pilots-fail/?bc=OTH) — [vendor press release]; [The 2025 AI Agent Report](https://composio.dev/blog/why-ai-agent-pilots-fail-2026-integration-roadmap) — [practitioner analysis]
**Date:** 2025-2026
**Agent level:** Industry
**What:** The MIT NANDA study finding (95% of enterprise AI pilots deliver zero measurable return) is now cited across 5+ independent sources. Key failure modes converging: (1) agents treated as add-ons rather than workflow-integrated, (2) governance/compliance shuts down pilots at scale, (3) organizational gap between builders and operators, (4) "prompt doom loop" — users rewriting prompts to supply context agents should already have, (5) "confidently wrong results executed at scale" from incomplete context. CIO Dive and Composio provide practitioner-level analysis of failure modes.
**Evidence level:** Level 3 (convergence — multiple independent sources citing same finding with consistent failure-mode taxonomy)
**Key claims:** 95% pilot failure rate (MIT); organizational gap is root cause; governance kills at scale; incomplete context produces confidently wrong results at scale

## Orientation

### Four signals this cycle:

1. **Legal domain is the richest practitioner signal source right now.** Artificial Lawyer produces genuine domain trade journalism with named people, named companies, and specific claims. Today's Factor survey (83% access, 22.1% trust) quantifies the access-trust gap better than anything in any other domain. The Stockholm GC revolt ("we get nothing") is a named-venue, practitioner-witnessed event. Legal is where the "pilot-to-production" story is most visible — and most honestly reported. **Action:** Prioritize Artificial Lawyer and legal domain for practitioner discovery. The legal AI access-trust gap may be a cross-domain meta-pattern.

2. **The 95% pilot failure rate is now Level 3 convergence.** MIT NANDA + CIO Dive + Composio + Salesforce (ironically) + multiple domain trade publications all converge on the same finding and the same failure taxonomy. This is the strongest counter-evidence finding in our research program. **For CTOs:** The question is not "which platform?" but "can we get past the pilot?" Platform choice is downstream of organizational readiness.

3. **The access-trust gap may be the cross-domain meta-pattern (Level 4 candidate).** Legal: 83% access, 22.1% trust. Supply chain: 62% experimenting, but MIT says 95% get zero return. Operations: 23% scaling, but only in 1-2 functions. The pattern across domains: access/experimentation is widespread, but trust/ROI is rare. This suggests the bottleneck is not technology adoption but organizational trust-building. The Epic Healthcare strategy (start with verifiable, low-risk tasks to build trust) may be the playbook that works.

4. **EU Digital Omnibus timing correction.** Previous cycles assumed a March 26 plenary vote based on committee vote timing. This cycle's search suggests the full plenary vote may not be on the March 25-26 agenda. The committee vote (March 18, 101-9) is confirmed, but the plenary vote may be in a later session. **Action:** Monitor April plenary schedules. Do not report March 26 plenary vote as confirmed until agenda is verified.
