## Finding 1: Mollick's "Leadership, Lab, Crowd" Framework — NEW, Level 2

**What:** Ethan Mollick presented a three-element organizational AI adoption framework at UNLEASH America (March 18-19, 2026): **Leadership** (C-Suite teams), **Lab** (dedicated non-technical R&D teams), and **Crowd** (everyone using AI). He also made the case that **HR — not IT** — is the function best positioned to lead organizations through AI transformation. This is a significant reframing: the transformation is about people and work design, not technology selection.

**Relevance to Agents 102:** Directly validates our model. "Leadership, Lab, Crowd" maps almost exactly to our five-step journey: Bootstrap = seeding the Crowd, Champions = building the Lab, the buyer persona = Leadership. Mollick's framework gives us academic legitimacy for what we designed from practitioner experience.

## Finding 2: Mollick's Jagged Frontier — Threshold Effects Reframe — NEW, Level 2

**What:** In a March 4, 2026 conversation with Insight Partners, Mollick described a shift in the "jagged frontier": threshold effects in model improvement can multiply agentic task capacity by 2-3x with small accuracy gains. Many such thresholds have "quietly been crossed." His key business insight: "organizational inertia is a delay, not a moat."

He also noted that Fortune 50 CTOs are saying "We're not signing another five-year contract" — suggesting that enterprise buyers are aware the landscape is shifting too fast for long-term platform bets.

**Source:** https://www.insightpartners.com/ideas/generative-ai-ethan-mollick/ [practitioner direct — Mollick's own analysis]
**Date:** March 4, 2026
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (practitioner opinion, well-grounded)
**Relevance:** Reinforces our "no platform-first" positioning. If CTOs themselves won't commit to 5-year contracts, advising organizations to train people first (durable asset) rather than bet on platforms (shifting ground) becomes even more defensible.

## Finding 5: Nordic Transformation Gap — NEW, Level 2

**What:** The Vivicta Nordic AI Navigator (2026) surveyed ~340 Nordic decision-makers across Sweden, Norway, and Finland. Key findings:

- Most Nordic organizations are at AI maturity level 1-2 out of 5
- Only 31% report having a detailed AI strategy
- Only 2% of Nordic manufacturers have fully integrated AI (vs 8% EMEA average)
- Main barriers: fragmented data, AI/data talent shortage, governance uncertainty
- Finland ranked 15th globally (down from 10th in 2023), Sweden 25th, Norway 26th
- **"Leadership, not technology, is the decisive factor"** — Vivicta CEO Satu Kiiskinen

Sweden leads within the Nordics: 87% of Swedish CxOs have integrated AI into most initiatives, and 77% are investing in employee upskilling (vs 61% Nordic average).

**Source:** https://news.cision.com/vivicta/r/nordic-organizations-are-guarded-optimists-about-ai---new-study-reveals-gap-between-ambition-and-exe,c4322463 [domain trade publication — Cision press release for the research, Vivicta is a consultancy so treat findings with appropriate skepticism]
**Source:** https://www.vivicta.com/en/blog/25/ai-transformation-impacts-nordic-industries/ [vendor analysis — Level 0-1]
**Date:** 2026 (exact date not specified, published Q1 2026)
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single survey, but substantive methodology with 340 respondents + 10 C-level interviews)
**Relevance to Agents 102:** This IS our market. The gap between aspiration (high) and execution (low) is exactly where Agents 102 sits. The finding that only 31% have a strategy and leadership is the bottleneck validates our buyer persona (CEO/CTO who wants to own the transformation). The upskilling data (Sweden at 77%) suggests Sweden may be more receptive to training-first approaches than Finland/Norway.

## Finding 6: Pilot Purgatory Is the Defining Failure Mode — Strengthened, Level 3

**What:** Fortune (March 19, 2026) published "From pilot mania to portfolio discipline" describing the now-universal pattern: companies running 30-900 AI pilots with no path to production.

Key insight: pilot purgatory is not a technology problem but a **governance and ownership problem**. Companies that escape it have cross-functional leadership groups (CIO + CFO + CHRO + business-unit heads) accountable for outcomes.

Cox Automotive cited as counter-example: focused on solving real pain points → 20 AI solutions in production delivering measurable value.

**Source:** https://fortune.com/2026/03/19/from-pilot-mania-to-portfolio-discipline-ai-purgatory/ [general press — but substantive analysis, not a news report]
**Date:** March 19, 2026
**Evidence level:** Level 3 (this pattern is now documented across MIT research, Gartner, BCG, Fortune, and multiple practitioner accounts)
**Relevance:** Pilot purgatory IS what happens without our Step 2 (Make Your Own — change strategy lab). Organizations need to go from "let's try AI" to "here's what we're transforming and who owns it." That strategic clarity is the cure for pilot purgatory.

## What We Did Not Find

2. **No competence-first failure stories.** No examples of "we trained our workforce in AI and it didn't help." Absence of counter-evidence strengthens the pattern.
