## Findings

### Finding 1: Mollick's MBA Experiment — Management Skills = AI Skills (Level 2, single experiment)

Ethan Mollick ran an experimental class at Wharton where executive MBA students (doctors, managers, corporate leaders — NOT engineers) created startup prototypes in four days using Claude Code and Google Antigravity. Result: "an order of magnitude further along the path to a real startup than I had seen out of students working over a full semester before AI."

**The WOW moment for business people:** MBA managers — who had never coded — built working prototypes with functioning core features in days. Their management skills (delegation, evaluation, knowing what "good" looks like, explaining intent clearly) turned out to be the exact skills needed for AI work. Mollick: "they already knew how to manage. All that training, it turns out, was accidentally preparing them for exactly this moment."

**Key insight:** PRDs, design intent docs, and clear delegation ARE AI prompts. Management training is accidentally AI training. This is a powerful WOW delivery mechanism for non-engineers because it validates their EXISTING skills rather than requiring new ones.

**Source:** [practitioner direct] Ethan Mollick, "Management as AI superpower," One Useful Thing, January 27, 2026. https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/management-as-ai-superpower

**Evidence level:** Level 2 — single experiment, one cohort, one instructor. But from a highly credible practitioner (Wharton professor, published AI researcher). Needs replication to reach Level 3.

### Finding 2: Microsoft/HBR Peer Influence Study — Peers Beat Leadership Communication (Level 2, rigorous single study)

Survey of 557 U.S.-based information workers (July 2025). Researchers: Nancy Baym, Eleanor Dillon, Sonia Jaffe (Microsoft Research). Published HBR March 3, 2026.

**Key statistics:**
- Peer influence: +8.9 percentage points on heavy AI usage
- Facilitating conditions (tools, IT support, training): +8.5pp
- Peer influence on advanced AI agent use: +10.4pp vs. 6.1pp for facilitating conditions
- Leadership communication: **0pp direct effect** on AI usage (when controlling for peer factors)
- Among top-quartile AI users: 88% cited peers as influential vs. 50% of bottom quartile

**Critical nuance:** Leadership communication has NO direct effect on usage. But it has an INDIRECT effect: each standard deviation of leader encouragement produces a 0.38 SD increase in peer influence. Leaders create conditions for peers to teach each other. The actual behavior change is peer-to-peer.

**Connection to experience-first sequence:** Peer influence works because peers deliver the WOW through proximity. "Hearing another coworker telling me how he used an AI...was a huge incentive." This is experiential proof from a trusted source — not a strategy deck, not a CEO email. The WOW transfers person-to-person, not top-down.

**Source:** [academic/research] Nancy Baym, Eleanor Dillon, Sonia Jaffe, "Peer Influence Can Make or Break Your AI Rollout," Harvard Business Review, March 3, 2026. https://hbr.org/2026/03/peer-influence-can-make-or-break-your-ai-rollout

**Evidence level:** Level 2 — single study, 557 respondents, U.S.-only. But rigorous methodology from Microsoft Research, published in HBR.

### Finding 3: Coursera "AI Sparks" — Voluntary Peer WOW Delivery at Scale (Level 2, single company)

Coursera CEO Greg Hart runs monthly "AI Sparks" sessions — voluntary, peer-led meetings where employees at any level share how they're using AI in their jobs. No mandates. No goalposts. Staff "encouraged to experiment with AI as they see fit, currently without any goalposts in place for what they should be trying to achieve."

**Result:** "Those are by far the most well-attended and popular meetings that we do at the company."

**Connection to experience-first:** AI Sparks is a WOW delivery mechanism. Peers show role-specific demonstrations. Attendees see their own future ("if she can do it in marketing, I can do it in finance"). No strategy deck. No mandate. The WOW creates pull, not push.

Hart explicitly rejects the "stick approach": mandates "might achieve short-term goals but risk failing the long-term objective of building an organization that is much more nimble and resilient."

**Source:** [general press — CEO interview, substantive] Fortune, "Greg Hart: Coursera CEO on the lessons he learned from Amazon's Jeff Bezos and Andy Jassy," December 26, 2025. https://fortune.com/2025/12/26/coursera-ceo-greg-hart-amazon-leadership-lessons-bezos/

**Evidence level:** Level 2 — single company, CEO self-report. But specific mechanism, specific result (most well-attended meetings), and explicit rationale.

### Finding 4: Workera — Skeptics Convert Through Operational Proof, Not Messaging (Level 1-2)

Workera's Taylor Sullivan: "Skeptics convert when they see role-specific proof of AI impact" — cycle time drops, rework decreases, error rates maintained or decreased. "Messaging won't win over skeptics; workforce conditions have to change first."

**Key distinction:** Skeptics are "rational professionals, not technophobes." They want "concrete proof that their work will benefit." Not prompt tips — "professional judgment: when to use AI, when not to, how to verify quickly, how to spot confident mistakes."

**Connection to experience-first:** This is the "sees their own future" step. Generic training or vision decks don't convert skeptics. Role-specific operational proof does — because the skeptic can see THEIR workflow improving, not someone else's.

**Source:** [vendor blog — Workera is a training platform, so this is somewhat self-serving but substantive analysis] Workera blog, "AI Adoption Will Remain Uneven in 2026," Taylor Sullivan. https://www.workera.ai/blog/ai-adoption-will-remain-uneven-in-2026-heres-why-and-how-to-fix-it

**Evidence level:** Level 1 — practitioner opinion with logical framework but no experimental data. Workera is a vendor, so claims about training effectiveness are self-serving. However, the mechanism described (role-specific proof > generic messaging) aligns with Findings 2 and 3.

### Finding 5: BCG "AI at Work 2025" — The Five-Hour Training Threshold (Level 2-3, large survey)

BCG's third annual survey (June 2025), global scope:

**Key statistics:**
- 79% of employees with 5+ hours of training became regular AI users vs. 67% with less
- Only 33% of employees say they've been properly trained
- 18% of regular AI users received NO training at all
- In-person, hands-on training correlates with significantly higher adoption than online-only
- 76% of leaders/managers use AI weekly, but frontline employees stalled at 51% — the "silicon ceiling"

**The training-experience connection:** "While workshops lay important groundwork, the real transformation happens when employees apply new skills to their actual work." Organizations need "structured opportunities for staff to practice using AI in the core of their work, with coaching support."

**Source:** [vendor analysis — BCG is a consultancy but this is survey data, not prediction] BCG, "AI at Work 2025: Momentum Builds, but Gaps Remain," June 2025. https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/ai-at-work-momentum-builds-but-gaps-remain

**Evidence level:** Level 2 approaching 3 — large global survey, third annual edition, but consultancy-produced (BCG has skin in the training game). The 5-hour threshold and in-person preference are specific, testable claims.

### Finding 6: McKinsey — Workflow Redesigners 2.8x More Likely High Performers (Level 2, large survey)

McKinsey's State of AI 2025 (March 2025): AI high performers are 2.8x more likely to report fundamental workflow redesign (55% vs. 20% of others). Only 6% of organizations qualify as high performers. 88% use AI in at least one function, but nearly two-thirds remain in pilot mode.

**Connection to experience-first:** The 2.8x finding suggests workflow redesign IS the mechanism, not tool deployment. But workflow redesign requires competence — you can't redesign what you don't understand. This supports the sequence: WOW (see possibility) → competence (learn to use) → redesign (apply to own workflow) → high performance.

**Source:** [vendor analysis — McKinsey is a consultancy] McKinsey, "The state of AI in 2025: Agents, innovation, and transformation," March 2025. https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai

**Evidence level:** Level 2 — large survey but McKinsey-produced. The 2.8x correlation doesn't prove causation (maybe high performers were already redesigning workflows for other reasons). Still, the gap is striking.

### Finding 7: The Perception Gap — Leaders 2x Off the Mark (Level 2)

HBR/BCG/Columbia study (November 2025): 76% of executives believe employees are enthusiastic about AI. Only 31% of individual contributors actually are. Leaders are more than 2x off the mark on employee sentiment.

**Connection to experience-first:** This gap explains why vision-first fails. Leaders who think employees are excited deliver strategy decks and expect adoption. But employees are anxious, uncertain, and overwhelmed. The WOW moment is the bridge — it converts anxiety into excitement through direct experience, not through messaging about a future the employee hasn't seen.

**Source:** [academic/research + consultancy] Deborah Lovich (BCG) and Stephan Meier (Columbia Business School), "Leaders Assume Employees Are Excited About AI. They're Wrong." HBR, November 2025. https://hbr.org/2025/11/leaders-assume-employees-are-excited-about-ai-theyre-wrong

### Finding 8: Infosys/MIT — Psychological Safety as Prerequisite (Level 2)

Infosys and MIT Technology Review Insights (December 2025): 83% of business leaders say psychological safety directly impacts AI initiative success. 22% have personally hesitated to lead an AI project due to fear of blame. Only 39% rate their organization's psychological safety as "very high."

**Connection to experience-first:** Psychological safety is the PRECONDITION for the WOW to work. If people fear blame for experimenting, they won't experiment — and without experimentation, there's no WOW moment. The sequence might actually be: psychological safety → WOW → competence → sees future → acts.

**Source:** [vendor press release — Infosys is a vendor, MIT Technology Review Insights is a sponsored research unit] PRNewswire / Infosys, December 2025. https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/infosys-and-mit-technology-review-insights-report-reveals-the-critical-role-of-psychological-safety-in-driving-ai-initiatives--with-83-of-business-leaders-reporting-a-measurable-impact-302643644.html

**Evidence level:** Level 1-2 — sponsored research (Infosys-funded), survey methodology unclear, but the 83% and 22% numbers are specific. Treat as directional, not definitive.

### Finding 9: Mandate Approaches — Mixed Evidence, Strong Counter-Cases (Level 2-3)

Three mandate case studies from 2025:

**Shopify (mandate that worked):** CEO Tobi Lutke, April 2025: "Reflexive AI usage is now a baseline expectation." Managers must prove AI can't do a job before hiring. AI use becomes part of performance reviews. Result: 31% revenue growth in 2025, $11.56B full year. Eight months later, "the rest of the industry is adopting the same metric."

**Duolingo (mandate that backfired):** CEO Luis von Ahn, April 2025: "AI-first." Cut 10% contractors, told teams to automate or lose headcount. Result: massive backlash, 400K+ TikTok followers lost, CEO forced to backtrack within weeks. "I do not see AI as replacing what our employees do."

**Fiverr (mandate as cover for layoffs):** CEO Micha Kaufman, April 2025: warned employees to upskill or "face a career change." Five months later: 30% workforce cut. Result: destroyed trust.

**The pattern:** Mandates work ONLY when the leader personally practices (Lutke codes with AI daily and shared his memo publicly) AND the mandate empowers rather than threatens. Mandates fail when used as cost-cutting cover (Fiverr) or when the human impact is an afterthought (Duolingo).

**Sources:**
- [general press] Shopify: multiple sources including CNBC (April 2025), Inc Magazine, MIT CDO. https://www.cnbc.com/2025/04/07/shopify-ceo-prove-ai-cant-do-jobs-before-asking-for-more-headcount.html
- [general press] Duolingo: Fortune (May 2025, August 2025). https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/duolingo-ceo-admits-controversial-ai-memo-did-not-give-enough-context-insists-company-never-laid-off-full-time-employees/
- [general press] Fiverr: Fortune (March 2026), IT Pro. https://fortune.com/2026/03/25/ai-integration-fiverr-ceo-micha-kaufman-layoffs-meta/

### Finding 10: Counter-Evidence — Top-Down Strategy Does Correlate with Success (Level 1-2)

Deloitte's State of AI 2026: Companies with a formal AI strategy report 80% implementation success vs. 37% without one. Top-down programs where "senior leadership picks the spots for focused AI investments" outperform bottom-up crowdsourcing.

Deloitte-HKU AI Adoption Index 2026: Nearly half of respondents say realized returns fall short of expectations. More than one-third still exploring, 56% in limited implementation. The biggest barriers are organizational, not technical: siloed departments (33%), lack of quick wins (32%), poor data (31%).

**This is real counter-evidence.** Vision-first / strategy-first approaches DO correlate with better outcomes at the enterprise level. The question is whether "strategy" and "experience-first" are actually in tension, or whether they're complementary — strategy sets direction, experience creates adoption within that direction.

**Sources:**
- [vendor analysis] Deloitte, "State of AI in the Enterprise 2026." https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/what-we-do/capabilities/applied-artificial-intelligence/content/state-of-ai-in-the-enterprise.html

## Synthesis: Can This Reach Level 3?

**Current assessment: Level 3 emerging — approaching convergence but not yet there.**

The evidence from 7+ independent sources (Mollick, Microsoft/HBR, Coursera, BCG, McKinsey, Workera, Infosys/MIT) converges on a consistent pattern, though none of them explicitly name the full four-step sequence:

| Step in Sequence | Supporting Evidence | Evidence Level |
|---|---|---|
| **WOW** (experience creates desire) | Mollick MBA experiment, Coursera AI Sparks, BCG 5-hour threshold | L2 (multiple independent) |
| **Competence** (doing builds skill) | BCG training data, McKinsey workflow redesign correlation | L2-3 |
| **Sees future** (person maps to own role) | Workera role-specific proof, HBR peer influence (+8.9pp) | L2 |
| **Acts** (person changes behavior) | McKinsey 2.8x high performers, Shopify mandate success | L2 |

**What makes this almost Level 3:** Seven independent sources — academic, corporate research, practitioner — all point to the same underlying mechanism: direct experience drives adoption more than top-down communication. No single source describes the full four-step sequence, but they each validate different steps.

**What keeps it at Level 2:** No one has formally named or tested this as a complete sequence. Mollick describes the WOW. BCG describes the training threshold. Microsoft/HBR describes peer transmission. McKinsey describes the outcome. But nobody has connected them into "WOW → competence → sees future → acts" as a testable framework. The practitioner who validated it across two cohorts (NPS 80-90%) is the only person who has run it as a deliberate methodology.

**The counter-evidence is real but not contradictory.** Deloitte's finding that top-down strategy correlates with success (80% vs. 37%) doesn't contradict experience-first — it suggests the sequence might be: **Strategy sets direction + Experience-first creates adoption.** The Shopify case supports this: Lutke set the mandate (strategy) but also practices daily and shared publicly (experience/WOW). The failures (Duolingo, Fiverr) had strategy without experience.

### The Resolved Sequence (hypothesis)

The evidence suggests the full sequence may be:

1. **Psychological safety** (precondition) — Infosys/MIT: 83% say it impacts AI success
2. **Leadership creates conditions** (not direct adoption) — HBR: 0pp direct effect, but 0.38 SD boost to peer influence
3. **WOW moment** (delivered by peer, not CEO) — Mollick, Coursera AI Sparks, BCG in-person training
4. **Competence through doing** (5+ hours threshold) — BCG: 79% regular users with 5+ hours vs. 67%
5. **Sees own future** (role-specific proof) — Workera, peer influence mechanism
6. **Workflow redesign** (the actual value creation) — McKinsey: 2.8x high performers
7. **Acts / sustains** — becomes self-reinforcing through peer cascade

This is more nuanced than the original four-step model. The leadership role is INDIRECT — creating safety and conditions, not driving adoption directly.

## What I Looked For But Did Not Find

1. **No one has formally named this reversed sequence.** Searched extensively for "experience first change management," "reversed adoption sequence," "try first understand later" in organizational psychology and adult learning theory. Kolb's experiential learning cycle (1984) describes experience-before-theory in general learning, but no one has applied it specifically to AI/technology adoption as a named methodology. This is a gap — and an opportunity.

2. **No replication of Mollick's MBA experiment.** Single cohort, single instructor. No other business school or training program has published comparable results with non-engineers building AI prototypes. The finding is compelling but unreplicated.

3. **No direct measurement of "WOW moment" effectiveness.** Everyone talks about hands-on experience driving adoption, but no study specifically measures the emotional/motivational impact of the first successful AI interaction. The WOW is described anecdotally (Mollick's "order of magnitude," Coursera's "most well-attended meetings") but never measured as a variable.

4. **No Nordic-specific evidence.** None of the sources are Nordic-origin or Nordic-specific. The practitioner's two-cohort validation (50 engineers, 12 business people, NPS 80-90%) at a Finnish company would be the first Nordic data point if published.

5. **No study comparing experience-first vs. vision-first as deliberate A/B test.** The counter-evidence (Deloitte 80% vs. 37%) measures strategy presence vs. absence, not experience-first vs. vision-first as competing approaches. Nobody has run the experiment.

6. **No formal theory of the "WOW threshold."** Did the WOW become possible only with GPT-4/Claude 3.5 and above? Mollick's experiment used Claude Code (late 2025 / early 2026 vintage). The METR finding that AI task duration doubles every 7 months suggests the WOW threshold is recent — pre-2024 models couldn't sustain the multi-step work that creates the WOW for non-engineers. But this is inference, not evidence.
