## Findings

### Finding 1: The Future Society — First Comprehensive Analysis of AI Agents Under the EU AI Act
**Source:** [https://thefuturesociety.org/aiagentsintheeu/](https://thefuturesociety.org/aiagentsintheeu/) — [academic/research]
**Date:** June 2025 (updated)
**Agent level:** Company (regulatory framework)
**What:** The AI Act was not written for AI agents but does apply to them. Most current agents rely on GPAI models with systemic risk, triggering provider obligations. The Act's static compliance model (pre-market conformity, role-based obligations) is structurally mismatched with dynamic agent behavior. Ten specific governance measures proposed across three roles (model provider, system provider, deployer). Critically: **15 months after the Act entered force, the AI Office has published zero guidance specifically addressing AI agents, autonomous tool use, or runtime behavior.** MEP Lagodinsky formally asked the Commission in Sept 2025 to clarify — no response found.
**Evidence level:** Level 1 (expert analysis, not deployment data)
**Key claims:**
- AI Act applies to agents but was not designed for them
- Zero AI Office guidance on agents after 15 months
- Static conformity assessment fails when agents select tools at runtime
- Ten measures proposed across four pillars (risk assessment, transparency, technical controls, human oversight)

### Finding 2: "Agentic Tool Sovereignty" — Cross-Border Agent Actions Have No Legal Framework
**Source:** [https://www.europeanlawblog.eu/pub/dq249o3c](https://www.europeanlawblog.eu/pub/dq249o3c) — [practitioner analysis]
**Date:** Late 2025 / early 2026
**Agent level:** Company
**What:** An AI recruitment agent in Paris could autonomously invoke a US psychometric API, UK verification service, Singapore skills platform, and Swiss salary tool in under five seconds — triggering four jurisdictional violations. The AI Act has no mechanism for when a system that passed conformity assessment later adds autonomous agent capabilities. The author defines "Agentic Tool Sovereignty" (ATS) — the inability of states to maintain lawful control over how AI systems autonomously invoke cross-border tools. This is structural, not a policy gap.
**Evidence level:** Level 1 (legal analysis)
**Key claims:**
- 20M EUR in GDPR fines for cross-border AI violations already issued (OpenAI 15M, Replika 5M)
- No AI Office guidance exists for autonomous tool use
- Act's "described in advance" requirement fails for runtime tool selection

### Finding 3: Singapore Published World's First Agentic AI Governance Framework (Jan 2026)
**Source:** [https://www.imda.gov.sg/resources/press-releases-factsheets-and-speeches/press-releases/2026/new-model-ai-governance-framework-for-agentic-ai](https://www.imda.gov.sg/resources/press-releases-factsheets-and-speeches/press-releases/2026/new-model-ai-governance-framework-for-agentic-ai) — [academic/research] (government framework)
**Date:** January 22, 2026
**Agent level:** Company
**What:** Announced at WEF Davos by Minister Josephine Teo. Four dimensions: (1) Assessing and bounding risks upfront (autonomy level, access scope, action reversibility), (2) Making humans meaningfully accountable, (3) Technical controls (kill switches, purpose binding, sandboxed environments, whitelisted services), (4) End-user responsibility through transparency. Living document, open for public feedback. This is the reference framework practitioners are using to fill the gap while EU AI Act guidance remains absent.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (government framework, first of its kind)
**Key claims:**
- First governance framework specifically for agentic AI globally
- Four dimensions of agent governance
- Compliance voluntary but organizations remain legally accountable

## Orientation

**The regulatory-practitioner gap IS the story for EU AI Act.** Frameworks exist (Singapore MGF, OWASP Agentic Top 10). Standards developing (NIST CAISI). Finland is operationally ahead on enforcement infrastructure. But the ground truth: organizations deploy agents without governance (88% incident rate, 14% with full approval), and zero credible "here's how to actually do agent governance" practitioner accounts exist. The Act's structural problem — static conformity assessment cannot govern runtime tool selection — will likely require rethinking the compliance model, not just guidance.

**Business-domain practitioners are publishing, but thin and concentrated.** SaaStr/Lemkin is the strongest business agent deployment evidence found — real revenue, specific numbers, multiple independent coverage. Leapfin/Luca shows constrained agent architecture works for accounting. Virat Singh shows finance research agents have massive community traction (49K+ stars). But: zero HR, zero compliance, zero supply chain practitioners. The "builder" community publishes about coding agents on X.com. Business-domain deployers publish almost nowhere publicly.

**Four emerging convergence patterns from practitioner discovery:**
1. "Training > Tooling" — Lemkin, Quintero, and others independently say agent quality depends more on training/context than platform choice
2. "Small human core + agent fleet = full team" — SaaStr (1.2 + 20 agents), Klarna (AI = 850 agents), Every (15 people)
3. "Agents degrade without active management" — Klarna quality suffered, Lemkin spends 15-20 hrs/week managing
4. "Business knowledge > coding skill" — Siemiatkowski, Quintero, Raviv all independently say domain expertise is the bottleneck

**For our positioning:** The EU AI Act governance gap + the practitioner silence on governance implementation = a positioning opportunity. Module 4 (Security) and the Agent Platform Advisory can address "how do you actually govern agents in practice" ahead of August 2026 enforcement. The SaaStr evidence strengthens Pattern 12 (competence creates pull) — Lemkin explicitly says "training is more important than picking the perfect vendor."
