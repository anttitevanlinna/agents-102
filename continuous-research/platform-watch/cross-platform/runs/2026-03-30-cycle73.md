## Findings

### Finding 1: DORA 2025 — AI Amplifies Existing Conditions, Does Not Create Them
**Source:** https://dora.dev/research/2025/dora-report/ — [academic/research]
**Additional coverage:** https://www.infoq.com/news/2026/03/ai-dora-report/ — [domain trade publication]
**Date:** 2025-2026
**What:** The 2025 DORA report (Google's annual State of DevOps research) finds that "AI does not automatically improve software delivery performance. Instead, it acts as a multiplier of existing engineering conditions, strengthening high-performing teams while exposing weaknesses in organizations with fragmented processes." The report identifies seven critical capabilities that must be in place: clear AI strategy, healthy data ecosystem, knowledge accessibility, foundational engineering practices, user-centric development, platform engineering, and small batch working. Without these, "AI can accelerate the creation of technical debt, increase code review complexity, and introduce instability."
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (annual research program, large sample, but single study for this specific AI finding)
**Key claims:**
- 90% of developers now use AI assistance
- AI amplifies existing strengths AND weaknesses — it does not fix broken teams
- Seven organizational capabilities required as prerequisites, not add-ons
- Tools alone cannot compensate for weak workflows or cultural issues
- Unlike 2024, 2025 shows positive relationship between AI adoption and delivery throughput — suggesting teams ARE learning, but only those with the prerequisites
**Compound Trigger relevance:** CRITICAL. This validates that **Tool Shift alone is insufficient** (Trigger 6) and that multiple conditions must be in place simultaneously. The seven capabilities map to our trigger model: foundational practices (prerequisite), clear strategy (mandate/direction), platform engineering (conditions creation), knowledge accessibility (peer cascade infrastructure).

### Finding 2: BCG — 85% of Employees Stuck Below the Value Inflection Point
**Source:** https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/ai-adoption-puzzle-why-usage-up-impact-not — [vendor press release] (BCG consultancy framework; adoption stage data is survey-based)
**Date:** 2025
**What:** BCG identifies four adoption stages: (1) information assistance, (2) task assistance, (3) delegation, (4) semiautonomous collaboration. More than 85% of employees remain at stages 2-3. The value inflection point is at stage 4 — "semiautonomous collaboration" — where AI shapes workflows rather than just responding to requests. Getting to stage 4 "requires true workflow reinvention as well as changes to roles, team structures, and ways of working." Five employee personas cluster at different stages: AI champion, independent explorer, organizational adopter, passive observer, cautious skeptic.
**Evidence level:** Level 1 (BCG framework, but survey-backed adoption stage data)
**Key claims:**
- 85%+ employees at stages 2-3 (using AI for tasks, not transforming work)
- Stage 4 (semiautonomous collaboration) = inflection point for real value
- Workflow reinvention + role changes + team structure changes needed for stage 4
- When leaders champion AI actively (not just talk), positive sentiment jumps from 15% to 55%
**Compound Trigger relevance:** HIGH. The 85% stuck figure explains WHY tool shift alone fails — most employees use AI for task assistance without redesigning how they work. The inflection point at stage 4 requires **Workflow Redesign (Trigger 2)** combined with role changes. Tool shift gets you to stage 2-3. You need additional triggers to reach stage 4.

### Finding 3: McKinsey — Workflow Redesign Is THE Strongest Predictor of AI Impact
**Source:** https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai — [vendor press release] (McKinsey consultancy; large survey N=1,993 provides useful data points)
**Date:** June-July 2025 (survey fielded), published late 2025
**What:** McKinsey's 2025 State of AI survey (1,993 participants, 105 nations) finds that AI high performers are 2.8x more likely to have fundamentally redesigned workflows (55% vs 20%). "The single strongest predictor of enterprise-level AI impact is whether an organization fundamentally redesigned its workflows." Only ~6% of respondents qualify as AI high performers (attributing >5% of EBIT to AI). 88% of organizations use AI in at least one function, but nearly two-thirds haven't scaled beyond pilot.
**Evidence level:** Level 1 (McKinsey = consultancy framework, but large survey with specific metrics)
**Key claims:**
- Workflow redesigners are 2.8x more likely to be AI high performers
- Only 6% of organizations are high performers
- 88% use AI somewhere, but 66% haven't scaled
- Workflow redesign is the strongest predictor — stronger than technology choice, budget, or talent
**Compound Trigger relevance:** HIGH. Directly validates **Workflow Redesign (Trigger 2)** as the most impactful single trigger. But note: even among redesigners, only 55% are high performers — meaning redesign alone isn't sufficient either. Other triggers must combine.

### Finding 4: Faros AI — Tool Shift Without Workflow Redesign Creates New Bottlenecks
**Source:** https://www.faros.ai/blog/ai-software-engineering — [practitioner analysis]
**Date:** June 2025
**What:** Faros AI analyzed telemetry from 1,255 teams and 10,000+ developers. Teams with high AI tool use completed 21% more tasks and merged 98% more PRs, BUT PR review time ballooned 91% and bugs increased 9%. The gains in code generation were erased by downstream bottlenecks. Amdahl's Law applies: the slowest part of the pipeline dictates overall speed.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single study, but large-scale real telemetry data — stronger than surveys)
**Key claims:**
- 21% more tasks completed with AI
- 98% more PRs merged
- 91% increase in PR review time (new bottleneck)
- 9% increase in bugs
- Gains evaporate when the rest of the workflow isn't redesigned
**Compound Trigger relevance:** CRITICAL. This is the strongest evidence that **Tool Shift (Trigger 6) without Workflow Redesign (Trigger 2) is actively harmful**. The tool creates more output, but without redesigned review processes, the human bottleneck grows. This proves the compound model — you need triggers in combination.

### Finding 5: METR — Tool Shift Made Experienced Developers 19% SLOWER
**Source:** https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/ — [academic/research]
**Date:** July 2025
**What:** METR ran a randomized controlled trial with 16 experienced open-source developers (repos averaging 22K+ stars, 1M+ lines). Developers working on 246 real issues were randomly assigned AI-allowed or AI-disallowed conditions. Result: developers using AI took 19% longer on average. But they ESTIMATED they were 20% faster. Acceptance rate was <44%, and rejected generations wasted review time.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (rigorous RCT, but small N=16 and early-2025 tools)
**Key claims:**
- AI tools slowed experienced developers by 19% on familiar codebases
- Developers perceived a 20% speedup (massive perception gap)
- <44% of AI generations were accepted
- Review and editing of AI code consumed the productivity gains
**Compound Trigger relevance:** HIGH. The strongest counter-evidence to "just give people tools." For experienced practitioners working on familiar codebases, tool shift alone was NEGATIVE. The perception gap (thought they were faster, actually slower) means self-reported adoption metrics are unreliable. This validates that additional triggers (workflow redesign, verification infrastructure) are needed.

### Finding 6: HBR — Fear-Driven Compliance Mimics Adoption But Destroys Commitment
**Source:** https://hbr.org/2026/02/why-ai-adoption-stalls-according-to-industry-data — [academic/research]
**Date:** February 2026
**What:** Study of 2,000+ respondents (Fall 2025, cross-national) plus 1,000 US respondents (Spring 2025). High-anxiety employees reported 65% job AI-assistance vs 42% for low-anxiety workers — but simultaneously showed 2.2x higher resistance scores. Fear-driven compliance produces "performative rather than participatory adoption." 80% of employees expressed concern about at least one AI-related threat. Four personas identified: Visionaries (40%), Disruptors (30%), Endangered (20%), Complacent (10%).
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (rigorous survey methodology, large sample)
**Key claims:**
- High-anxiety employees USE AI more (65% vs 42%) but RESIST more (2.2x)
- "Usage can just as easily reflect self-protective compliance rather than genuine engagement"
- 65% worry about replacement by AI-skilled workers
- 61% fear AI diminishes their unique value
- Surface-level usage metrics mask underlying resistance
**Compound Trigger relevance:** CRITICAL. This explains WHY **Mandate (Trigger 3)** has mixed results. Mandates increase USAGE but not COMMITMENT. The fear-driven compliance pattern means adoption metrics lie — a company can show 90% tool usage while having 2.2x resistance. This is the mechanism behind the Shopify backlash and the Amazon Kiro petition. Mandates need to be paired with **psychological safety (Conditions Creator from Cycle 72)** and **peer cascade (Trigger 5)** to convert compliance into commitment.

## Pattern Analysis: What the Matrix Reveals

### The Minimum Viable Trigger Combination

Based on the matrix, **successful transformations that maintain organizational health** (not just financial metrics) require at minimum:

1. **Seed Practitioner** — someone who actually knows what they're doing, placed deliberately
2. **Peer Cascade mechanism** — guild, champion network, spark sessions — the infrastructure for peer influence to spread
3. **Tool Shift** — necessary but never sufficient on its own

**Three triggers minimum.** Every success case (Varonis, Citi, PwC NL) has at least these three. Spotify and Shopify add Mandate and Project Catalyst on top.

### The Amplifiers (Not Required, But Powerful)

4. **Workflow Redesign** — the strongest single predictor per McKinsey (2.8x). Present in all high-performing cases. May be a RESULT of seeds + peer cascade rather than a separate trigger.
5. **Mandate** — works ONLY when leader personally practices AND combined with enablement. Without enablement: Amazon (catastrophe) or Fiverr (financial gains + organizational damage).
6. **Conditions Creator** (from Cycle 72) — the meta-trigger that activates Peer Cascade. Present in every successful case. May be the mechanism rather than a separate trigger.

### The Definitively Insufficient

- **Tool Shift alone:** DORA 2025, Faros AI, METR, BCG all prove this is insufficient. The Nordic 96%/4% gap is the macro evidence.
- **Mandate alone:** Amazon Kiro = catastrophic failure. HBR angst study = compliance without commitment.
- **Mandate + Tool Shift (without seeds or peers):** Not a single success case in the matrix.

### The Sequencing Question

Evidence suggests a pattern, though not yet conclusive:

1. **Seed Practitioner FIRST** — Varonis (detect enablers = phase 1), Spotify (engineers built Honk before leadership noticed), Shopify (Scout team existed before memo)
2. **Tool Shift CONCURRENT** — seeds need tools to demonstrate value
3. **Peer Cascade SECOND** — guild/champion structures built after seeds prove value
4. **Mandate THIRD (if at all)** — leadership amplifies what's already working, doesn't initiate from scratch

**Counter-evidence for this sequencing:** Shopify's mandate came from the top, but Lutke was personally practicing (he WAS the seed). Citi's champion program was organizationally designed (mandate-like), but staffed with natural influencers (seeds). The boundary between "mandate" and "seed + amplification" may be blurrier than the model suggests.

### The Failure Pattern

**Mandate → Tool Shift → No Seeds → No Peers → Failure/Damage**

This is the Amazon Kiro pattern. Also appears to be the pattern behind the BCG finding that 85% are stuck at stage 2-3: companies deployed tools (stage 2), mandated usage (stage 3), but never created the peer cascade or seed practitioner infrastructure needed for stage 4.

## What I Looked For But Did Not Find

1. **No documented case where ALL eight triggers were present but transformation STILL failed.** The failure cases consistently show missing triggers — never all triggers present. This is absence of evidence, not evidence of absence, but it's suggestive.

2. **No rigorous study of trigger COMBINATIONS.** McKinsey studies individual factors. DORA studies capabilities. HBR studies peer influence. Nobody has studied which COMBINATION of factors predicts success. The compound trigger model remains our synthesis, not an externally validated framework.

3. **No documented minimum trigger count from academic research.** Our matrix suggests 3 minimum (seed + peers + tools), but this is pattern-matching across case studies, not controlled research.

4. **No Nordic company-level compound trigger case.** Klarna is cost-cutting, not team transformation. BCG Nordic data shows the gap (96%/4%) but no company-level success story of an existing Nordic team becoming AI-native through compound triggers. This remains the biggest gap — and the biggest opportunity for original research.

5. **No non-engineering compound trigger cases.** Every case in the matrix is engineering/technology. Where are the finance teams, HR teams, marketing teams that transformed? PwC NL is cross-functional but details are thin. The compound trigger model is unvalidated outside engineering.

6. **No evidence that mandate-first EVER works without an existing seed.** Searched specifically for this. Every successful mandate case (Shopify, Spotify, Citi) had seeds already in place when the mandate arrived. Amazon mandated without seeds and failed catastrophically. But this could be selection bias — companies with seeds might be the ones that bother to write about their success.

7. **No longitudinal data on sustainability.** Varonis achieved 100% adoption — is it still 100% a year later? Spotify shipped 1000 PRs/10 days in March 2026 — will that pace hold? All evidence is cross-sectional snapshots, not longitudinal tracking.

### The Revised Model: Three Required + Two Amplifiers + One Meta-Trigger

**Required (must have all three):**
1. **Seed Practitioner** — someone with genuine depth who demonstrates the target state
2. **Peer Cascade Infrastructure** — guild, champion network, spark sessions, or equivalent mechanism for peer influence to spread
3. **Tool Shift** — necessary enabler, but never sufficient alone

**Amplifiers (dramatically increase success probability):**
4. **Workflow Redesign** — the strongest single predictor of impact (McKinsey 2.8x). Can emerge from seeds + peers, or be deliberately designed
5. **Mandate with Leader Practice** — works only when the leader personally practices AND enablement exists. Without both conditions: damage guaranteed

**Meta-Trigger (the mechanism that connects the others):**
6. **Conditions Creator** (Cycle 72 finding) — the person or team that identifies seeds, builds peer infrastructure, provides tools, creates psychological safety, then steps back. Present in every success case. May be the orchestration layer that activates the other triggers in the right sequence.

### What This Means for CTOs

The compound trigger model converts to actionable advice:

1. **Find your seeds first.** Don't start with tools or mandates. Find 2-5 people who genuinely understand AI-native work. If you don't have them, hire or develop them. (Varonis phase 1, Spotify's Honk builders, Citi's first accelerators)

2. **Build peer infrastructure.** Create the mechanism for seeds to influence others. Guild, champion network, spark sessions, office hours. Make adoption visible and social. (Varonis AI Guild, Citi 4,000 accelerators, PwC NL network analysis)

3. **Provide tools with support, not tools with mandates.** Tool access is necessary but mandating usage without enablement creates fear-driven compliance that looks like adoption but isn't. (HBR angst study, Amazon Kiro counter-example)

4. **Redesign workflows around AI capabilities, not around AI tools.** This is the 2.8x multiplier. Don't add AI to existing processes — redesign processes around what AI makes possible. (McKinsey, Faros AI Amdahl's Law finding)

5. **If you mandate, mandate from personal practice.** Lutke works. Kaufman's "don't be a cheerleader" is the principle. 70% of C-suite uses AI <1hr/week (HBR) — if you're in that 70%, your mandate will produce compliance, not transformation.

6. **Appoint a Conditions Creator.** Someone whose job is: find seeds → build peer infrastructure → provide tools with support → measure peer influence (not just usage) → step back. This is the orchestration role.
