# Early Plays

Try keep it intentional. Try keep it open enough to allow for discovering the real goal. Open doors to something still bigger. I should not tell you the vision. But what we do gives us clues on where we are really going. Hence early plays. Here goes.

## Core — Make the Train Compound

The engineering adoption train is already moving. 70% is a strong base. The question is: why isn't it compounding?

In my experience the answer is almost always the same. Individual productivity gains don't compound. They stay local — one developer codes faster, but the system around them hasn't changed. The handovers, the reviews, the dependencies, the release process — all still the same.

Compounding happens when you change the system, not just the tool usage. Context infrastructure — making your architecture, processes, and decisions AI-ready and version-controlled. Measurement that connects tool adoption to end-to-end outcomes. Engineering practices that are designed for agentic work, not adapted from pre-agentic habits.

The methods for this are now getting codified in the agentic engineering space. This is no longer uncharted territory. It's hard work but it's known work.

The moonshot: take the 70% adoption and make it compound. Visible roadmap velocity impact within months, not years.

## All Trains — Go Broad or Go Home

Here's the provocative one.

WithSecure's CEO could ask F-Secure's CEO for a copy of the original training material I built there. Same founder behind both companies. Same family. The material exists and is actively being used — the next group and internal trainers are running it at F-Secure right now.

If you already have good stuff, we merge and make the evolved version. Then: train all mid-management. Not info flashes — hands-on, build agents with your own work, same format that got NPS 80-90% at F-Secure. Train-the-trainer if needed for scale.

Then: keenly observe what happens. When managers start building, they see their own processes differently. They start rearranging things on their own. They find the waste, the duplication, the manual steps that agents could handle. Nobody has to tell them — they discover it.

The goal is to get managers to the point where they start making their own prioritisation decisions for AI actions. Not because someone mandated it. Because they've experienced what's possible and they want it for their teams.

That's the D in ADKAR — Desire. When managers get there, they start driving the right changes downward on their own — compounding engineering, but also whatever else we've chosen as company priority. The system starts fixing itself from within.

The end-game here is teams that really grok agentic ways of working. This does not emerge from bottom-up adoption alone. From what I've seen, mid-down is the optimal way to scale it — managers who get it, driving it into their teams.


## Company — Open Essential Information for AI

This can be enabled concurrently and incrementally — you don't wait for the other moonshots to finish.

The concrete action: open up customer data, strategy numbers, and business metrics to everyone's AI and agents. As easy as getting to a data lake with MCP/CLI to make queries with your agent.

Super-interesting things start to happen when you democratise information and numbers. This pretty much looks like unlocking the high-end AI "business use" application. No wonder: the old dashboards and customer reports were very hard to action on. When anyone can query the data with their agent, the decisions get better — because the people making them can finally see what they need to see.

That's not a roadmap speed play. That's a roadmap quality play. Better context, better decisions, less waste. The roadmap capacity problem starts solving itself. "Thought leader in AI" stops being an aspiration and starts being a description.

I like to float this kind of idea — just because it causes you to think a year ahead. Not actionable today. But worth having in the back of your mind as everything else takes shape.

# Learning and Scaling Approach

This is not a transformation programme with a Gantt chart.

The approach that works: place smart bets, act deliberately, assume to be wrong in a few ways, understand progress, learn. Every week. The strategy emerges from doing, not from planning. The first plan will be wrong — and that's fine, because the learning from the first week is worth more than the plan written before it.

A continuous learning loop where the people doing the work are also the people shaping the direction.

I understand networks and how the network is the power. I deeply appreciate how Kotter has this right. The change scales in the network. I always look to that first.

I know this sounds loose. It's not. It's the most disciplined approach there is — because it requires measurement, failure accounting, and adjustment every single week.
