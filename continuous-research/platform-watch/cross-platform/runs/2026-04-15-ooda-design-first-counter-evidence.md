# OODA Cycle: Counter-Evidence Search — "Design First, Then AI"

**Date:** 2026-04-15
**Thesis tested:** Competence-first (Level 3, 58+ cycles) — does counter-evidence exist for "organizational redesign before AI deployment"?
**Method:** 3 rounds of targeted search across 5 communities (10X ORG / Org Topologies, LeSS, sociotechnical design, SAFe, Bayer DSO)
**Honest question:** Is it possible that org redesign before AI works, and we just haven't found the evidence because we weren't looking?

---

## Target 1: 10X ORG / Org Topologies (Krivitsky, Larman, Flemm)

**What they claim:** "First Fix Your Org Design, Then Adopt AI." AI is a "force multiplier" that only works after the org structure is right. Published as a bestselling book (Feb 2026).

**What evidence they provide:** Zero specific companies with measurable results. The book is a business novel featuring "Hanna" — a fictional HR director. On a Scrum.org podcast, Krivitsky describes one unnamed company under NDA where Org Topologies revealed the wrong Target Operating Model — but no outcomes were measured. Dave West mentions a CPG company that couldn't deploy an AI solution due to organizational silos — also under NDA, no metrics.

**Evidence level:** Level 1 (opinion). The framework is logically coherent but has no public evidence. The entire "design first" claim rests on the authors' consulting experience, presented as fiction. No company is named. No metric is cited. No before/after is documented.

**Honest assessment:** This is a consulting framework selling a sequence, not evidence for a sequence. The fact that they chose to write a novel instead of publishing case studies is itself informative.

## Target 2: LeSS Adoptions + AI

**What we searched for:** Any LeSS-organized company that subsequently deployed AI and attributed better results to their LeSS structure.

**What we found:** Nothing. Zero results. The LeSS community (less.works) has case studies from BASE Company, a German insurance firm, and others — none mention AI deployment. The Scrum/Agile community has started discussing "AI agents as team members" and "AI-augmented Scrum" but these are forward-looking thought pieces, not deployment case studies.

**Evidence level:** No evidence found. Not even Level 1.

**Honest assessment:** The LeSS community appears to have completely missed the AI wave in their case study documentation. This could mean: (a) LeSS companies aren't deploying AI agents yet, (b) they are but nobody's writing about it through a LeSS lens, or (c) the LeSS structure doesn't particularly help or hinder AI adoption. We cannot distinguish between these.

Sources:
- [less.works case studies](https://less.works/case-studies/) [practitioner direct — but no AI content found]
- [Scrum.org AI-augmented framework](https://www.scrum.org/resources/blog/ai-augmented-scrum-framework-when-half-your-team-autonomous-agents) [practitioner analysis — forward-looking, not evidence]

## Target 3: Sociotechnical Design + AI (Tavistock / Emery & Trist lineage)

**What we searched for:** Academic evidence from the sociotechnical tradition documenting "we redesigned the sociotechnical system, then AI worked better."

**What we found:** Active academic interest (2025-2026 papers, AIES conference calls for papers) but no empirical before/after study. The literature says the right things — "joint optimization of social and technical systems" — but provides frameworks, not deployment evidence. A 2025 SME study found AI transformation "unfolds through ongoing negotiation, improvisation, and adaptation, challenging linear and static models" — which actually supports the competence-first thesis (the sequence is emergent, not pre-designed). A 2025 study on AI in public administration found "complex interdependencies between AI materiality and social/technical systems" — again, mutual adaptation, not design-first.

**Evidence level:** Level 1 (academic opinion/framework). The sociotechnical community provides theoretical justification for joint optimization but no empirical evidence for the specific claim that redesigning the org BEFORE deploying AI produces better results.

**Critical observation:** The most empirically grounded paper (SME study, Tandfonline 2025) actually found that transformation "challenges linear and static models of technological change" — i.e., the real world doesn't follow a clean "design first, then deploy" sequence. This is evidence AGAINST the design-first thesis, from the design-first community's own academic tradition.

## Target 4: SAFe + AI

**What we searched for:** Any SAFe-organized company reporting that their SAFe structure helped AI adoption.

**What we found:** Scaled Agile Inc. launched "AI-Native" as a new training/certification program. Their own website describes it as "designed to transform enterprises by building AI fluency." One case: a manufacturing conglomerate used AI agents to analyze retrospective data from 50+ teams, reducing defects by 20%. But this is AI helping SAFe, not SAFe helping AI. Dr. Natalia Kuzmina (S&P Global Ratings) presented on "AI Enhanced SAFe" at SAFe Summit 2025 — but the framing is AI enhancing SAFe, not SAFe enabling AI.

**Evidence level:** Level 0-1. The SAFe community's AI story is vendor marketing (Scaled Agile selling AI-Native certifications) plus isolated anecdotes. No evidence that having SAFe in place made AI deployment easier or more effective.

**Honest assessment:** The entire SAFe+AI narrative runs in the wrong direction for our question. The SAFe community is asking "how does AI help us do SAFe better?" not "does SAFe help AI work better?" This absence is telling.

## Target 5: Bayer's Dynamic Shared Ownership (DSO)

**What they did:** Starting 2023 under CEO Bill Anderson, Bayer cut layers from 12-13 to 6-7, reduced management by ~50%, eliminated ~12,000 positions, created ~2,000 autonomous teams of 6-10 people with 95% decision authority. Target: EUR 2B in org savings by end 2026.

**The sequence question:** Did Bayer redesign the org BEFORE deploying AI, and did the redesign help AI deployment?

**What we found:**
- The org redesign is massive and ongoing (phases through 2026+). Early results claimed: some teams doubled revenue in 90-day cycles, one product launched a year ahead of schedule, production lines cut from 15 specialists to 5.
- AI is mentioned as part of Bayer's broader strategy ("AI-ready"), and they're using AI in drug discovery (novel herbicides), but we found NO specific evidence that the org redesign made AI deployment easier or more effective.
- Employee adoption: ~1/3 leaning in, ~1/3 cautious, ~1/3 waiting. Mixed.
- Critical perspective exists: Medium article "Flat Hierarchies, Flat Growth" calls DSO a failed experiment (paywalled, couldn't extract specifics). Stock price recovered from 20-year lows but the company faces structural challenges (Monsanto litigation, etc.) unrelated to DSO.
- Harvard Business School published a teaching case on Bayer's DSO — but HBS cases document what happened, not whether it worked.
- Org Topologies studied Bayer's DSO alongside Haier's RenDanHeYi — but the Org Topologies site didn't render actual content (Wix site issue).

**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single experiment), but critically: the experiment is about org redesign, NOT about org redesign enabling AI. The AI connection is aspirational ("AI-ready"), not evidenced. Bayer redesigned for speed and delayering, and separately uses AI in R&D. Nobody has documented that the DSO structure made AI deployment work better.

**Honest assessment:** Bayer is the strongest case in the entire search — a real company, at massive scale, doing "design first." But even Bayer doesn't provide evidence for the specific claim "redesign first, then AI works better." They provide evidence for "redesign to remove bureaucracy" (which may or may not help with AI). The causal chain from "fewer layers" to "better AI deployment" is assumed, not documented.

## Bonus: McKinsey "Workflow Redesign" Finding

**What's claimed:** Barry O'Reilly and others cite McKinsey's State of AI 2025 as evidence that "organizations that redesigned workflows before selecting models were twice as likely to see significant AI returns."

**What McKinsey actually says:** "55% [of high performers] fundamentally redesigning workflows **when deploying** AI" (emphasis mine). The word is "when" — concurrent, not "before." McKinsey's relative weights analysis found workflow redesign has "one of the strongest contributions to achieving meaningful business impact" (R-squared = 0.20 for the full model, 25 variables). Sample: 1,993 respondents across ~105 countries.

**Critical problems with the "design first" interpretation:**
1. McKinsey says "when deploying" — this is concurrent redesign during AI deployment, not design-first-then-AI. The sequence is not separated.
2. This is correlation, not causation. McKinsey explicitly uses "relative weights analysis" to estimate contribution, not causal inference. High performers may redesign workflows BECAUSE they have more competence, not the other way around.
3. "Workflow redesign" is narrower than "organizational redesign." Changing how a claims process works is not the same as flattening management layers or creating autonomous teams.
4. The R-squared of 0.20 means the full 25-variable model explains only 20% of variance in high-performer status. 80% is unexplained.

**Evidence level:** Level 0 (analyst prediction / consultancy framework). McKinsey surveys are paid research. But even taking the finding at face value, it supports "redesign workflows while deploying AI" — which is compatible with competence-first (people learn to build, then redesign their work). It does NOT support "redesign the org first, then deploy AI."

Sources:
- [McKinsey State of AI 2025](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai) [analyst — Level 0 by our rules]
- [McKinsey PDF report](https://www.mckinsey.com/~/media/mckinsey/business%20functions/quantumblack/our%20insights/the%20state%20of%20ai/2025/the-state-of-ai-how-organizations-are-rewiring-to-capture-value_final.pdf) [analyst — Level 0]
- [Barry O'Reilly AI Adoption 2026](https://barryoreilly.com/explore/blog/ai-adoption-2026-leadership-organizational-redesign/) [practitioner analysis — but miscites McKinsey's sequence]

**What it claims:** Companies should redesign how they "sense, frame, decide, and act" by combining human and machine intelligence.

## Additional Cases Investigated (Round 2)

### Moderna — HR/IT Merger (2025)

Moderna merged technology and HR departments under a "Chief People and Digital Technology Officer" (Tracey Franklin). The merged org manages 5,800 humans and 3,000+ GPTs. Previously, mChat (internal chatbot, 2023) achieved 80% employee adoption. Approach: "work planning, not workforce planning" — assessing tasks for automation/augmentation/human.

**Critical assessment:** The reorg happened AFTER massive AI deployment (mChat 2023, 3,000+ GPTs by 2024). This is competence-first in action: they deployed AI widely, saw what worked, then reorganized to optimize. The merger is a response to AI success, not a prerequisite. Also: Moderna subsequently laid off 10% of the digital team amid falling vaccine revenues — suggesting the reorg was partly cost-driven.

## Verdict: Does the Counter-Evidence Change the Competence-First Thesis?

**No. The thesis holds. But the picture is more nuanced than "zero counter-evidence."**

### What we found:

The "design first, then AI" community consists of:
1. **Framework vendors** (10X ORG, SAFe AI-Native) selling a sequence with no public evidence
2. **One massive experiment** (Bayer DSO) that's about org redesign but hasn't documented the AI connection
3. **Misinterpreted McKinsey data** that actually says "when deploying" (concurrent), not "before deploying"
4. **A book** (O'Reilly's Artificial Organizations) whose own cases actually describe competence-first sequences

### The structural problem with "design first":

Nobody has documented a company that redesigned their org for AI and THEN saw AI work better. The reason this evidence doesn't exist may be fundamental: you can't design an org for a capability nobody in the org understands yet. This is the competence-first mechanism — the design requires the knowledge that only comes from building.

### What's genuinely true from the other side:

McKinsey's finding that high performers redesign workflows concurrently with AI deployment is real and important. But "concurrent redesign" is exactly what the competence-first model predicts: train people → they build → they discover what needs to change → org adapts. The adaptation happens DURING deployment, not before it.

### The refined thesis:

**Competence-first does not mean "never redesign."** It means: the redesign is informed by competence, not the other way around. Competence → Discovery → Redesign → More Deployment. The "design first" community inverts this to Redesign → Deployment, and has no evidence the inversion works.

### Confidence update:

Competence-first thesis remains Level 3. Counter-evidence search was thorough (5 communities, 3 rounds each, 15+ searches). No counter-evidence found at Level 2 or above. The closest candidate (Bayer) is a Level 2 single experiment that doesn't actually test the relevant sequence. McKinsey data, properly read, supports concurrent rather than sequential redesign.

**The absence of evidence after a systematic search IS evidence — of absence.**
