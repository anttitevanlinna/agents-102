Two canvases: one for the **builder leader** (buyer), one for the **end learner** (user). The builder leader buys the transformation. The learner experiences it.

## Canvas 1: The Builder Leader (CEO / CTO / SVP HR)

### Customer Profile

#### Jobs to be done

**Functional jobs:**
1. **Build organizational AI agent capability** — not just awareness, but the ability to build, deploy, and govern agents across the business
2. **Figure out which of our 200 processes should use agents** — and which are vendor fantasies
3. **Choose and deploy an agent platform** — that works with our existing infrastructure (M365, AWS, GCP, hybrid)
4. **Show the board measurable progress on AI transformation** — not slides, not pilots, not "we're exploring"
5. **Avoid another transformation theatre cycle** — they've lived through digital/agile/cloud transformations that produced frameworks but not felt change

**Social jobs:**
6. **Be seen as the leader who owns the transformation** — not the one who outsourced it to Deloitte
7. **Have a credible answer when the board asks "what's our AI agent strategy?"** — one that comes from understanding, not from a consultant's slide deck
8. **Join a peer group of leaders navigating the same challenge** — not a vendor ecosystem, not a consulting dependency

**Emotional jobs:**
9. **Feel confident they understand what agents actually do** — not governance of an abstraction
10. **Feel urgency is being channeled productively** — not into premature platform decisions or vendor-shaped initiatives

#### Pains

**Barrier pains:**
1. **Can't even formulate the right questions about agents.** Four horizontal platforms, 5+ vertical SaaS vendors, emerging standards (MCP, A2A, Agent Skills) — and no way to evaluate any of them without competence the organization doesn't have yet. Every evaluation is shaped by whichever vendor presented last.
2. **Every vendor demos one process brilliantly — but we run 200 processes.** Salesforce shows 80% chat resolution at Finnair. Impressive — but customer service is 1 of 200 processes Finnair runs. The vendor owns one silo. The other 199 are ours to figure out.
3. **Platform selection depends on infrastructure we've already chosen** — but nobody frames it that way. Microsoft's agent strategy is an Azure upsell. An AWS-native company evaluating Copilot Studio is actually evaluating an Azure migration. Nobody tells the CTO this.
4. **The research landscape actively deceives.** Google's deployment evidence is circular (all traces back to Google's own materials). Microsoft's "1,400 connectors" is misleading (orchestration across them requires custom development). OpenAI's vision runs 12-18 months ahead of reality. MIT: 95% of organizations report no measurable ROI from AI.
5. **Consultancy frameworks are theatre without competence.** McKinsey's "Factory vs Artisan" is meaningless if nobody in the room has built an agent. IBM's "manage agents as workers" is a metaphor without substance if nobody has worked alongside one. Governance structures govern nothing real.
6. **The urgency is real but the direction is unclear.** BCG: only 19% of Nordic workers use GenAI weekly vs 61% globally. The board is asking questions. Competitors are moving. But moving where?

**Outcome pains:**
7. **Risk of choosing a platform too early** — before knowing which processes actually benefit. The entire business agent space is pre-chasm. OpenAI's own COO: "We have not yet really seen enterprise AI penetrate enterprise business process."
8. **Risk of building vendor dependency** — every platform locks you in differently. No promotion path exists from personal agents to team agents to company agents on any platform. Promoting means rebuilding.
9. **Risk of another expensive pilot that produces a demo, not capability.** The board approved the AI budget; it can't be wasted on consultancy theatre again.

**Undesired outcome pains:**
10. **Training that produces certificates, not competence.** Aalto EE and MOOCs teach awareness. Vendor training teaches one product. Neither builds the capability to think agents across the business.
11. **Dependency on external partners who leave.** When Solita/Reaktor finishes the engagement, the slides go stale and the capability walks out the door.

#### Gains

**Required gains:**
1. People who can build working agents with the organization's own policies, on real processes
2. A clear view of which processes are good agent candidates — and why (bounded scope, clear success criteria, escalation tolerance, data in one system)
3. Internal capability that scales without external dependency — champions who can train others

**Expected gains:**
4. A credible AI agent strategy grounded in hands-on understanding, not vendor narratives
5. Structured context for platform evaluation — when the time comes, the question answers itself
6. Access to what works and what doesn't across industries — not vendor case studies, but peer-validated patterns

**Desired gains:**
7. Becoming the organization where the transformation is real — not theatre, not slides, felt change
8. A peer network of leaders navigating the same challenge — high-trust, cross-industry, Nordic
9. The ability to re-evaluate as the landscape changes — not a one-time decision that ages

**Unexpected gains:**
10. The advisory itself demonstrates what agentic work looks like — the medium is the message
11. Discovering that the "which platform" question dissolves once you have competence — the right answer becomes obvious when you know what to ask

## Canvas 2: The End Learner (Non-Technical Business Professional)

### Customer Profile

#### Jobs to be done

1. **Understand what agents actually are** — not from a slide deck but by building one
2. **Build something that works with my real job** — not sample_data.csv, not a toy
3. **Become the person in my org who knows how this works** — the go-to for "can agents do X?"
4. **Make the case for agents in my domain** — with a working example, not a pitch deck
5. **Not feel left behind** — AI is moving fast and I don't know where I fit

#### Pains

1. **AI training is either too technical (developer-focused) or too shallow (awareness-only).** Nothing exists for "I'm not a developer but I want to build real things."
2. **Vendor training teaches one tool, not understanding.** If the tool changes, the training is worthless.
3. **Fear of looking stupid.** AI conversations are full of jargon, acronyms, and assumed knowledge.
4. **No clear entry point.** Where do I even start? ChatGPT? Claude? Copilot? An API?
5. **The agent I build might fabricate, break, or embarrass the organization.** How do I trust it?

#### Gains

1. **"I built that."** The personal empowerment moment — a working agent I made myself.
2. **Transferable understanding.** Patterns that work across platforms, not tool-specific tricks.
3. **Career relevance.** Being the person who can bridge business knowledge and agent capability.
4. **Confidence to experiment.** Knowing what agents can do, what they can't, and what to watch out for.
5. **A community of peers.** Others learning the same thing, sharing mistakes and successes.

## Fit Assessment

### Where the fit is strong (validated by research + field evidence)

- **Pain 1 (can't formulate questions) ↔ Bootstrap + Advisory** — this is the core fit. No competitor addresses it. Consultancies sell answers to questions the buyer can't formulate. We build the competence to ask the right questions.
- **Pain 2 (vendor demos one process) ↔ 1-of-200 framing** — research-grounded. Finnair case makes it tangible. No vendor will tell you this.
- **Pain 4 (research landscape deceives) ↔ evidence-graded research** — our platform watch system produces exactly the sourced, evidence-graded assessment CTOs need. This is IP no competitor has.
- **Pain 5 (consultancy theatre) ↔ exercise-led training** — F-Secure validates this. Real agents, real policies, real competence.
- **Competence creates pull (F-Secure field evidence):** After 2 modules, pretty much everyone started building — dashboards, agents, apps — unprompted. The competence itself creates discovery. You don't need a process assessment to find agent candidates. Teach people to build and they find their own processes. This inverts the consultancy sequence (assess → identify → build → train) to the sequence that actually works (train → they identify → they build → assess what worked).
- **Three predictable enablers emerge (F-Secure field evidence):** Builders hit three walls: (1) data access — agents need Snowflake, Salesforce, internal systems; (2) a platform to run what they built — "where does this live?"; (3) discoverability — how do others find and use what builders created. These three are the natural Bootstrap→Advisory handoff. "Your people are building. Here's what they need next."

### Where the fit is assumed (needs validation)

- **Gain 6 (peer network) ↔ Connect** — untested. Assumption map Priority 1, 4, 13.
- **Gain 8 (re-evaluate over time) ↔ co-owned advisory system** — not yet built. Assumption map Priority relates to advisory delivery.
- **Pain reliever (infrastructure-first platform analysis)** — the insight is strong but the advisory system isn't built yet.
- **Three enablers as convergence** — F-Secure is one deployment. Watch for the same three walls at the next org to confirm convergence.

### The core fit statement

**We are the prerequisite, not the complement.** Without agent competence, the CTO can't see what to research, can't evaluate platforms clearly, can't identify which of 200 processes to try first, and can't tell the difference between a vendor demo and a real capability. Bootstrap builds the competence. Advisory builds the structured context. Together they turn "I don't know what to ask" into "I know exactly what to do next."

The sequence matters: **Competence → discovery → context → platform.** We own the first three. The platform decision falls out of them.

**The sharpest formulation (from F-Secure field experience):** Competence doesn't lead to a strategy deck. **Competence leads to the ability to make lightweight choices that enable real progress by real people.** Not "which platform should we select?" but "we need MCPs for Snowflake and a place to run simple apps." Scoped, reversible, practical decisions made by people who know what they need — because they've built things. The heavyweight vendor evaluation is what organizations do when they lack competence. The lightweight choice is what organizations do when they have it.

**The two-way door test:** Nobody should be making non-two-way-door decisions on the agent landscape right now. The entire space is pre-chasm. Every platform is immature. Every vendor story is 12-18 months ahead of reality. Betting small while making progress is what a smart CTO does. Competence turns one-way-door decisions (big platform bets) into two-way-door decisions (lightweight, reversible choices informed by real usage).
