## Finding 1: Second Independent Cowork Practitioner Review — "Task Executor, Not a True Agent"

**Source:** https://thoughts.jock.pl/p/claude-cowork-dispatch-computer-use-honest-agent-review-2026 — [practitioner direct]
**Date:** April 2026
**Agent level:** Personal
**What:** Paweł Józefiak, a developer running a custom AI agent system 24/7 on a Mac Mini with 25 background processes, published a hands-on review of Cowork. Core verdict: "a stripped and limited version of Claude Code, but a quite good version of that — effective for discrete tasks but fundamentally limited as an agent." Critical limitation identified: **no persistent memory across sessions** — "operates in isolation" with each interaction independent. This prevents contextual awareness across time. MacStories testing showed ~50% success rate on 12 operations. Dispatch described as "currently slow." Computer use struggled with sharing results and app interactions. What worked: file organization, spreadsheet analysis, 50+ service connectors (Google Calendar, Slack, Linear, GitHub, Stripe).
**Evidence level:** Level 2 — second independent practitioner test (after Willison, cycle 96)
**Key claims:**
- Cowork = capable task executor, not a persistent agent — no cross-session memory
- ~50% success rate on multi-step operations (MacStories source, cited within article)
- Dispatch slow; computer use inconsistent
- 50+ service connectors functional for read operations

## Finding 6: AI-Native Team Leadership — CMR + P&G Evidence

**Source:** https://cmr.berkeley.edu/2026/02/when-ai-joins-the-product-team-will-leadership-still-drives-innovation/ — [academic/research, California Management Review]
**Author:** Shivam Srivastava, Research Associate, Centre for Business Innovation, Indian School of Business
**Date:** February 2026
**What:** Academic insight piece drawing on real company case studies. Core finding: leadership's role in AI-native teams shifts from execution management to **curation and judgment**. Key insight: "Intelligence is becoming abundant; judgment remains scarce." The competitive advantage is not which AI tools you use, but the clarity of human judgment about which problems to prioritize and what success looks like. **P&G finding referenced:** "If you want to be in that top 10% of performers, a full human team plus AI seems like the recipe for success" — hybrid outperforms pure-AI. Leadership responsibilities that don't transfer to AI: defining ambition, setting success criteria, accepting accountability, decision literacy (questioning probabilistic AI outputs).
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (academic synthesis + P&G case data, not deployment evidence)
**Key claims:**
- AI-native leadership = curation and judgment, not direction-setting for individual tasks
- Top 10% performance = hybrid (human+AI), not full-AI substitution
- "Decision literacy" becomes a new leadership competency — knowing when to override AI outputs
- Governance role expands: staged investments with go/no-go criteria replace open-ended pilots

**Connection to open user signal:** This directly addresses the March 30 signal: "What does leadership look like for AI-native teams?" The answer from Srivastava/P&G: leaders become system architects, not task managers. They set the context, define the judgment criteria, and own accountability.
