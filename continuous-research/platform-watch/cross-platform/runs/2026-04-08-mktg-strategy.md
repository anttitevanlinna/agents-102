# Marketing Strategy with AI: Beyond Content Creation

**Date:** 2026-04-08
**Research focus:** How marketing leaders use AI for STRATEGY, POSITIONING, and BRAND work — not content creation
**Method:** Web search, people-first investigation of named practitioners and platforms

---

## Executive Summary

Marketing AI for strategic work (as distinct from content creation) is in a **transitional state**. The landscape splits into three tiers:

1. **Actually deployed and producing results:** Media mix modeling / budget allocation, competitive intelligence, dynamic pricing, and paid media autonomy (Meta Advantage+, Google Performance Max). These are platform-level, not practitioner-level.
2. **Emerging with early practitioner evidence:** Synthetic market research (Evidenza + Qualtrics), AI-assisted ICP definition, competitive positioning (Klue + Ignition).
3. **Heavily discussed but thin on practitioner evidence:** Brand strategy, positioning workshops, GTM planning, campaign strategy. Lots of "CMOs should" articles. Almost zero "here's what I actually did."

**The biggest finding is what we did NOT find.** See below.

## 2. Synthetic Market Research — STRONGEST PRACTITIONER EVIDENCE

### Evidenza + EY Americas (Toni Clayton-Hine, CMO)
- **WHO:** Toni Clayton-Hine, CMO of EY Americas ($25B firm). Mark Ritson (advisor to Evidenza). Evidenza founder team.
- **WHAT:** Evidenza created synthetic customer panels matching EY's real survey sample. Asked synthetic customers the same qualitative and quantitative questions. Clayton-Hine reported "95% correlation" between synthetic and real results, "astounding" match. EY used it for segmentation across nine IT categories and creative messaging refinement.
- **SOURCE:** https://www.mi-3.com.au/27-08-2024/synthetic-customers-meet-synthetic-cmos-and-cfos-evidenza-clones-sharp-ritson-binet (August 2024 — at freshness boundary)

- **EVIDENCE LEVEL:** Level 2. Single named practitioner (Clayton-Hine at EY) with specific, measurable outcome. Strongest piece of practitioner evidence found in this entire investigation.

### Evidenza "Synthetic CMOs"
- **WHAT:** AI clones of Byron Sharp, Mark Ritson, Les Binet, Peter Field. Marketers can consult these for strategic feedback on marketing plans.
- **SOURCE:** https://askditto.io/news/evidenza-ai-review-2026-synthetic-cmos-enterprise-research-and-what-it-costs

### Mark Ritson's trajectory on this

## 4. Paid Media Autonomy — MOST MATURE AGENTIC DEPLOYMENT IN MARKETING

### Meta Advantage+ and Google Performance Max
- **WHAT:** These are the most widely deployed agentic AI systems in marketing, managing billions in ad spend. Meta Advantage+ reports 18% ROAS improvement with enriched chat signals, 9% lower CPA, 25% CPA drop for fashion with AI-generated UGC-style video. Google Performance Max reports 27% average increase in conversions, now behind 62% of all Google ad clicks (Feb 2026).
- **SOURCE:** https://www.fluency.inc/blog/2026-trends-and-performance-benchmarks-google-ads-meta-ads and Google Ads Blog (cited indirectly)
- **SOURCE TYPE:** [mixed — vendor claims from Google/Meta + third-party benchmarking]
- **EVIDENCE LEVEL:** Level 2-3 for existence and adoption. These are genuinely deployed at scale. Performance claims are vendor-sourced (Level 0). The paradox: "operational burden on AdOps teams is increasing as they struggle to maintain strategic control across channels."

### Key insight
Paid media is where agentic marketing is most mature. But this is EXECUTION autonomy, not STRATEGY autonomy. The AI decides bid amounts, audience targeting, and creative variants — it does NOT decide brand positioning, value proposition, or go-to-market strategy.

## WHAT WE DID NOT FIND — The Most Important Section

This is where the real insight lives.

### 1. No named CMO or VP of Marketing sharing "here's how I used AI for brand strategy"
We searched extensively. Every search returned vendor content, analyst predictions, or "CMOs should" guidance articles. **Not a single named marketing leader publishing "I used Claude/ChatGPT/Gemini to do my brand positioning work, and here's what happened."** This is the single most telling finding of this investigation. Content creation has thousands of practitioner stories. Brand strategy has zero.

### 2. No practitioner evidence of AI-assisted positioning workshops
April Dunford has adapted her frameworks FOR the AI era (how to position AI products). She has NOT reported using AI IN her positioning process. AMA offers workshops on "using AI to define positioning" but no outcomes reported. The workshop exists. The practitioner stories do not.

### 3. No evidence of AI doing GTM strategy
"GTM AI" is a heavily marketed category (ZoomInfo, Copy.ai, Reply.io). Every result is vendor marketing for tools that automate GTM EXECUTION (outbound, pipeline). Not a single instance of AI being used for the strategic questions: which market to enter, what positioning to use, which segments to prioritize, what the competitive differentiation should be. The tools automate "how to reach" not "who to reach and why."

### 4. Campaign strategy and channel mix decisions — AI executes, humans still decide
Meta Advantage+ and Google Performance Max make TACTICAL decisions (bids, targeting, creative rotation). No evidence of AI agents making STRATEGIC channel mix decisions (should we be on TikTok or LinkedIn? should we invest in brand or performance?). Sellforte's MMM agents come closest, but they optimize within a human-defined budget, not set the strategy.

### 5. No convergence pattern for strategic marketing AI
Content creation AI hit Level 3 (convergence) in 2024. Marketing strategy AI is at Level 1 (opinions) with pockets of Level 2 (single experiments, mainly Evidenza/synthetic research). There is no convergence signal. Individual practitioners are not independently discovering that AI transforms their strategic work. This is a significant absence.

### 6. The "strategy" being automated is really "analysis"
When articles say "AI for marketing strategy," they mean: analyzing data faster, generating research reports, creating synthetic audiences, optimizing budgets. These are analytical inputs to strategy. The actual strategic decisions — positioning, differentiation, brand architecture, market selection — remain human. Nobody found AI making those calls.

## Interpretation: Why Marketing Strategy AI Lags Behind

Three hypotheses for the gap:

**Hypothesis 1: Strategy requires judgment AI can't yet provide.**
Brand positioning requires understanding competitive context, organizational capabilities, customer psychology, and market timing simultaneously. Current AI can analyze each input but cannot synthesize them into a positioning decision a CMO would trust. The "last mile" of strategic synthesis is still human.

**Hypothesis 2: The practitioners using AI for strategy aren't talking about it.**
Possible but unlikely at this scale. Content creation practitioners talked immediately. If brand strategists were getting value from AI, someone would be blogging about it. The silence is probably signal, not secrecy.

**Hypothesis 3: The tooling doesn't exist yet.**
Generic LLMs (ChatGPT, Claude) can brainstorm positioning ideas, but there's no purpose-built tool for "run my positioning workshop with AI." Evidenza is the closest — synthetic research that feeds strategy — but even they position it as research input, not strategy output. Klue + Ignition could become this for competitive positioning, but it's early.

**Most likely: a combination of all three.** Strategic marketing work is higher-judgment, lower-frequency, and more context-dependent than content creation. The AI tools that exist serve the analytical layer well but don't yet reach the synthesis layer where strategy actually happens.
