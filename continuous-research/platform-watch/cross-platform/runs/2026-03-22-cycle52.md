### 2. Agentforce Technical Debt Inheritance — Practitioner Convergence (Level 2-3)
**Source:** [https://www.nexgenarchitects.com/blog-posts/salesforce-agentforce-implementation-fails](https://www.nexgenarchitects.com/blog-posts/salesforce-agentforce-implementation-fails) — [practitioner analysis]
**Also:** [https://www.sweep.io/blog/the-5-salesforce-errors-that-break-agentforce](https://www.sweep.io/blog/the-5-salesforce-errors-that-break-agentforce) — [practitioner analysis]
**Also:** [https://www.salesforceben.com/4-critical-features-for-agentforce-architecture-in-2026/](https://www.salesforceben.com/4-critical-features-for-agentforce-architecture-in-2026/) — [practitioner direct]
**Date:** Q1 2026
**Agent level:** Company
**What:** Multiple independent Salesforce practitioners documenting the same pattern: **Agentforce inherits all your org's technical debt.** Five specific failure modes:
1. **CPU Time Limit Exceeded** — Agent Flow actions trigger every automation on the object (triggers, Flows, validation rules), consuming CPU time from same 10-second budget.
2. **DML Statement Limits** — Un-bulkified automations hit 150 DML statement limit before agent finishes a single request.
3. **Permission Failures** — Agent User needs access to every object/field/record any action might touch. "Works in testing (admin account) and fails in production" — the most insidious error pattern.
4. **15 topics / 15 actions per topic ceiling** — restricts complex multi-departmental workflows. Combined with prior finding of 20 agent maximum.
5. **Data quality as prerequisite** — Healthcare: 23% inaccuracies from duplicate records. Manufacturing: 40% longer lead times when agents couldn't adapt to vendor changes.

The convergent insight: "Agentforce fails when deployed on fragmented CRM architecture. The issue isn't AI capability — it's data readiness."
**Evidence level:** Level 2, approaching Level 3 (3+ independent practitioner sources documenting same failure patterns)
**Key claims:**
- 15 topics / 15 actions per topic hard limit
- Agent User permission model = works in test, fails in production
- Governor limits inherited from org = scaling wall
- Healthcare 23% inaccuracy, manufacturing 40% longer lead times (from practitioner sources, specific industries documented)
- Enterprise/higher edition required — locks out SMBs
- $2/conversation consumption pricing creates budget unpredictability

## Orientation

This cycle deepens the vertical SaaS picture with an important nuance: **vertical SaaS leads on revenue but lags on evidence exactly like horizontal platforms.** The announcement-to-deployment gap (Pattern 31) is universal — Salesforce's $800M ARR and ServiceNow's >$1B AI revenue are real business metrics, but they don't tell us how well the agents work in practice. The strongest vertical SaaS evidence remains in customer service (Finnair, Williams-Sonoma, IRS) — now 3-6 months old with no updates.

The Agentforce technical debt inheritance finding is the most actionable for our advisory: it explains WHY Agentforce deployments stall at a level our CTO audience will recognize. Governor limits, permission models, and data quality aren't AI problems — they're organizational readiness problems. This connects directly to Pattern 44 (clarity is the ceiling) and our competence-first positioning.

ServiceNow's governance-first approach (AI Control Tower, accountability framework) is architecturally the most mature answer to the governance question, but the accountability gap flagged by CIO.com ("who's accountable when the AI specialist goes wrong?") is exactly the question Nordic CTOs will ask.
