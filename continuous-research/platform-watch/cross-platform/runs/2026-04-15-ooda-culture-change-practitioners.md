# OODA Cycle: AI-Era Organizational Culture Change — Practitioner Accounts

**Date:** 2026-04-15 (updated — extends prior cycle with new practitioners and patterns)
**Cycle type:** People-first research
**Focus:** First-person practitioner accounts of "we tried to change our org's culture for AI — here's what happened"
**Method:** Tracked named practitioners across their own platforms (blog, Substack, X.com, podcast appearances, conference talks). Did NOT do broad topic searches.

## ORIENT: What We're Looking For

Not frameworks. Not prescriptions. People who lived through AI culture change inside an organization and wrote about what actually happened. The signal is in the gap between the memo and the reality.

### 1. Yoav Lax — AI Solutions Architect, Varonis (Transform-in-Place, Succeeded)

**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single experiment, practitioner direct)
**Source type:** [vendor blog — but written by the practitioner, not marketing]

**What he wrote:** "From Hype to Culture: How We Turned AI Adoption into Everyday Impact" (~April 2026)
**URL:** https://www.varonis.com/blog/impact-of-ai-adoption-engineering

**What actually happened:**
- Lax spent two years moving AI from experimentation to everyday engineering practice at Varonis
- Created an **AI Guild** — "an exclusive hub of practitioners who shape standards, share patterns, and unblock teams"
- Appointed **AI Champions** across teams as "field agents" for enablement
- Ran hands-on workshops lifting people from basic to advanced usage in a single day
- Ran internal **gen AI hackathons** focused on real day-to-day problems (not blue-sky projects) — described as "dry runs" before touching core product code
- Built an **internal AI Hub** — a web app centralizing how teams discover, use, and measure AI so adoption is "consistent, secure, and tied to outcomes"
- **Weekly feedback loops** to surface friction quickly and create momentum
- **Result:** 100% engineering team adoption by 2025, faster delivery cycles, fewer production bugs

**Key insight:** "Practical building beats theoretical training every time." The framework's sequence matters: detect enablers → broad rollout → monitor → level-up activities (guild, hackathons) → normalize in delivery processes. The AI Hub as organizational infrastructure — not just a tool — is the telling detail.

**Limitation:** Published on Varonis corporate blog. While written by the practitioner (not marketing), it's still a company blog — the incentive structure skews toward success narrative. No counter-evidence about what failed or who pushed back.

### 2. Farhan Thawar — VP & Head of Engineering, Shopify (Transform-in-Place, Succeeded with Caveats)

**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single experiment, practitioner reporting own results — unusually detailed across multiple independent interviews)
**Source type:** [practitioner direct] via multiple channels

**What happened after the Lütke memo (April 2025):**

*First Round Capital interview — "From Memo to Movement" (2026):*
- Thawar brought GitHub Copilot into Shopify "so early he couldn't even pay for the product yet" — a year before ChatGPT's release
- Achieved **80% Copilot adoption before the famous memo even existed** — the culture was already there
- AI reflexiveness now part of **biannual performance reviews** — "how quickly they turn to AI when encountering problems"
- Hired **1,000 interns** specifically for AI-native thinking and beginner's mindset
- Internal **prompt library** for reusing workflows
- Fastest-growing AI tool adoption was in **support and revenue, not engineering**

*Pragmatic Engineer podcast (July 2025, live at LDX3 London):*
- Built **LLM proxy** routing all AI requests through single platform layer — "MCP everything" to make every piece of internal data accessible
- **No cost limits on AI tokens** — uses internal leaderboard of highest token consumers
- Every engineering **director+ hire must pass a coding interview** — "still deeply in love with technology"
- Ordered **3,000 Cursor licenses** (two batches of 1,500)

*Bessemer Venture Partners interview (2026):*
- Estimates team is **20% more productive**
- 2026 focus: agentic harnesses — 10 agents working simultaneously with human review
- Warning about **"comprehension debt"** — AI accelerates development but engineers can't let their thinking atrophy
- Built **"Quick"** — internal tool where anyone deploys a JS/TS/HTML app by drag-and-drop

**Key insight:** Shopify's culture shift predated the memo by years. The memo accelerated something already underway. Infrastructure preceded mandate: LLM proxy, no token limits, coding interviews for leaders. The "comprehension debt" warning is the most honest thing any leader has said about AI culture change — the tool works but the human cost is skill atrophy.

**Counter-evidence (important):** Glassdoor reviews paint a different picture. Employees report "the wonderful people focused culture Shopify once had is dead" and "morale and engagement is extremely low." The Logic (Canadian outlet) reported exhaustion from constant layoffs alongside the AI mandate (14% cuts 2022, 20% cuts 2023, 2.5% by Dec 2024). The culture story from leadership and the culture story from the floor may be very different.

### 4. Luis von Ahn — CEO, Duolingo (Transform-in-Place, Mandate → Backtrack)

**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single experiment, CEO direct — candid about failure)
**Source type:** [general press] — Fortune, TechRadar, CNBC

**What happened — a year-long arc:**
- **April 2025:** Published "AI-first" memo. AI usage would be assessed in performance reviews. Contractors would be replaced by AI.
- **Public backlash:** Users threatened to delete the app. Employees questioned whether they were using AI "for AI's sake."
- **August 2025:** von Ahn admitted memo "did not give enough context." Insisted no full-time employees were laid off.
- **September 2025:** Claimed "4-5x content production with same headcount" — productivity gains real even as mandate was toxic.
- **April 2026:** Full reversal: "At the end, we backtracked. The most important thing in your performance is that you are doing whatever your job is as well as possible. A lot of times AI can help you with that. But if it can't, I'm not going to force you."
- **Admitted:** "We were trying to push something that in some cases did not fit."
- **No full-time employees laid off.** Headcount actually increased since the memo.

**Key insight:** The mandate was counterproductive — it turned AI into a political issue rather than a practical tool. When Duolingo shifted from "you must use AI" to "you must do your job well, and AI might help," adoption presumably continued without the cultural toxicity. Process beat mandate. The productivity gains were real, but the mandate nearly destroyed the culture that produced them.

### 5. Sebastian Siemiatkowski — CEO, Klarna (Transform-in-Place, Full Replacement → Reversal)

**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single experiment — the failure is the finding)
**Source type:** [general press] — Bloomberg, Fortune, CNBC

**What happened:**
- **Jan 2024:** Deployed OpenAI chatbot across 23 markets. Handled 2.3M conversations in first month.
- **Claimed:** AI doing work of 700 customer service agents. Company shrank from ~5,000 to ~3,000 employees.
- **The quality collapse:** Customer satisfaction deteriorated on complex interactions — edge cases, emotionally charged conversations, multi-step problem resolution.
- **May 2025 reversal:** Siemiatkowski admitted: "We focused too much on efficiency and cost. The result was lower quality, and that's not sustainable."
- **By early 2026:** Quietly rehiring human CS staff. Shifted to hybrid model — AI handles routine, humans handle escalation.
- **Oct 2025:** Siemiatkowski warned other "tech bros" are "sugarcoating just how badly" AI will impact jobs — a remarkable pivot from AI-cheerleader to doomsayer.

**What went wrong culturally:**
- No guild, no champions, no measurement of what matters (customer trust), no safe space for experimentation
- Employees notified via pre-recorded videos and blanket emails — "feeling discarded and devalued"
- Optimized for resolution speed and cost per ticket; didn't measure trust erosion or brand promise abandonment
- Gary Marcus dubbed this "The Klarna Effect" — arc from premature triumph declaration to quiet human rehiring

**Key insight:** Volume ≠ quality. The AI handled the throughput but failed on the interactions that build customer loyalty — the hard ones. You can't skip the competence-building step. Klarna had the CEO mandate but none of the infrastructure. The result was replacement, not transformation.

### 6. Amjad Masad — CEO, Replit (Born-AI-Native, Cultural Purge)

**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single experiment, practitioner direct — brutally honest)
**Source type:** [practitioner direct] via multiple interviews

**What happened:**
- Had 130 employees. Culture had "gotten slow and soft."
- Created an **"Agent Task Force"** to pivot to AI agents
- **People not on the task force felt demoralized** — didn't feel they were working on something important
- **Lost half the team** — went from 130 to ~65 employees
- **Result:** Revenue went from $2.8M to $150M ARR in under a year. $9B valuation.
- **New hiring philosophy:** "I would rather hire very smart generalists that can manage more AI agents." Looks for clear thinkers and communicators.
- **Company runs with ~70 people at $150M revenue** — versus 700 at comparable SaaS companies a decade ago
- **AI agents as literal coworkers:** "You can message them in Slack to create a PR, and they can go work for an hour or two"
- **HR team member built her own org chart software in three days** using agent capabilities

**Key insight:** The cultural purge was intentional. Masad didn't try to bring everyone along — he created a new identity (Agent Task Force), and the people who didn't fit self-selected out. Half the team leaving wasn't a failure; it was the mechanism. The culture changed by changing the people, not by changing people's minds. At 70 people producing $150M, the 10x organizational leverage is not a metaphor.

- https://www.vanta.com/resources/replit-future-of-code [practitioner direct via VC interview — "10x'ing in a Year"]

### 7. Jack Dorsey — CEO, Block (Transform-in-Place, Aggressive Restructuring — Contested)

**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single experiment — outcome genuinely unclear)
**Source type:** [general press] + [practitioner analysis]

**What happened:**
- **Cut 4,000 of ~10,000 employees** in Feb 2026 (40% workforce reduction)
- Replaced with **three-role structure:** individual contributors (build), directly responsible individuals (own outcomes on 90-day cycles), player-coaches (mentor while building)
- AI handles coordination, product decisions, internal alignment — functions middle managers previously performed
- Made AI fluency part of performance evaluations
- **Dorsey's trigger:** "capability shift he observed in December" in Opus 4.6 and Codex 5.3
- **Employee response:** Workers describe morale as "worst in years." Current/former employees report ~95% of AI-generated code changes still require human modification.
- **Quiet rehiring:** Some staff laid off in February were brought back in March.

**Counter-narrative (strong):** Gil Pignol (Medium) and Bloomberg documented the "AI-washing" argument:
- In **March 2025** layoff memo, Dorsey said: "None of the above points are trying to replace folks with AI."

- Sam Altman acknowledged the phenomenon: "There's some AI washing where people are blaming AI for layoffs that they would otherwise do."
- UVA Darden professor asked bluntly: "Is AI the strategy — or the scapegoat?"

**Key insight:** The gap between the CEO's AI narrative and the employee experience IS the story. Whether this is visionary restructuring or AI-washing of cost cuts remains genuinely unclear. The changing narrative between 2025 and 2026 is the most damning evidence.

### 9. Dario Amodei — CEO, Anthropic (Born-AI-Native, Culture by Design)

**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single experiment, practitioner direct via multiple interviews)
**Source type:** [general press] — Fortune, Inc, Benzinga (based on Lenny's Podcast interview)

**What happened:** Not a transform-in-place story — Anthropic built AI culture from founding. But the mechanisms are practitioner-relevant:

- **Spends 30-40% of his time on culture** — not products, not strategy, not fundraising
- **Biweekly "DVQ" (Dario Vision Quest)** — stands in front of entire company with 3-4 page document, speaks for an hour on topics from product strategy to geopolitics to AI industry
- **Every employee has a public Slack "notebook" channel** — shares ideas, questions, work-in-progress. Amodei has one too.
- **"Just argue with Dario" culture** — Amol Avasare (head of growth) described an employee publicly disagreeing with Amodei's statement during all-hands, posting criticism on his notebook channel, triggering company-wide debate. This is encouraged, not punished.
- **Self-described as "high-trust, low-ego"** — direct communication, assume good intentions, take responsibility regardless of role
- **Small teams own projects end-to-end** — significant engineer autonomy
- **2,500 employees, $380B valuation** as of Feb 2026

**Key insight:** Culture maintenance is the CEO's primary job allocation. 30-40% of time on culture at a $380B company is a radical resource allocation decision. The "notebook" channels + "argue with Dario" norm creates structural transparency that most companies only have in theory. This is what the born-AI-native company looks like when culture is designed rather than inherited.

**Caveat:** This is not a transformation story. The mechanisms are interesting for designing AI-era culture, but can't be transplanted to a company that already has a culture to change.

### 10. Rob Bowley + DX Research — "AI Accelerates Whatever Culture You Already Have" (Empirical Meta-Pattern)

**Evidence level:** Level 3 (convergence — 250,000+ developers surveyed, corroborated by DORA report)
**Source type:** [practitioner analysis] + [academic/research]

**What the data shows:**
- **91% of developers now use AI coding assistants** — adoption is near-universal
- **AI accelerates whatever culture you already have** — good practices compound; dysfunction compounds
- **Meetings, interruptions, review delays, and slow CI pipelines cost more than AI saves** — individual productivity tools can't fix organizational dysfunction
- **Time to 10th PR dropped from 91 to 49 days** for daily AI users
- **Staff+ engineers report biggest time savings (4.4 hrs/week)** but have lowest adoption rates
- **Modern engineering practices (small batch, trunk-based, automated testing, fast feedback) are the real multiplier** — AI makes their presence more valuable and their absence more costly
- Corroborated by DX Q4 2025 Impact Report and DORA State of AI-assisted Software Development Report

**Key insight:** This is the anti-mandate finding. You can't fix culture by adding AI tools. The orgs that benefit from AI are the ones that already had good engineering culture. The ones that didn't see AI amplify their existing problems. The gains from adopting continuous delivery dwarf what AI alone can deliver.

**Sources:**
- https://blog.robbowley.net/2025/11/05/findings-from-dxs-2025-report-ai-wont-save-you-from-your-engineering-culture/ [practitioner analysis — Rob Bowley's engineering blog, November 2025]

## ORIENT: Cross-Cutting Patterns

### Pattern A: The Mandate Trap (Duolingo, Block, IgniteTech vs. Shopify, Varonis)

Companies that mandated AI usage via performance reviews or threats encountered resistance, backlash, or cultural toxicity. Companies that built infrastructure, removed friction, and let adoption spread organically (Shopify's no-token-limits + LLM proxy; Varonis's AI Guild + hackathons) got adoption without the cultural damage.

**The exception:** IgniteTech mandated AND fired 80% — and it worked financially. But that's cultural replacement, not cultural change.

**Scale evidence (April 2026):** WalkMe survey found 80% of white-collar workers refuse AI adoption mandates outright. 29% admit to actively sabotaging their company's AI strategy (44% among Gen Z). Mandated AI use is emerging as a direct driver of resignations.
**Source:** https://fortune.com/2026/04/09/ai-backlash-quiet-quitting-fobo-obsolete-white-collar-rebellion/ [general press — Fortune, citing WalkMe survey]

### Pattern B: The Amplifier Effect (DX/DORA data, Level 3)

AI accelerates whatever culture you already have. This is the strongest empirical finding in the entire research (250,000+ developers, corroborated by DORA). Good engineering practices + AI = compounding gains. Bad practices + AI = compounding dysfunction. **Implication: fix your culture BEFORE deploying AI, not by deploying AI.**

### Pattern C: Three Approaches to AI Culture Change

| Approach | Examples | Mechanism | Speed | Durability | Human Cost |
|----------|----------|-----------|-------|------------|------------|
| **Infrastructure-first** | Shopify, Varonis | Build tools, remove friction, create communities | Slower | High | Low |
| **Mandate-first** | Duolingo, Block, IgniteTech | Declare mandatory, put in reviews | Fast on paper | Low (backlash) | Medium-High |
| **Structural-first** | Moderna, Bayer, Replit | Reorganize org to make AI integration structural | Varies | Potentially highest | Varies |

### Pattern D: The CEO's Time Allocation Tells the Real Story

- **Amodei:** 30-40% on culture → thriving born-AI-native culture
- **Lütke/Thawar:** Wrote memo + built infrastructure + trusted teams → organic adoption
- **von Ahn:** Wrote memo + put it in reviews + backtracked → mandate failure
- **Dorsey:** Wrote shareholder letter + cut 40% workforce → contested results
- **Vaughan:** "Changing minds was harder than adding skills" → replaced the people
- **Masad:** Created Agent Task Force → half the team self-selected out → 10x revenue

The CEO who spends the most time on culture (Amodei) has the healthiest culture. The CEOs who spent time on memos and mandates created the biggest backlash.

### Pattern E: The Success Playbook (3 independent practitioners converge)

Three independent practitioners (Lax/Varonis, Thawar/Shopify, Franklin/Moderna) describe the same playbook:
1. **Champions/Guild structure** — distributed enablement through identified power users, not top-down mandate
2. **Safe experimentation space** — hackathons, prompt contests, internal forums before production use
3. **Measurement of adoption** — token leaderboards, adoption percentages, GPT counts
4. **Infrastructure, not just tools** — internal LLM proxy, AI Hub, mChat platform
5. **Performance review integration** — making AI usage a formal evaluation criterion (but AFTER infrastructure, not before)

Klarna is the counter-case: skipped all five, went straight to replacement, collapsed on quality.

### Pattern F: "AI-Washing" as Cover for Cost Cuts (Emerging)

Block is the canonical case: CEO narrative changed from "not replacing folks with AI" (March 2025) to "intelligence tools changed what it means to build a company" (February 2026). Industry data: only 4.5% of 2025 layoffs cited AI; 59% of hiring managers admit using AI as cover for cuts driven by overhiring. Sam Altman himself acknowledged the phenomenon. UVA Darden asking "strategy or scapegoat?" The gap between the AI narrative and the cost-cutting reality is becoming a recognized pattern.

## What We Did NOT Find

1. **No rank-and-file employee at any company** has written publicly about what AI culture change feels like from the inside. Every account comes from C-suite or VP level. The people most affected by AI culture change are the least documented.

2. **No Shopify engineer below VP level** has written about the post-memo experience. Glassdoor reviews suggest the floor-level story contradicts leadership's narrative.

3. **No former Klarna CS employee** has written about the AI replacement experience. The displaced workers are voiceless in the public narrative.

4. **No Block employee has written by name** about the internal experience. Anonymous quotes to reporters only.

5. **No Nordic practitioner accounts** of AI culture change. Zero. Not one Nordic CTO, VP Engineering, or team lead has published a first-person account. Klarna is Swedish but the story is CEO-level, not practitioner-level.

6. **No practitioner accounts from traditional industries** (manufacturing, logistics, energy, healthcare operations). All accounts are from tech, fintech, or biotech. The traditional enterprise transformation is invisible at the practitioner level.

7. **No "it failed and here's why" insider account.** Failure stories are all told by outsiders or CEOs doing damage control. Nobody who lived through a failed AI culture change has written about it from the inside.

8. **No HR/operations/finance practitioner voice** on culture change for AI in their function. All accounts are from engineering/technology leaders. The business function side is silent.
