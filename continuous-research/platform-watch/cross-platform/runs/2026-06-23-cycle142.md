### Finding 2: July 8 Persona ID verification — biometric system quietly live since April 14, physical documents only, data held by Persona not Anthropic

**Source:** [TechTimes June 21 2026](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/318778/20260621/claude-identity-verification-starts-july-8-what-facial-data-anthropic-collects.htm) — domain trade publication; [Cybernews June 2026](https://cybernews.com/ai-news/anthropic-privacy-policy-id-verification/) — domain trade publication; [Medianama June 2026](https://www.medianama.com/2026/06/223-anthropic-widens-data-collection-id-verification-government-id-selfie-claude-users/) — domain trade publication
**Date:** June 21, 2026 (reporting); July 8, 2026 (system effective date)
**Agent level:** company
**What:** Three new details not captured in cycle 141's initial finding. (1) **The system was already running in limited form since April 14, 2026** — Anthropic quietly launched biometric ID verification through Persona Identities for "a few use cases" four months before the June announcement. July 8 is a formal expansion to all consumer tiers, not an entirely new system. (2) **Only original physical government documents qualify** — passports, driver's licenses, state/provincial ID cards, national identity cards. Photocopies, screenshots, digital IDs, student badges, and temporary paper IDs are all rejected. (3) **Government ID images and selfie data are stored on Persona Identities' servers, NOT on Anthropic's** — Persona is a San Francisco-based KYC platform backed by Founders Fund. Scope remains: Free, Pro, Max only (Enterprise, Team, API exempted).
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (three independent trade publications on implementation detail; policy document publicly verifiable)
**Key claims:**
- System quietly running since April 14, 2026 in limited form — not a new system, an expansion
- Physical-only documents (passports, driver's licenses, national ID cards) — no digital IDs, photocopies, student cards
- Biometric data (government ID + selfie + facial geometry templates) stored on Persona's servers, not Anthropic's
- Persona Identities = SF-based KYC vendor backed by Founders Fund
- Consumer accounts only (Free/Pro/Max) — Enterprise, Team, API exempted
- **Enterprise implication**: Employees without physical government-issued IDs (various categories including non-US citizens at US firms on consumer plans) face access restrictions to consumer Fable 5 restoration under this path

### Finding 3: GLM-5.2 — Fable 5 ban creates open-source acceleration, MIT licensed, 1/6th cost, top BridgeBench reasoning

**Source:** [VentureBeat June 2026](https://venturebeat.com/technology/z-ais-open-weights-glm-5-2-beats-gpt-5-5-on-multiple-long-horizon-coding-benchmarks-for-1-6th-the-cost/) — domain trade publication; [The Decoder June 2026](https://the-decoder.com/zhipu-ais-glm-5-2-closes-in-on-closed-source-leaders-in-coding-marathons/) — domain trade publication; [CNBC June 16 2026](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/16/anthropics-fable-shutdown-is-a-big-moment-for-open-source-ai.html) — general press; [Fortune June 16 2026](https://fortune.com/2026/06/16/us-anthropic-ban-open-source-ai-deepseek-zai/) — general press; [Pandaily June 2026](https://pandaily.com/zhipu-ai-glm-5-dot-2-open-source-mit-jun2026) — domain trade publication
**Date:** June 13–16, 2026
**Agent level:** company
**What:** Zhipu AI (rebranded Z.ai) launched GLM-5.2 on June 13, one day after the Fable 5 ban. The timing is significant: CNBC (June 16) and Fortune (June 16) both ran pieces explicitly framing the Fable 5 ban as "a big moment for open-source AI" and "Fable fiasco leaves the door open for open-source AI, particularly cheaper models from China." Technical specs: 744B total / 40B active parameters MoE, 1M token context window (4x predecessor), MIT open-source license, available on Hugging Face and 20+ third-party coding environments. **Performance:** tops BridgeBench Reasoning at 42.8; beats GPT-5.5 on multiple long-horizon coding benchmarks; matches or beats Opus 4.8 on most production workloads. **Cost:** ~$1.40/M input, $4.40/M output via OpenRouter (vs GPT-5.5 at $5/$30, Claude Opus at $5/$25) — approximately 1/6th the cost of US frontier models. Enterprise tiers from $12.60/month. The strategic implication per CNBC: "access can be cut off without warning — that could accelerate momentum behind open-source AI, as companies look for models they can download, run on their own infrastructure and customize around their own data." Fortune explicitly names DeepSeek and Z.ai as beneficiaries. The New Stack framed it: "4 open models responded before Anthropic could restore access."
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (VentureBeat + The Decoder independent trade coverage; CNBC + Fortune independent general press; benchmarks are third-party published on BridgeBench)
**Key claims:**
- GLM-5.2 launched June 13, one day after Fable 5 ban — timing explicit in multiple sources
- MIT license, Hugging Face available, 20+ coding environments — independently redistributable across jurisdictions
- BridgeBench Reasoning: 42.8 (tops the benchmark per Zhipu's own claim, independently referenced by VentureBeat + The Decoder)
- Cost: ~1/6th of US frontier models at OpenRouter rates ($1.40/$4.40 vs $5/$30 for GPT-5.5)
- CNBC (June 16) + Fortune (June 16): both independently characterize ban as strategic opening for open-source AI
- Key enterprise risk thesis now externally confirmed: "closed-source access can be cut off without warning" = open-source resilience argument
- An export control on open weights is "a different problem entirely — weights exist on servers across many jurisdictions" (CNBC framing)

## Orientation

The Fable 5 ban is now producing measurable second-order effects. GLM-5.2 launching one day after the ban — and CNBC/Fortune explicitly framing the ban as "a big moment for open-source AI" — marks the point where the US export control story crosses into a strategic platform-watch finding. For CTO audiences: the risk of closed-source model dependency is now validated by a real event, and the alternatives (MIT-licensed, self-hostable, significantly cheaper) are competitive on key benchmarks. This strengthens the "open-source as resilience strategy" argument that was previously theoretical. The July 8 Persona path (consumer-only, physical documents, biometrics on a third-party's servers) has significant enterprise adoption friction — it is not a smooth Enterprise/API restoration mechanism.
