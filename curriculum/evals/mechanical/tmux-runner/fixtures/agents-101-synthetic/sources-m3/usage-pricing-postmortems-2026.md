# Two Years of Usage-Based Pricing in SMB: A Postmortem Roundup

_Practitioner roundup · published last month · fresh field reports, not vendor copy_

Most of the usage-based-pricing debate argues from first principles. This is a
roundup of operators who actually shipped it to small-business segments and
lived with the result for 18–24 months. The pattern is sharper than the debate.

## Marit Halvorsen — ran the pricing migration at a fleet-logistics SaaS

Halvorsen's team moved an SMB-heavy book from per-seat to usage-based and
watched **two SMB logos churn in week six** of their own pilot — both over a
single spiky invoice, exactly the bill-shock failure the skeptics warn about.
Then they changed one thing and the churn stopped: a **graduated cap**. The
customer's bill cannot rise more than a fixed percentage month-over-month, no
matter what usage does; the overage above the cap is smoothed into the next
period. Halvorsen's claim is specific and falsifiable: *SMB churn under
usage-based pricing is a pricing-design problem, not a segment problem.* The
volatility is real, but it is an artifact of the **invoice shape**, not of
small customers being structurally wrong for usage-based pricing. After the cap
shipped, the SMB cohort's churn fell back to its per-seat baseline within two
quarters while expansion revenue kept the upside.

This contradicts the harder "structurally wrong for volatile SMB" reading head
on. Halvorsen's position: the people who concluded "SMB just can't take
usage-based" had simply never shipped a cap — they killed the model before
fixing the invoice.

## Where the roundup still hedges

The cap is not free. It caps your upside in exactly the months a customer
spikes — the months usage-based pricing was supposed to capture. Halvorsen
concedes the graduated cap trades ~15% of peak-month expansion revenue for the
retention. Whether that trade is worth it depends on how spiky your SMB
segment's usage actually is, which is the one number none of the postmortems
could give a clean answer on.
