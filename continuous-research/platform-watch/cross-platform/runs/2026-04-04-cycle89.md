## Findings

### Finding 1: Rishi Baldawa — Little's Law Explains Why AI Breaks Your Process
**Source:** https://rishi.baldawa.com/posts/pr-throughput/littles-law-ai-breaks-process/ — [practitioner direct]
**Date:** November 15, 2025
**What:** Explicit application of Little's Law (L = λW) to AI-assisted development. When AI increases PR arrival rate (λ↑) and each PR is harder to review due to more copy-paste and higher bug rates (service rate μ↓), queues grow exponentially. Cites GitClear analysis of 211M lines of code (2020–2024): copy-pasted code up 48%, code refactoring down from 24.1% to 9.5%, bug rates up 41% for Copilot users. Cites Cisco study: review effectiveness drops sharply above 450 LOC/hour.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (practitioner directly applying a recognized operations research framework to AI deployment data)
**Significance:** This is the most direct named-practitioner application of Little's Law to AI-induced review bottleneck found in research. Names the two-sided problem: arrival rate up, service rate down simultaneously.

### Finding 2: Faros Research — The AI Productivity Paradox (n=large enterprise dataset)
**Source:** https://www.faros.ai/blog/ai-software-engineering — [domain trade publication / research]
**Date:** July 23, 2025
**What:** Quantitative research report finding that AI increases individual output while organizational metrics stay flat. Key data: 98% more PRs merged per developer, but PR review time increases 91%. Teams complete 21% more tasks, yet delivery metrics (lead time, defect rate, deployment frequency) remain flat. Explicitly states: "AI-driven coding gains evaporate when review bottlenecks, brittle testing, and slow release pipelines can't match the new velocity." Frames this as Amdahl's Law — system speed limited by slowest component, now shifted to review.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (quantitative dataset, not a single practitioner but not yet independent convergence)
**Significance:** Provides the numbers behind the pattern. 98% more PRs + 91% more review time = the absorption paradox in concrete form.

### Finding 3: Simon Willison — Cognitive Exhaustion as Absorption Constraint
**Source:** https://simonwillison.net/2026/Apr/2/lennys-podcast/ — [practitioner direct] (also https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/an-ai-state-of-the-union)
**Date:** April 2, 2026
**What:** Willison — Django co-creator, ~25 years experience — describes running four parallel AI coding agents as "mentally exhausting...using every inch of my 25 years of experience." He is wiped out by 11 AM. Identifies a "capacity inversion": computational tasks become easier while human attention becomes the scarce resource. Also notes an addiction pattern: agents working overnight while he can't stop checking. The bottleneck shifts from implementation to: (a) quality evaluation (he lacks confidence without extended real-world usage), and (b) cognitive load of managing parallel agents. Describes coding well with agents as taking "every inch" of his experience.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single senior practitioner, direct testimony, highly credible source)
**Significance:** Willison is the most credible single practitioner voice on this. His description of cognitive exhaustion from parallel agent oversight is the first direct evidence of the absorption bottleneck at the individual level — not the org level.

### Finding 4: Joseph Ruscio (Heavybit) — Write-Only Code: AI Will Exceed Review Capacity
**Source:** https://www.heavybit.com/library/article/write-only-code — [practitioner direct / domain trade publication]

**Evidence level:** Level 1 (opinion/prediction, but from an informed VC position with visibility into developer tool adoption)
**Significance:** Represents the industry layer processing the bottleneck shift signal. Not practitioner deployment evidence, but signals where the infrastructure-layer ecosystem is moving.

### Finding 5: Ethan Mollick — "Bottlenecks and Salients" Framework
**Source:** https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/the-shape-of-ai-jaggedness-bottlenecks — [practitioner direct / academic]
**Date:** December 20, 2025
**What:** Mollick identifies "process-based bottlenecks" as a distinct category: even with superior AI performance, institutional processes create delays. Drug discovery example: AI identifies candidates faster, but "clinical trials still need actual human patients who take actual time to recruit, dose, and monitor." Key phrase: "the bottleneck migrates from intelligence to institutions, and institutions move at institution speed." Doesn't specifically address coding review but provides the theoretical framework — Goldratt's constraint logic without naming Goldratt.
**Evidence level:** Level 1 (analytical framework, not deployment evidence)
**Significance:** Mollick provides independent convergence on the TOC thesis from an organizational behavior perspective. His framing "institutions move at institution speed" is the decision-speed bottleneck claim in academic form.

### Finding 6: Bertrand Duperrin — AI Productivity Gains Are Absorbed by Organizational Complexity
**Source:** https://www.duperrin.com/english/2025/10/22/ai-productivity-technology/ — [practitioner analysis]
**Date:** October 22, 2025
**What:** Duperrin (management/digital transformation consultant) applies Jevons' Paradox to AI productivity: "Increased efficiency often generates more usage and controls, creating overload and complexity." Key claim: "Improving one link without addressing the bottleneck creates queues and waste." Gains "frequently absorbed by existing organizational complexity, as if the organization had an almost mechanical ability to consume any local improvement without ever turning it into overall progress." Second relevant article (Oct 7, 2025, https://www.duperrin.com/english/2025/10/07/theory-constraints-productivity-global-local/): explicitly applies TOC to knowledge work, stating that "AI enables spectacular gains in specific tasks, but these isolated advances do not guarantee better overall performance." Also: Nov 28, 2025 article on Goldratt's The Goal applied to knowledge organizations.
**Evidence level:** Level 1 (analytical, consultant perspective)
**Significance:** Duperrin provides the TOC-in-knowledge-work framing from a non-coding perspective. His "organization has a mechanical ability to consume any local improvement" is a compact statement of the absorption bottleneck.

### Finding 7: ActivTrak Report / Emma Burleigh (Fortune) — AI Doesn't Reduce Workloads, It Intensifies Them
**Source:** https://fortune.com/2026/03/13/ai-isnt-reducing-workloads-its-straining-employees-time-spent-emailing-doubled-deep-focus-work-fell/ — [general press, sourcing ActivTrak report, n=10,584 users over 180 days]
**Date:** March 13, 2026
**What:** ActivTrak behavioral analytics data (10,584 users, 180 days): time spent on email increased 104%, messaging 145%, business management tools 94%. Deep-focus work sessions fell 9%. The report states: "The data is unambiguous: AI does not reduce workloads." BCG research embedded in the article: efficiency gains only occur with three or fewer AI tools; productivity plummets with four or more. Identifies "AI brain fry" — cognitive overwhelm from managing multiple tools.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (large-scale behavioral data, though from a vendor — ActivTrak sells workforce analytics; methodology not fully disclosed; use with caveat)
**Significance:** Large-sample behavioral data showing the absorption bottleneck outside coding. Email + messaging increases from AI outputs (AI generates more drafts, summaries, action items) while focus time falls — the pattern holds in knowledge work broadly, not just engineering.

### Finding 8: UC Berkeley Study — AI Adoption Generates More Tasks, Not Less Work
**Source:** https://fortune.com/2026/02/10/ai-future-of-work-white-collar-employees-technology-productivity-burnout-research-uc-berkeley/ — [general press sourcing academic research]
**Date:** February 10, 2026
**What:** UC Berkeley researchers studied a 200-person tech firm over 8 months, 40 in-depth interviews. Finding: workers didn't reduce hours — they added more tasks. One employee: "You had thought that maybe, 'Oh, because you could be more productive with AI, then you save some time, you can work less.' But then really, you don't work less. You just work the same amount or even more." AI filled natural breaks with tasks, increasing multitasking and reducing overall productivity despite initial gains. Potential for burnout and cognitive fatigue.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (academic research, n=200 single company, 8 months, qualitative)
**Significance:** Cross-domain confirmation of the absorption claim: AI accelerates task generation, humans absorb new tasks rather than reducing time. The bottleneck shifts from task generation to human cognitive capacity — the Little's Law pattern in knowledge work terms.

### Finding 9: Andrew Baker — The Dark Factory and Org Chart as Bottleneck
**Source:** https://andrewbaker.ninja/2026/03/22/the-dark-factory-why-most-teams-are-getting-slower-while-a-few-are-building-software-without-any-humans/ — [practitioner direct]
**Date:** March 22, 2026
**What:** Baker (CIO) argues that coordination layers designed for human implementation become pure friction once AI handles coding. The bottleneck shifts: "Copilot makes writing code cheaper but owning it more expensive." METR study (2025): experienced developers took 19% longer with AI tools, believed they were 20-24% faster. Most developers remain at Level 2-3 (reading every diff) while Level 5 (dark factory / StrongDM) eliminates the review bottleneck entirely via automated QA. The path between is the problem: review bottleneck is real at current adoption levels.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (practitioner analysis, cites METR study)
**Significance:** Identifies the important nuance: the review bottleneck is an intermediate state, not permanent. The dark factory (StrongDM) counter-example shows review bottleneck can be automated away — but requires building scenario-based holdout testing infrastructure most teams don't have.

### Finding 10: StrongDM — Counter-Evidence: Review Bottleneck Solved via Automated QA
**Source:** https://simonwillison.net/2026/Feb/7/software-factory/ — [practitioner direct / practitioner analysis]
**Date:** February 7, 2026 (Willison's writeup of StrongDM's McCarthy's approach)
**What:** StrongDM (three engineers) produced 32,000 lines of production code since July 2025 without writing or reviewing a single line by hand. The bottleneck is eliminated not by removing review but by automating it: behavioral scenarios as holdout validation, hidden from the agent during development. "Satisfaction" metric quantifies passing trajectories. Digital twin universe mimics Okta, Jira, Slack, Google Docs, etc. The review constraint is replaced by specification quality — writing scenarios detailed enough to validate unknown agent behavior.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single team, three engineers, practitioner-reported)
**Significance:** Critical counter-evidence. The review bottleneck CAN be eliminated — but requires significant infrastructure investment (building digital twins, scenario harnesses). The constraint doesn't disappear — it shifts to specification and scenario quality. This is Goldratt confirmed: solve one constraint, next constraint appears.

## What I Looked For But Did Not Find

1. **Named enterprise teams explicitly tracking Little's Law or TOC metrics against their AI adoption.** No enterprise has published "we measured our WIP before and after AI adoption and here's the queue data." The Faros report comes closest with 98% more PRs / 91% more review time, but this is researcher-compiled data, not a named company doing this measurement internally.

2. **Will Larson specifically on review capacity or throughput constraints.** His blog (lethain.com) surfaced in searches but no specific posts on this topic in the Oct 2025–Apr 2026 window. The searches returned infrastructure/hardware bottleneck articles, not his practitioner writing on engineering management.

3. **Named enterprise (non-engineering) practitioners explicitly framing the decision-speed bottleneck.** The TOC and decision-speed claims are present in consultant and analyst content (Duperrin, AgileSherpas, Mollick framework) but no named company has published "our decisions became the constraint after AI accelerated generation." This is the most important gap.

4. **Nordic practitioner evidence.** No Nordic-specific practitioner observations on review bottleneck or absorption. The search found general Nordic AI maturity reports (BCG, Accenture) showing only 4% of companies achieve strong returns — consistent with the absorption pattern but not practitioner-direct.

5. **Goldratt practitioners explicitly applying TOC to AI agent deployment.** The Stanford lecture (Andy Bromberg, Feb 2026) applies TOC to AI application architecture (prompt engineering, context) — not to organizational throughput. The organizational-level Goldratt application remains at the consultant-opinion level (Duperrin, AgileSherpas), not practitioner-direct.

6. **Evidence that overworked organizations do WORSE with AI due to the bottleneck shift.** The UC Berkeley study (more tasks, not less work) and METR study (19% slower) support the general claim, but no study specifically tracks whether teams with pre-existing high WIP perform worse with AI than teams with low WIP. The specific "overloaded teams → WIP explosion → worse outcomes" chain is unverified at Level 3.

7. **Jason Fried / DHH published views on AI output and review capacity.** Searches found DHH is integrating AI into Basecamp but no posts on review or absorption.

## Pattern Assessment: Where Is This on the Evidence Ladder?

**Absorption bottleneck (Little's Law applied to AI):**
- Level 3 approaching, not fully there yet. Multiple independent signals point the same direction:
  - Rishi Baldawa: explicit Little's Law application to PR reviews [practitioner direct]
  - Faros Research: 98% more PRs + 91% more review time = absorption paradox [research]
  - Simon Willison: cognitive exhaustion from 4 parallel agents by 11 AM [practitioner direct]
  - ActivTrak (n=10,584): 104% more email, 9% less focus time [behavioral data]
  - UC Berkeley (n=200, 8 months): more tasks not less work [academic]
  - Andrew Baker: review bottleneck confirmed for developers not yet at dark factory level [practitioner]
  - Duperrin: organizational complexity absorbs any local productivity gain [practitioner analysis]
- 6-7 independent signals across 3+ domains (software engineering, knowledge work broadly, academic). Pattern is at the boundary of Level 2→3. Not fully Level 3 because the signals cluster in engineering; broader enterprise is less documented.

**Decision-speed bottleneck (Goldratt's TOC applied to AI):**
- Level 1-2. Theoretical convergence, not deployment evidence:
  - Mollick: "institutions move at institution speed" [academic framing]
  - Duperrin: AI relieves non-constraint, overall throughput unchanged [practitioner analysis]
  - AgileSherpas: "AI doesn't eliminate constraints, it shifts them" [consultant content, Level 0]
  - Anthropic 2026 report: "bottleneck no longer code writing but decision-making" [vendor, Level 0]
- No named practitioner has published "we deployed AI and our decision approval process became the constraint." The claim is theoretically sound and aligns with Goldratt, but lacks convergence at the practitioner level.

**Counter-evidence (dark factory / StrongDM):**
- The StrongDM case (Level 2) shows the review bottleneck CAN be eliminated via automated QA — but this requires significant infrastructure investment. StrongDM is an existence proof, not a template. Goldratt confirmed here: eliminate one constraint (review), next constraint appears (specification quality / scenario writing).

## Orientation

The absorption bottleneck claim is approaching Level 3 convergence — enough independent practitioner signals exist to treat it as a reportable pattern with appropriate uncertainty labeling. The decision-speed bottleneck (TOC applied to approval/judgment cycles) is at Level 1-2 — theoretically sound and consistent with all available evidence, but no practitioner has yet published "our approval cycles became our constraint after AI deployment." The StrongDM counter-example is important: the review bottleneck is an intermediate state that can be automated away, but only with significant QA infrastructure investment — meaning the pattern holds for most teams but is not structural.
