## The Original Source -- Found

**The report exists.** It is not a phantom citation. The source is:

> **"The GenAI Divide: State of AI in Business 2025"**
> Authors: Aditya Challapally, Chris Pease, Ramesh Raskar, Pradyumna Chari
> Published: July 2025
> Publisher: MIT Project NANDA (MIT Media Lab)
> PDF: https://mlq.ai/media/quarterly_decks/v0.1_State_of_AI_in_Business_2025_Report.pdf
> [academic/research -- MIT Media Lab project]

**What it actually says (exact language from the executive summary, p.3):**

> "Despite $30-40 billion in enterprise investment into GenAI, this report uncovers a surprising result in that 95% of organizations are getting zero return. The outcomes are so starkly divided across both buyers (enterprises, mid-market, SMBs) and builders (startups, vendors, consultancies) that we call it the GenAI Divide. Just 5% of integrated AI pilots are extracting millions in value, while the vast majority remain stuck with no measurable P&L impact."

**And more precisely from p.6 (section 3.2, "The Pilot-to-Production Chasm"):**

> "Only 5% of custom enterprise AI tools reach production."

The chart on p.6 shows a funnel:
- General-Purpose LLMs: 80% investigated -> 50% piloted -> 40% "successfully implemented"
- Embedded/Task-Specific GenAI: 60% investigated -> 20% piloted -> **5% successfully implemented**

So the 95% failure rate applies specifically to **embedded or task-specific GenAI tools** -- NOT to all AI use, NOT to ChatGPT-type tools (which show ~40% implementation), and NOT even to "all GenAI pilots."

## The Methodology -- This Is Where It Gets Ugly

**Sample:**
- 52 structured interviews with organization representatives
- 153 survey responses from senior leaders at 4 industry conferences
- Systematic review of 300+ publicly disclosed AI initiatives

**The report's own disclaimer (p.6):**

> "**Research Limitations:** These figures are directionally accurate based on individual interviews rather than official company reporting. Sample sizes vary by category, and success definitions may differ across organizations."

**Definition of success (p.7):**

> "We define successfully implemented for task-specific GenAI tools as ones users or executives have remarked as causing a marked and sustained productivity and/or P&L impact."

**Critical methodological problems:**

1. **Tiny sample, huge claim.** The 95% figure derives from 52 interviews. Not 52 companies with rigorous measurement -- 52 conversations where someone gave their impression. This is not a statistically valid sample for a claim about enterprise AI globally.

2. **Self-reported, subjective.** Success is defined by whether someone "remarked" on productivity/P&L impact. No independent measurement. No baseline. No control group.

3. **"Directionally accurate" = we're guessing.** The report itself says the numbers are "directionally accurate" -- i.e., roughly in the right direction but not precise. Yet the headline is "95%" -- a specific, precise-sounding number derived from admittedly imprecise data.

4. **Narrow success definition.** Only "marked and sustained productivity and/or P&L impact" counts. Efficiency gains, cost avoidance, employee satisfaction, risk reduction, learning -- none of these count. Many legitimate AI deployments that deliver real value would be classified as "failures" under this definition.

5. **The 95% is about a SUBSET, not all AI.** The report's own data shows general-purpose LLMs (ChatGPT, Copilot) achieve ~40% successful implementation. The 95% failure rate applies only to "embedded or task-specific" custom tools. The media universally dropped this distinction.

6. **Conference-recruited survey.** The 153 survey respondents were collected at industry conferences -- a self-selecting sample biased toward people actively exploring AI (likely more sophisticated than average, or alternatively, people frustrated enough to attend sessions about it).

7. **No peer review.** This is a working paper from a project group, not a peer-reviewed publication. The version number in the PDF filename (`v0.1_State_of_AI_in_Business_2025_Report.pdf`) suggests it may be a draft or early release.

## Who Is Behind This?

**Project NANDA** = "Networked Agents and Decentralized Architecture" -- an MIT Media Lab project led by Prof. Ramesh Raskar focused on building infrastructure for an "Internet of AI Agents." It is a legitimate MIT research group, but its core mission is agent interoperability infrastructure, not enterprise adoption measurement.

- URL: https://www.media.mit.edu/projects/mit-nanda/overview/ [academic/research]

**Aditya Challapally** (lead author) = Principal Applied Scientist at Microsoft, teaches GenAI at Stanford, co-leads a working group at MIT Media Lab. He has relevant credentials but is not primarily a survey methodologist or enterprise adoption researcher.

- Source: LinkedIn profile and DPW NYC speaker bio [practitioner direct]

**The conflict of interest nobody mentions:** Project NANDA is building agent infrastructure. A report that says "95% of GenAI deployments fail because tools don't learn, adapt, or integrate" and the solution is "agentic systems with memory and learning" -- which is exactly what NANDA is building -- has a structural alignment between the finding and the funder's mission. This doesn't mean the finding is wrong, but it's worth noting.

## The Citation Chain -- How 95% Went Viral

**Stage 1: The Fortune article (Aug 18, 2025)**
- Sheryl Estrada, Fortune: "MIT report: 95% of generative AI pilots at companies are failing"
- https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/
- [general press] -- This is the article that went viral. The headline drops all nuance. It says "95% of generative AI pilots" when the report says "95% of embedded/task-specific GenAI tools." Fortune is not a source that understands agents.

**Stage 2: Republication cascade**
Every downstream article cites either the Fortune article or the PDF directly, but virtually ALL of them:
- Drop the distinction between general-purpose LLMs and task-specific tools
- Report "95% of AI pilots fail" rather than "95% of custom enterprise GenAI tools show no P&L impact"
- Omit the "directionally accurate" caveat
- Omit the 52-interview sample size

**Stage 3: Vendor content marketing**
Dozens of vendors (Dataiku, Dawiso, Congruity360, Selector AI, Sweep, Salesforce, Unframe, etc.) published "Why 95% of AI pilots fail -- and how OUR product fixes it" blog posts. Every single one cites the MIT report uncritically and uses the stat as a lead-in to their sales pitch. These are Level 0 sources exploiting a Level 2 finding.

## The Zombie Stat Family -- This Is a Pattern

The "95% of GenAI pilots fail" is the latest member of a long-running family of AI/IT failure statistics that all share the same DNA: scary number + prestigious attribution + vague methodology + vendor-friendly narrative.

**The family tree:**

| Stat | Attribution | Original Claim | What It Actually Said | Methodology |
|------|------------|----------------|----------------------|-------------|
| **85% of AI projects fail** | "Gartner" | AI projects fail at 85% rate | "Through 2022, 85% of AI projects will deliver **erroneous outcomes** due to bias" (Feb 2018 forecast) | Analyst prediction, not measurement. A forecast about output quality, not project failure. |
| **80% of AI projects fail** | RAND Corp (2024) | 80% failure rate, 2x IT baseline | "By some estimates, more than 80%" -- RAND is citing others, not measuring. | 65 interviews with data scientists. Qualitative, not quantitative. Similar to MIT NANDA approach. |
| **87% of ML projects never reach production** | VentureBeat (2019) | ML projects don't deploy | Widely cited, original methodology unclear | [SOURCE NEEDED for original VentureBeat methodology] |
| **95% of GenAI pilots fail** | MIT NANDA (2025) | 95% of pilots get zero return | 95% of **custom/embedded GenAI tools** show no P&L impact per interviews | 52 interviews, self-reported, "directionally accurate" |

**The pattern:** Each of these stats originates from a small-sample qualitative study or an analyst prediction, gets stripped of caveats by press coverage, and then gets cited by hundreds of vendors as a lead-in to their sales pitch. None of them are based on rigorous, large-sample quantitative measurement of actual project outcomes.

## Alternative Data -- What IS the Best Available Evidence?

There is no rigorous, large-sample, independently-verified measurement of GenAI pilot success/failure rates. Period. The best available is:

**1. RAND Corporation (2024)** -- "The Root Causes of Failure for Artificial Intelligence Projects and How They Can Succeed"
- https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA2680-1.html [academic/research]
- 65 interviews with data scientists/engineers
- "By some estimates, more than 80% of AI projects fail -- twice the rate of IT projects that don't involve AI"
- Better methodology than MIT NANDA (interviews with practitioners, not executives at conferences), but also qualitative and small-sample. Notably, RAND is citing OTHER estimates for the 80% number, not claiming it as their own finding.

**2. Gartner (various years)** -- prediction, not measurement
- 2018: "85% will deliver erroneous outcomes" (a forecast about bias, not failure)
- 2025: "30% of GenAI initiatives will be abandoned after proof of concept by end of 2025"
- [analyst prediction] -- Level 0 by our standards, but at least the 30% number is more conservative and specific

**3. The real answer:** Nobody actually knows the real number because:
- "Pilot" is not consistently defined
- "Failure" is not consistently defined
- Companies don't publicly report pilot failures
- The denominator (total pilots) is unknown
- Success may take 9-18 months to manifest, but most studies measure at 6 months

## Verdict

**Classification: Semi-zombie stat. The source exists, but the stat is significantly misleading as commonly cited.**

The MIT NANDA report is real. It IS from MIT (Media Lab). It WAS published by credentialed researchers. But:

1. **The 95% applies to a subset (custom/task-specific tools), not all GenAI.** The report's own data shows ~40% success for general-purpose LLMs. The media universally dropped this distinction.

2. **The sample is too small for the claim.** 52 interviews cannot support a headline claim about global enterprise GenAI adoption. The report acknowledges this ("directionally accurate").

3. **The success definition is too narrow.** Only "marked P&L impact" counts. Efficiency gains, risk reduction, learning, process improvement -- none qualify. This inflates the "failure" rate by defining success so narrowly that most legitimate deployments don't qualify.

4. **The report has a structural conflict of interest.** Project NANDA builds agent infrastructure. The finding that "static GenAI tools fail, agentic systems succeed" directly supports NANDA's product thesis.

5. **The citation chain is a textbook example of stat degradation.** MIT report (nuanced) -> Fortune headline (simplified) -> vendor blogs (weaponized). By the time it reaches a CTO's inbox, "95% of custom enterprise GenAI tools showed no P&L impact in 52 interviews" has become "95% of ALL AI projects fail, says MIT."

**Should we cite it?**

**No -- not as "95% of GenAI pilots fail."** That framing is misleading.

**Yes -- with heavy qualification** if we want to reference the underlying finding. Something like:

> "A 2025 MIT Media Lab study based on 52 enterprise interviews found that while general-purpose AI tools like ChatGPT show reasonable adoption (~40% reaching production use), custom enterprise GenAI tools have a much steeper drop-off, with only about 5% reaching sustained production impact. The study's authors note these figures are 'directionally accurate' rather than statistically precise. [MIT NANDA, 'The GenAI Divide,' July 2025]"

**The real insight from this audit:** The GenAI pilot failure rate is genuinely unknown. Every commonly-cited number (70%, 80%, 85%, 95%) traces back to either a small qualitative study, an analyst prediction, or a stat that mutated through citation. The absence of rigorous data IS the finding. Any CTO who quotes a specific failure rate with confidence is citing a zombie.

## Recommended Action for the Research System

1. **Remove or heavily qualify** any unqualified citation of "95% of GenAI pilots fail" from our materials
2. **Stop citing failure rate percentages** as if they're established facts. They're not.
3. **Use the NANDA report for its qualitative insights** (learning gap, shadow AI economy, buy-vs-build pattern) which are more defensible than its headline number
4. **Add this audit to our patterns file** as an example of the "zombie stat" phenomenon in AI adoption discourse
