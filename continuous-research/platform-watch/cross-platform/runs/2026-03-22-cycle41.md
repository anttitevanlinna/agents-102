## Findings

### Finding 1: Mollick's "Leadership, Lab, Crowd" Framework — Most Structured Adoption Model
**Source:** [https://www.valence.co/ai-and-the-workforce/ai-agents-agentic-work-the-future-of-work-ethan-mollick](https://www.valence.co/ai-and-the-workforce/ai-agents-agentic-work-the-future-of-work-ethan-mollick) — [practitioner analysis]
**Date:** Q1 2026
**Agent level:** Company
**What:** Mollick proposes three integrated components for organizational AI transformation: (1) **Leadership** establishes vision, clarifies how efficiency gains will be used (growth vs layoffs), creates experimental zones; (2) **The Crowd** = employees discovering applications through trial-and-error (experienced workers assess AI utility better than outsiders, but many hide use due to fear); (3) **The Lab** = centralized ambidextrous team that rapidly distributes crowd-discovered solutions, builds benchmarks, creates "provocations" demonstrating transformative potential. Critical gap identified: individual performance gains don't translate to organizational impact automatically — this is the "organizational bridge" problem.
**Evidence level:** Level 1-2 (Mollick's framework with references to multiple studies — Danish knowledge workers halved working time for 41% of tasks, American workers claim 3x productivity — but no controlled organizational deployment evidence)
**Key claims:**
- Individual AI productivity gains are real but don't automatically translate to org-level impact
- Experienced workers discover better AI applications than outsiders/consultants
- Many workers hide AI use (fear of job loss or raised expectations)
- The organizational bridge between individual and team productivity is unsolved

### Finding 2: MIT AI Pilot Failure — Specific Mechanisms Identified
**Source:** [https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/](https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/) — [general press, citing academic/research]
**Date:** August 2025
**Agent level:** Company
**What:** MIT study (150 interviews, 350 employee surveys, 300 public AI deployments) found ~95% of enterprise AI pilots stall with little measurable P&L impact. Four specific failure mechanisms: (1) **Learning gap** — generic tools don't learn from/adapt to workflows; (2) **Resource misallocation** — 50%+ of GenAI budgets target sales/marketing, yet biggest ROI in back-office automation; (3) **Build vs buy** — internal builds succeed only 1/3 as often as vendor solutions (67% vendor vs ~33% internal); (4) **Centralized AI labs** vs empowered line managers — labs create bottlenecks, line managers deploy faster.
**Evidence level:** Level 3 (academic, 300+ deployments analyzed)
**Key claims:**
- 95% of enterprise AI pilots stall — specific to GenAI, not all AI
- Over 50% of GenAI budgets go to sales/marketing despite ROI being in back-office
- Vendor solutions succeed 2x vs internal builds
- Centralized AI labs are a failure pattern; empowered line managers succeed

### Finding 3: Quintero/Every — "Clarity Problem" as Root Cause
**Source:** [https://every.to/p/i-talked-to-more-than-100-companies-about-ai-here-s-what-s-actually-working](https://every.to/p/i-talked-to-more-than-100-companies-about-ai-here-s-what-s-actually-working) — [practitioner direct]
**Date:** December 2025 (updated January 2026)
**Agent level:** Personal → Team
**What:** From 100+ company engagements: three failure personas — (1) **Skeptics** (initial excitement fades), (2) **Overwhelmed** (no bandwidth to experiment), (3) **Tool-jumpers** (analysis paralysis, constantly switching platforms). Root cause: "They have a clarity problem. They lack a view on what they're trying to achieve, let alone how AI might help." Key insight: documentation culture enables automation — "financial institutions and engineering teams have a natural advantage" because they document processes. AI doesn't spread automatically because workflows are individually contextual.
**Evidence level:** Level 2-3 (100+ companies, but specifics behind paywall)
**Key claims:**
- Three failure personas: skeptics, overwhelmed, tool-jumpers
- Root cause is clarity, not technology
- Documentation culture is a prerequisite for AI-enabled automation
- AI doesn't spread automatically across organizations like traditional software

### Finding 4: Shadow AI / Secret Cyborgs — ~50% Unsanctioned
**Source:** [https://www.cio.com/article/4124760/roughly-half-of-employees-are-using-unsanctioned-ai-tools-and-enterprise-leaders-are-major-culprits.html](https://www.cio.com/article/4124760/roughly-half-of-employees-are-using-unsanctioned-ai-tools-and-enterprise-leaders-are-major-culprits.html) — [domain trade publication]
**Date:** Q1 2026
**Agent level:** Personal
**What:** Approximately 50% of employees use unsanctioned AI tools. Enterprise leaders are "major culprits." Connects to Mollick's observation that workers hide AI use. Organizational response is typically governance/prohibition rather than enablement.
**Evidence level:** Level 2-3 (survey-based, multiple surveys converging on similar numbers)
**Key claims:**
- ~50% of employees use unsanctioned AI tools
- Enterprise leaders are among biggest users of shadow AI
- Organizational response is typically prohibitive rather than enabling
