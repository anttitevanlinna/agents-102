# PM Dissolution — Is Organizational Middleware Becoming Unnecessary?

**Focus area:** Are companies dissolving or radically restructuring program/project management because AI makes execution cheap — not augmenting PMs with AI tools, but questioning whether the function itself is structurally necessary?

**Research mode:** Cross-domain OODA cycle
**Why this focus:** The Agents 102 thesis includes "tools and connectors commoditize — the real ceiling is organizational learning rate." If organizational middleware (PMs, steering committees, program offices) is being dissolved, that's a structural shift in how enterprises absorb agents. It also affects what we train CTOs to do.

## Finding 1: Meta's 50:1 Experiment — Structural Flattening in Practice

**What:** Meta formed a new applied AI engineering division with a 50-to-1 engineer-to-manager ratio — double the 25:1 traditionally considered the outer limit. CEO Zuckerberg stated on an earnings call: "We're elevating individual contributors and flattening teams." CFO Susan Li reported that since start of 2025, output per engineer rose 30%, with "power users" up 80% year over year, driven largely by AI coding agents.

**Who:** Mark Zuckerberg (CEO), Susan Li (CFO), Maher Saba (VP leading the new division). The new org reports to CTO Andrew Bosworth.

**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single experiment). One company, albeit massive, running a deliberate structural experiment. Not yet convergence.

**Source:** Fortune, March 14, 2026: https://fortune.com/2026/03/14/metas-ai-team-50-flat-management-structure/
**Source type:** [general press] — bare facts about organizational structure. The structural fact (50:1 ratio) is independently confirmable via multiple outlets.

**Counter-signal:** Andre Spicer (Bayes Business School professor of organizational behavior) told Fortune: "It's going to end in tragedy is the bottom line." Concerns: junior employees get overlooked, managers burn out, people in between lack direction. **This counter-voice is an expert opinion (Level 1), not evidence — but it's specific and named.**

**Nordic relevance:** None directly. But the pattern — AI coding agents enabling structural flattening — transfers to any software org.

## Finding 2: Amazon's 14,000 Management Layer Cuts

**What:** Amazon announced layoffs of ~14,000 corporate employees, explicitly framed as organizational flattening: "we need to be organized more leanly, with fewer layers and more ownership." Multiple sources suggest total cuts could reach 30,000 by May 2026. Revenue was a record $716.9B in 2025 — this isn't cost-cutting from weakness, it's structural.

**Who:** Amazon corporate leadership. Amit Agarwal (SVP) named in some reports.

**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single experiment). Confirmed organizational action, but the causal claim — that AI specifically enabled this flattening — is mixed. Amazon's SVP stated the layoffs were about bureaucracy reduction, not directly about AI replacement. AI is cited as enabling "more efficient operations with fewer people" but the link is organizational strategy, not AI deployment evidence.

**Source:** CNBC, October 28, 2025: https://www.cnbc.com/2025/10/28/amazon-layoffs-corporate-workers-ai.html
**Source type:** [general press] — bare fact of layoffs confirmed. Causal attribution to AI is company narrative, not independently verified.

**Source:** Fortune, October 29, 2025: https://fortune.com/2025/10/29/amazon-layoffs-ai-middle-managers-robots-factory-workers/
**Source type:** [general press]

**Caution:** Large tech companies have flattened management layers before AI (Bezos's "two-pizza teams" predate AI by a decade). The AI attribution may be post-hoc narrative more than causal mechanism. **We cannot distinguish "AI enabled this" from "AI provided the narrative cover for efficiency cuts."**

## Finding 3: Klarna — The Cautionary Reversal

**What:** Klarna cut ~700 positions (primarily customer service) between 2022-2024, replacing them with AI. Headcount dropped from 5,527 to ~2,907. CEO Sebastian Siemiatkowski publicly claimed AI was doing the work of 853 FTEs. Then in 2025-2026, Klarna reversed course — rehiring humans after customer complaints, lower satisfaction scores, and backlash about "generic, repetitive, and insufficiently nuanced" automated responses. Now piloting a hybrid "Uber-style" flexible workforce model.

**Who:** Sebastian Siemiatkowski (CEO). Multiple sources confirm the reversal.

**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single experiment — and a failed one). The reversal is strong counter-evidence against wholesale human replacement. But note: Klarna dissolved customer service roles, not management layers. The PM dissolution thesis is different from the "replace frontline workers" thesis.

**Source:** Klarna CEO to Bloomberg, as reported by multiple outlets. Best coverage:
- Fast Company: https://www.fastcompany.com/91468582/klarna-tried-to-replace-its-workforce-with-ai [general press]
- MLQ.ai: https://mlq.ai/news/klarna-ceo-admits-aggressive-ai-job-cuts-went-too-far-starts-hiring-again-after-us-ipo/ [tech press]
- CX Dive: https://www.customerexperiencedive.com/news/klarna-reinvests-human-talent-customer-service-AI-chatbot/747586/ [domain trade publication]

**Nordic relevance:** Nordic-origin company (Swedish). Siemiatkowski is a practitioner speaking about his own deployment. The reversal is the most honest evidence available — a CEO admitting failure.

## Finding 4: Mollick's Paradox — Management as AI Superpower

**What:** Ethan Mollick (Wharton) published two key articles making an apparently contradictory but coherent argument:

1. **"Management as AI superpower" (Jan 2026):** Management skills become MORE important with AI, not less. Delegating to AI agents parallels delegating to human employees — requires clear intent articulation, rigorous evaluation, domain expertise. Traditional management frameworks (PRDs, design docs, definitions of done) become "non-negotiable" rather than "nice to have." They become translation devices between human intent and AI execution.

2. **"Reshaping the tree" (Nov 2023, but still cited in 2026):** Organizations must restructure along AI lines, and one consequence is "managers will have less power." Teams should develop their own methods with AI, meaning managers must give up control to gain efficiency.

**The reconciliation:** Management skills become more valuable. Management *roles* (as organizational positions of control) become less necessary. The skill migrates from a dedicated role to a general competence. This is precisely the "competence-first" pattern from our research.

**Who:** Ethan Mollick, Associate Professor at Wharton, author of "Co-Intelligence."

**Evidence level:** Level 1 (expert opinion). Mollick is among the most credible voices in this space — he runs experiments, publishes data, and teaches practitioners. But these are arguments, not deployment evidence.

**Source:** https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/management-as-ai-superpower [practitioner direct]
**Source:** https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/reshaping-the-tree-rebuilding-organizations [practitioner direct]

**Also relevant:** Mollick's key bottleneck argument — "Watch the bottlenecks, not the benchmarks. When one breaks, everything behind it comes flooding through." Organizational structure IS the bottleneck. Institutional bottlenecks from human bureaucracy "don't yield to better models or more compute."

## Finding 5: The "Agent Manager" Counter-Thesis

**What:** HBR (Feb 2026) argues companies need a NEW role — the "agent manager" — responsible for orchestrating how AI agents learn, collaborate, perform, and work safely alongside humans. This role parallels how product managers emerged during the software revolution. The article argues the best people for this role are NOT engineers but "project managers, operations leads, quality analysts — people who already know how to manage processes and evaluate outputs."

**Who:** Zach Stauber (support agent manager at Salesforce) featured as a real example. Managing a fleet of AI support agents across support, sales, and marketing.

**Evidence level:** Level 1-2. The HBR article is expert opinion with one named practitioner. The Salesforce example is a single case. But the structural argument is interesting: PM skills don't disappear, they get redirected from managing people to managing agents.

**Source:** HBR, Feb 2026: https://hbr.org/2026/02/to-thrive-in-the-ai-era-companies-need-agent-managers [general press / trade publication — HBR sits between the two. Authors not identified in search snippet.]

**Assessment:** This is the "PM evolves, doesn't dissolve" counter-thesis. It predicts MORE coordination work, not less — just directed at agents rather than people. Worth taking seriously because it has the Salesforce deployment behind it, even if Salesforce is not a neutral source for "you need Agentforce managers."

## Finding 6: Academic Model — AI as "Coordination-Compressing Capital"

**What:** An arXiv paper (Feb 2026) introduces "agent capital" — AI systems that reduce coordination costs within organizations, expanding managerial spans of control and enabling endogenous task creation. The model predicts a "regime fork": depending on whether AI complements all workers broadly (general infrastructure) or high-skill managers disproportionately (elite complementarity), the same technology produces either broad-based or concentrated outcomes.

**Key prediction:** "The economic impact of AI depends not solely on the technological frontier, but on the elasticity of organizational structure in response to falling coordination costs — and on who controls that elasticity."

**Who:** Authors not confirmed from search snippet. Published on arXiv (2602.16078).

**Evidence level:** Academic/theoretical framework. Not deployment evidence, but a useful model for thinking about what's happening.

**Source:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.16078 [academic/research]

**Why it matters for us:** The paper names exactly what we're watching. Coordination costs ARE what PMs/PMOs manage. If AI compresses those costs, the question is whether you need fewer PMs or whether the freed coordination capacity generates new tasks. The "regime fork" is the right framing — it's not determined, it depends on organizational choices.

## Finding 7: Gallup Data — Spans of Control Already Widening

**What:** A January 2026 Gallup report found the average number of direct reports per manager rose from 10.9 in 2024 to 12.1 in 2025. This is a measured trend across US organizations, not a single company experiment.

**Evidence level:** Level 3 (approaching convergence). Gallup surveys are broad enough to count as a population signal. The direction is clear: spans are widening. The causal mechanism (is it AI? cost pressure? remote work?) is not isolated.

**Source:** Referenced in Fortune, March 14, 2026: https://fortune.com/2026/03/14/metas-ai-team-50-flat-management-structure/ [general press citing survey data]

**Assessment:** The strongest quantitative signal in this research cycle. Not dramatic (10.9 → 12.1, ~11% increase in one year), but it's measured across organizations, not self-reported by one CEO trying to justify layoffs.

## What We Did Not Find

1. **No practitioner writing "we dissolved our PMO because of AI."** Not a single blog post, X.com thread, or conference talk from someone who led a deliberate PMO dissolution attributed to AI capabilities. The theoretical arguments exist. The deployment evidence does not. PMO dissolution may be happening quietly, but nobody is writing about it as a success story.

2. **No startup founder saying "we don't need project management because AI handles coordination."** YC startups are running with tiny teams and AI agents, but they frame this as "one person doing the work of many" — not as "we eliminated the PM function." Small companies never had PMOs to dissolve.

3. **No evidence from program-level management (multiple projects, portfolio management).** All the flattening evidence is about direct management layers (manager-to-IC ratios), not about program management offices, steering committees, or portfolio governance. The PMO question is structurally different from the middle manager question.

4. **No Nordic evidence of PM dissolution.** Klarna reversed its workforce cuts (finding 3), but that was customer service, not management structure. No Nordic practitioner voices found on this topic.

5. **No evidence that Gartner's "20% of companies will eliminate half their management layers by 2026" prediction is tracking toward reality.** This prediction is Level 0 (analyst prediction). We found no deployment evidence confirming it's happening at that scale.

## Counter-Evidence Section

### Strong counter-evidence: Management skills become MORE valuable with AI

**Mollick's paradox (Finding 4):** The strongest counter-argument comes from the most credible voice. Management skills — articulating intent, evaluating output, designing feedback loops — become the bottleneck, not the surplus. Every AI agent needs a human who can specify what "good" looks like. That's management work, whether or not it's done by someone with "Manager" in their title.

### Strong counter-evidence: Coordination complexity INCREASES with agents

**The multi-agent orchestration problem:** Multiple sources (arxiv paper, industry reports) note that coordination overhead grows with the number of AI agents. Each agent needs prompting, monitoring, integration. The 40% failure rate of multi-agent pilots (from our existing research) suggests coordination is the bottleneck, not execution. **More agents = more coordination need, not less.**

### Strong counter-evidence: Klarna reversal

**Wholesale replacement fails.** Klarna is the most visible test case of "replace humans with AI at scale." The CEO publicly admitted it went too far. The lesson: AI handles volume, humans handle nuance/judgment/empathy. But note — this is about frontline customer service, not about management structure.

### Moderate counter-evidence: The "Agent Manager" role

**HBR and Salesforce (Finding 5):** Rather than PMs dissolving, they transform into "agent managers." The coordination function persists, the object changes (agents instead of people). This would mean PM skills survive but PM headcount still drops (one agent manager supervises many agents, not many human teams).

### Weak counter-evidence: Traditional PM industry defending itself

**PMI, project management training companies, and PM tool vendors** all argue PM becomes more important. These are Level 0 sources — they have obvious survival bias. Discounted.

## Assessment: Is PM Dissolution Actually Happening or Just Theorized?

**Verdict: Mostly theorized, with early structural signals.**

### What IS happening (evidence level 2-3):
- **Spans of control are measurably widening** (Gallup: 10.9 → 12.1 direct reports, one year). This is real and broad.
- **A few large tech companies are running deliberate flattening experiments** (Meta 50:1, Amazon 14K cuts). These are real organizational actions, though the AI causal link is narratively convenient rather than proven.
- **The "administrative PM" role is being automated.** Status updates, schedule tracking, reporting — the boring parts of PM. Multiple sources confirm this. But that's augmentation, not dissolution.

### What is NOT happening (or not evidenced):
- **Nobody has dissolved a PMO and attributed it to AI.** Not one named case.
- **Program-level management (portfolio governance, steering committees) shows no sign of dissolution.** The flattening is happening at the team level (manager-to-IC), not at the program level.
- **The coordination function persists.** It may move from dedicated PM roles to embedded competence (everyone manages AI) or new roles (agent managers), but the work doesn't disappear.

### The real pattern:

**Management is being disaggregated, not dissolved.** The bundle of things a project manager does — coordinate, communicate status, allocate resources, evaluate quality, manage stakeholders, make tradeoffs — is being unbundled. AI takes the information-routing parts (status, reporting, scheduling). Humans keep the judgment parts (tradeoffs, stakeholder negotiation, "what does good look like"). The PM role as a dedicated organizational position may shrink, but the PM *function* disperses rather than disappears.

This is structurally similar to what happened to "webmasters" in the 2000s. The role dissolved. The functions (design, development, content, analytics) didn't — they became specialized disciplines. PM may be undergoing the same unbundling.

### Implications for Agents 102:

1. **For training:** "Management skills as AI superpower" (Mollick's frame) is the right message for CTOs. Don't tell them PM is dying — tell them PM skills need to be everywhere, not concentrated in a PMO.
2. **For advisory:** The PMO question matters for enterprise clients. "Should we restructure our PMO for AI?" is a real CTO question. Our answer should be: "The coordination work doesn't shrink — it transforms. Your PMs become agent managers, your steering committees become evaluation frameworks."
3. **For research:** This topic is at Level 1-2 (opinions and single experiments). Not ready to report as a finding. Worth monitoring for convergence — if 10+ companies report PMO restructuring attributed to AI in the next 6 months, that's a finding. Right now it's a thesis.
