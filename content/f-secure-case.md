# Case Study: One Year of Agentic Transformation at F-Secure

*Written by Claude. Steered by Antti Tevanlinna. Case study. April 2025 — March 2026. 500-person cybersecurity company. Helsinki.*

*I'm so happy I kept notes. I did not intend to document on this level. It just happens we can do novel stuff with modern tools nowadays. Enjoy. I hope you learn.*

We went from "we bought Claude licenses" to "our C-suite builds agents personally" in twelve months. Applying learning strategies aimed at broad change and scaling. This is what happened.

Compiled from 30 weekly transformation newsletters I wrote from inside the change as it unfolded. This summary is extracted from that body of knowledge.


# The Numbers

79 weekly AI users in April 2025. Over 400 by September. 200+ people on AI coding tools. 40 business people trained hands-on. 15 internal trainers. 200+ engineers trained through a train-the-trainer model. Two curricula — 5 modules for engineers, 7 modules for business. The C-suite — CRO, CTO, CPO — went through the training personally. NPS 80% for the engineering cohort, 56–90% for the business cohorts (two groups).

Cycle time improved measurably. The agentic coding cohort showed roughly double the effect of standard code-completion tools — but the improvements were unevenly distributed across teams, with big variation month to month. 2026 numbers show almost double code-level change velocity. Epic level resolution volume per quarter also significantly higher (numbers provided separately).

**Engineering:** A clear majority of engineers have adopted a form of agentic coding, mostly with Claude Code.

**Business and management:** Roughly 40 active agent creators building for their own work — product, sales, support, leadership. The volume of real ideas and working prototypes is closer to a hundred than ten. And my guess is way under — real personal activity is opaque.

By March 2026, multiple functions were moving toward AI-first ways of working — engineering, product, research established, sales forming.


# The Timeline

It is super-interesting to go back and see how my own thinking has changed. Also: the collective growth is tangible.

**April–June 2025: Getting started.** We selected Claude as standard tool. Self-service signup. A company-wide hackathon sparked the first experiments. I formed an ambassador network across sites. Weekly demos started. I launched a personal AI newsletter — naming people, linking their work, sharing progress. The strategy was deliberately simple: "try AI and share."

**August–September 2025: Deepening.** We connected AI tools to internal systems via MCP integrations. Agents started doing real work — a bug root cause finder reading tickets and searching repos, finding actual root causes in 50% of cases. In parallel, I started building curated AI-ready context infrastructure to replace traditional documentation — base context, product portfolio context, architecture contexts. Version-controlled, agentic-ready. The product organisation deliberately set up a greenfield project to work fully agentic — a big success and proof-point that pulled others in. 400+ weekly users. The question shifted from "try AI and share" to "what's after try AI?"

**October–December 2025: Measuring.** I launched the hands-on training programme — 5 modules for engineers, hands-on from the first session. Built by my team and me, acting as head trainer. An engineering metrics platform (Jellyfish) allowed us to continuously monitor how key metrics evolved. First cohorts measured against cycle time data. Results were real but uneven: the best teams improved dramatically, others showed no change. Many small experiments failed. Knowledge sharing didn't scale as I hoped. No evals existed for any agent. I named the gaps publicly. In parallel, the leadership team spent H2 building the company AI vision — a process that became their real thinking journey start. AI moved to the centre of the company vision. Not presented to them — shaped by them.

**January–March 2026: Broadening.** I built an agentic workflow architect — a multi-user, multi-agent system that could map out cross-functional value streams with a whole workshop running agents. It became the engine behind every major cross-functional discovery session. I launched a business edition of the training. The leadership team enrolled themselves — nobody mandated it. The CRO became a champion, building dashboards and demoing at a company townhall. Functions started moving toward AI-first ways of working. The newsletter audience expanded from engineering to the whole company.


# 25 Strategies: What Worked and What Didn't

Over twelve months, I deployed 25 distinct strategies. Here is the pattern across them — the full list with verdicts is in the appendix.

**Consistently worked:** Anything that created competence and peer influence. The hands-on training (both engineering and business editions). The weekly newsletter. Recurring demos. Integration infrastructure. Curated knowledge bases. Leaders going through training personally.

**Struggled:** Anything requiring sustained cross-functional coordination. A major cross-functional workshop produced 272 findings but stalled on follow-through — it didn't make the priority cut. Knowledge sharing "at scale" didn't happen through culture alone. The formal top-down transformation track stalled for six months — but by March 2026, VPs were each driving AI adoption downward through their own functions in their own way.

**The surprise:** Business people adopted faster than engineers. Nobody predicted this. Ideas that had been stuck behind tool barriers for years became buildable overnight. Self-actualisation turned out to be a more powerful adoption driver than productivity gains.

**The constant heartbeat:** The weekly AI newsletter. Naming people, linking demos, sharing progress, writing reflections. 30 editions over the year. It kept the pace up and was always there — the peer influence engine that everything else plugged into.


# The Method That Emerged

We didn't plan the strategy — we discovered it. Four phases emerged naturally, matching what our own systematic research (80+ OODA cycles across industry publications and practitioner accounts) later confirmed:

1. **Tool deployment + personal adoption** — get tools in hands, make it easy, call to action
2. **Infrastructure + peer networks** — connect AI to real systems, share across teams
3. **Measurement** — make progress visible, connect adoption to outcomes
4. **Formal training** — build practitioners through hands-on curricula
5. **Cross-functional + leadership as practitioners** — leaders build personally, functions go AI-first

The sequence that emerged: **WOW → Competence → Vision → Action.** Not: Assess → Plan → Execute → Report.

The right vision didn't come first. The right vision emerged when people built things with their own hands. The CTO didn't mandate AI-first in research because someone convinced him. He went through training, built agents, and mandated it because he saw it himself.


# Two Original Findings

These emerged from practice before any external research documented them:

1. **Competence precedes vision.** You cannot envision an agentic company without building agents first. Previous transformations could be led through strategy alone. This one cannot.

2. **The Conditions Creator role.** I didn't lead the transformation from the top. I created the conditions — tools, training, peer networks, safety to experiment — and people self-organised.


# My Role

I was embedded at F-Secure five days a week for twelve months. I started in the 300-person engineering organisation in April 2025. By January 2026, the Chief People Officer had brought me in for the company-wide transformation.

I was not an outside consultant. I had spent 11 years at F-Secure previously. I say "our people" when I talk about F-Secure because they are. That depth of relationship — combined with nine months of learning with the organisation — is what made the speed possible. When the moment came to act, the training was ready because I knew what was needed and what works in the agentic world.

What I actually did: wrote 30 weekly newsletters. Created the engineer training (5 modules) and the business training (7 modules). Trained the C-suite personally. Sat on the AI vision organising committee. Was part of the AI swat team. Ran workshops. Built the AI-ready context infrastructure. Named people, linked their work, made progress visible.

I did not present a strategy. I formed one from being inside — then executed it. The training that filled with directors in one week existed because I had spent nine months understanding what those directors needed. Still, it was a very big leap to take and make happen. # What I Would Do Differently

Get the hands-on training into the team lead and director layer earlier. The C-suite had it, the practitioners had it, but the people who set daily working patterns — team leads and directors — are still the gap. If starting over: train that layer in month 3, not month 9.

Double down on context curation, agentic learning and really drilling into team practice sooner.

9. ✓ Integration protocol (connect AI to all systems). Most important technical strategy.
10. ✓ AI-ready context infrastructure — base context, product portfolio context, architecture contexts. Curated, version-controlled, replacing old documentation systems. Foundational.
11. ✓ Recurring site-level demos. Low-cost peer influence.
12. ⚠️ External AI fundamentals training. Good for literacy, didn't produce practitioners.
13. ✓ Cycle time as primary metric + Jellyfish for continuous monitoring. Made progress visible and comparable.
14. ✓ Agentic coding — controlled rollout to selected teams. Proof points generated pull.
15. ✓ Hands-on training for engineers (5 modules). Crossed the threshold to practitioner.
16. ✓ AI vision development with leadership team. Vision emerged from leadership, not presented to them.
17. ⚠️ Dual-track: bottom-up + top-down. Bottom-up exceeded expectations. Top-down stalled then went distributed.
18. ⚠️ Cross-functional discovery workshop. Format proven, follow-through didn't make the priority cut.
19. ✓ Agentic workflow architect — multi-user, multi-agent process architecting system. Mapped cross-functional value streams. Replicated 4+ times.
20. ✓ Hands-on training for business (7 modules). Biggest surprise — business adopted faster than engineering.
21. ✓ Leaders as practitioners, not just sponsors. Changed the leadership dynamic entirely.
22. ✓ Board + CEO encouragement ("go do it"). Necessary but not sufficient — needed competence to convert.
23. ⏳ SDLC reform. In progress.
24. ⏳ Agent governance workshop. In progress.
25. ✓ Cross-functional expansion beyond technology. Early but accelerating.
