# OODA Cycle: Non-Silicon-Valley Pathways to AI-Era Culture Change

**Date:** 2026-04-15
**Cycle type:** Thematic deep-dive
**Research question:** What pathways exist for organizational AI culture change outside the SV playbook (founder memos, "everyone codes or leaves," builder-hero culture)? European companies, Nordic companies, non-tech companies, works councils.

---

## OBSERVE

### The SV Playbook Is Well-Documented but Culturally Specific

The Silicon Valley model -- Shopify's "prove you can't do it with AI first" memo, Klarna's 40% headcount reduction, founder-led mandates -- gets all the press. But most of the world's companies don't work this way. They have unions, works councils, consensus cultures, non-engineering leadership, and deeply embedded values that make the SV playbook either impossible or counterproductive.

**The gap this cycle investigates:** Practitioner-level accounts of AI culture change from *inside* non-SV organizations are structurally hard to find. The question is whether this absence means they don't exist, or whether these organizations simply don't perform their transformation publicly.

---

## ORIENT

### Six Angles Investigated

1. European companies doing AI culture change (especially Germany, Nordics, UK)
2. Works councils and co-determination meeting AI
3. HR-led vs. engineering-led AI transformation
4. Non-tech companies doing culture change for AI
5. Consensus culture + AI adoption
6. "Substance without swagger" -- quiet adoption vs. loud adoption

## FINDINGS

### 1. IKEA/Ingka: The Flagship European Counter-Model

**Evidence level: Level 2 (single experiment, well-documented)**

IKEA (via Ingka Group, operating in 32 countries) is running the most visible European alternative to the SV playbook. Key characteristics:

- **Goal:** Train 70,000 co-workers in AI literacy by end of 2026. Currently developing materials for 160,000+ workers across 31 countries.
- **Culture-first framing:** Chief People & Culture Officer Ulrika Biesert: "People have been at the heart of IKEA for over 80 years -- and that's exactly where they'll stay." Core values (togetherness, simplicity, care) are treated as *practical decision criteria* for every AI initiative.
- **Digital Ethics Group Rule:** Any AI partner or tool must be "robust, auditable, interpretable, fair, inclusive, and sustainable."
- **Training approach:** Foundational course "Say Hej to AI" (30 minutes), scaling to specialized courses (Responsible AI, Mastering Gen AI, Algorithmic Ethics). Over 4,000 co-workers already engaged.
- **External validation:** Featured in European Commission's Repository of AI Literacy Practices as a leading European example.
- **No layoff narrative.** The framing is empowerment, not replacement.

**What makes it non-SV:** No founder memo. No "code or leave." Values-led, consensus-driven, HR/People-function led. Change is negotiated, not mandated. Speed is sacrificed for durability and inclusion.

### 2. Deutsche Telekom: Co-Determination as AI Governance Framework

**Evidence level: Level 2 (single experiment, academically documented)**

Deutsche Telekom is the most documented case of works council co-determination being applied to AI governance. The group works council and management have co-developed three instruments since 2016:

- **AI Manifesto:** Principles aligned with EU AI Act and German national AI strategy. Employees must be informed when an AI system is used. AI will not be used for employee surveillance. Personnel decisions remain in human hands.
- **Digital Roadmap:** Joint management-works council plan for digital transformation.
- **Agile IT Company Agreements (Betriebsvereinbarungen):** New forms of flexible agreements that give works councils a veto right over AI deployment that affects workers.

By late 2024, approximately 90,000 employees had acquired AI-relevant skills through training -- essentially the majority of the global workforce.

The approach is described as "quiet" -- bottom-up support combined with guardrails, not top-down mandate.

**Sources:**
- Frontiers in AI / PMC, "Human-centered AI through employee participation" (2024) [academic/research -- peer-reviewed, uses DT as primary case study]
  https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10941644/
- Medium (johnreesphotos), "The Quiet AI Revolution at Deutsche Telekom: A Story of Bots, Culture, and Change," 2025-03-22 [practitioner analysis]
  https://medium.com/@johnreesphotos/the-quiet-ai-revolution-at-deutsche-telekom-a-story-of-bots-culture-and-change-7c20d55e2ca2
- Allianz Trade, "Gen AI ready in the workplace" [practitioner direct -- describes a similar model at Allianz]
  https://www.allianz-trade.com/en_global/news-insights/expert-voices/gen-ai-empowering-teams.html

**Nordic relevance:** Not Nordic, but the German co-determination model is the closest structural analog to Nordic labor relations. Transferable pattern for any company with strong employee representation.

### 3. German Works Councils and AI: Legal Framework Established, Practitioner Cases Emerging

**Evidence level: Level 2-3 (multiple cases emerging, academic documentation)**

The German Works Constitution Act was extended in 2021 to give works councils:
- Consultation rights on AI-based tools (Section 80(3))
- Information rights on AI in work process planning (Section 90)
- Co-determination rights on selection guidelines when AI is involved in hiring/transfers/terminations (Section 95(2a))
- Right to fund an external expert on proposed AI changes

A 2026 academic study (Doellgast, Kampf, Langes) compares works council initiatives across **three German ICT companies**:
- Works councils successfully strengthened worker voice in decisions about **management-automating AI** (performance monitoring, workforce analytics)
- They faced **more difficulty** influencing **work-automating AI** (skill development, employment restructuring)
- Institutional and discursive power resources explain both successes and varying outcomes

Key tension: The Hamburg Labour Court ruled (2024-01-16) that employers do NOT need works council consent for employees to voluntarily use ChatGPT with private accounts. This creates a gray zone between mandated AI tools (co-determination applies) and voluntary AI use (it doesn't).

**Sources:**
- Doellgast, Kampf, Langes, "Building Worker Voice and Power in AI Decisions: Three Cases in the German ICT Industry," Work and Occupations (2026) [academic/research]
  https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/07308884251412886
- Bird & Bird, "First Judgement on the Rights of Works Councils when Employees use AI Systems" (2024) [domain trade publication -- legal analysis]
  https://www.twobirds.com/en/insights/2024/germany/erstes-urteil-zu-rechten-des-betriebsrats-bei-einsatz-von-kuenstlicher-intelligenz
- Hogan Lovells, "AI in German Employment -- Navigating the AI Act, GDPR, and National Legislation" [domain trade publication -- legal analysis]
  https://www.hoganlovells.com/en/publications/ai-in-german-employment-navigating-the-ai-act-gdpr-and-national-legislation

**Nordic relevance:** The Finnish and Swedish labor models share the co-determination principle though with different mechanisms. The German cases are the most documented and provide transferable patterns.

### 4. European Collective Bargaining on AI: Early but Accelerating

**Evidence level: Level 2 (multiple single cases, not yet convergence)**

Eurofound analysis found:
- Only **20% of European trade unions** have a collective bargaining agreement addressing AI
- **42%** are engaged in social dialogue or negotiations on AI
- Spain has the most company-level AI clauses (El Norte de Castilla, Air Nostrum, Acciona Mobility, AXA)
- Ireland's Public Service Agreement 2024-2026 includes AI cooperation provisions
- A two-year **iMET project** (April 2024 - March 2026) is enabling innovative social dialogue on AI in the metal/engineering/technology sector

**Critical regulatory trigger:** The EU AI Act (Article 26(7)) requires employers to inform and consult employee representative bodies **prior to deploying high-risk AI systems**, effective August 2026. This will force negotiation in every European company with employee representation.

### 5. Klarna: The SV Playbook Applied in Sweden (and Its Backlash)

**Evidence level: Level 2 (single experiment, well-documented including reversal)**

Klarna is the **counter-example** -- a Swedish company that adopted the SV playbook and ran into the structural limits of doing so in a Nordic labor context.

- Workforce cut from 5,527 (end 2022) to ~3,000 (2025), targeting 2,000. CEO Siemiatkowski publicly attributed 40% reduction to AI.
- AI chatbot handling work previously done by ~700-853 customer service agents
- **Glassdoor rating dropped** from 3.8 (2022) to 3.0
- **Customer service quality dipped**, forcing CEO to publicly admit "cost unfortunately seems to have been a too predominant evaluation factor"
- **Partial reversal:** Klarna began hiring human agents again, but as gig-style on-demand workers without permanent contracts
- Swedish labor organizations **voiced concern** over use of technology to replace workers and "loose employment models"
- Union response was criticism, not blockade -- illustrating the Swedish model's limits (high trust + high flexibility means companies CAN do this, but face reputational cost)

**What it shows:** The SV playbook is *technically possible* in the Nordics, but it carries higher social and reputational costs than in the US. Klarna's partial reversal is evidence of those costs materializing.

**Sources:**
- CNBC, "Klarna CEO says AI helped company shrink workforce by 40%," 2025-05-14 [general press -- bare fact]

### 6. Spotify: HR-Led AI Adoption in a Nordic Tech Company

**Evidence level: Level 2 (single experiment, early stage)**

Spotify's new CHRO Anna Lundstrom (appointed 2025) set three priorities: AI-readiness, culture, and employee well-being. Key practices:

- **AI rules** released to entire organization (co-authored with Chief Product Officer Gustav Soderstrom)
- **Training range:** From prompt engineering to advanced courses, available to all employees
- **AI Momentum Program:** Employees and managers share, on an ongoing basis, how they're using AI. Leadership tracks adoption levels across the company.
- **"Band jams":** Regular digital workshops where managers and teams discuss company values
- **Modern Health by Heart & Soul:** Personalized well-being platform for employees in 40+ countries

**What makes it non-SV:** Despite being a tech company, Spotify chose the HR-led, values-embedded path rather than the engineering-mandate path. AI adoption is framed as learning and empowerment, not performance requirement.

### 7. Zapier: HR-Led AI Transformation (US, but HR-Led Model)

**Evidence level: Level 2 (single experiment, structured and public)**

### 10. The Macro Picture: Europe-US AI Adoption Gap

**Evidence level: Level 3-4 (academic convergence from multiple studies)**

Three major studies provide the macro framing:

**Brookings/CEPR/NBER "Mind the Gap" (Spring 2026):**
- 43% of US workers use AI vs. 32% average in Europe (range: 26% Italy to 36% UK)
- 7% of US firms use AI for production vs. 4% of EU firms
- **Critical finding:** When employer encouragement is included, **more than 80% of the gap** is explained. The gap is NOT about regulation, culture, or institutions -- it's about whether firms actively encourage AI use.
- AI adoption is closely linked to firm personnel management practices

**EIB Working Paper 2026/02:**
- AI adoption boosts European firm labor productivity by 4%
- Driven by capital deepening, NOT job replacement (short-term)
- Gains concentrated in medium and large firms
- **Complementary investments** in software, data, and training are essential

**BCG "Nordic AI Inflection Point" (March 2026):**
- Only **4% of Nordic firms** achieve 5x+ returns on AI investment
- 40-50% of Nordic AI spend goes to off-the-shelf productivity tools (vs. 8-11% globally)
- Companies are automating existing tasks rather than building new products/services
- BCG warns of a "local AI value bubble" if this persists

**Sources:**
- Brookings, "Mind the gap: AI adoption in Europe and the US" [academic/research]
  https://www.brookings.edu/articles/mind-the-gap-ai-adoption-in-europe-and-the-us/
- EIB, "AI adoption, productivity and employment: Evidence from European firms" [academic/research]
  https://www.eib.org/en/publications/20250383-economics-working-paper-2026-02
- Finnish AI Region, "Nordic firms pour record sums into AI -- but only 4% see strong returns" [domain trade publication, summarizes BCG study]

### 11. Nordic-Specific: Low Anxiety, High Access, Slow Redesign

**Evidence level: Level 2-3 (survey-based, fresh data)**

A survey of 170 senior executives across Denmark, Finland, Norway, and Sweden (reported April 14, 2026):

- Share of Nordic orgs where 40%+ of employees have access to approved AI tools rose from **37% to 56%** in one year
- Only **16% report extensive redesign of work** to support new ways of working
- **35% expect significant productivity gains** from AI
- **Fear of mass job losses remains low** among Nordic workers
- Companies "typically negotiate significant workplace changes rather than impose them" -- a process that slows transformation but may reduce resistance

**The Nordic paradox:** High access, high expectations, low redesign. Tools are being provided, but work isn't being restructured to use them. This is the "substance without swagger" profile -- or possibly just substance without actual transformation.

### 12. The "Quiet Rebellion" Counter-Signal

**Evidence level: Level 2 (single large survey)**

WalkMe's 2026 State of Digital Adoption report (3,750 executives and employees across 14 countries) reveals a significant backlash:

- **54% of workers** manually bypassed AI tasks in the past 30 days
- **33%** haven't used AI at all
- **75% of executives** admit their company's AI strategy is "more for show" than actual guidance
- **52-point trust gap** between executives (61% trust AI for complex decisions) and workers (9%)
- **51 working days per employee per year** lost to technology friction -- up 42% from 2025

This is NOT a European-specific finding, but it demolishes the assumption that the SV mandate model "works." If 75% of executives admit their strategy is performative, the quiet European approach may not be slower -- the loud SV approach may just be louder without being more effective.

**Sources:**
- Fortune, "White-collar workers are quietly rebelling against AI as 80% outright refuse adoption mandates," 2026-04-09 [general press -- cites WalkMe survey data and practitioner interviews]

### 14. Manager Engagement as the Real Bottleneck (Global, Relevant Everywhere)

**Evidence level: Level 3 (Gallup survey convergence)**

Gallup's February 2026 survey of 23,717 US employees:

- Employees whose manager actively supports AI use are **8.7x more likely** to say AI has transformed how work gets done
- Global employee engagement fell to **20% in 2025**
- Manager engagement dropped **9 points** (31% to 22%) between 2022-2025
- Europe's workforce engagement: only **13%**, significantly below global average of 23%

**The implication for non-SV models:** Neither the SV mandate nor the European consensus approach works if managers are disengaged. The real variable isn't top-down vs. bottom-up -- it's whether the middle layer (managers) champions or blocks the change. The 8.7x multiplier on manager support is the most actionable finding in this entire cycle.

**Sources:**
- Gallup, "Rising AI Adoption Spurs Workforce Changes" [academic/research -- Gallup's own primary survey data]
  https://www.gallup.com/workplace/704225/rising-adoption-spurs-workforce-changes.aspx

  https://www.hrdive.com/news/engagement-among-managers-is-slipping-affecting-ai-use-gallup/817069/

## WHAT WE DID NOT FIND

This section is as important as the findings. The absences are themselves signals.

### 1. No Nordic practitioner writing from inside their org about AI culture change

**This is the biggest gap.** We found IKEA, Spotify, Klarna, Novo Nordisk -- but from press coverage, not from practitioners writing their own accounts. There is no Nordic equivalent of a founder memo, a "here's how we did it" blog post, or a conference talk from inside a Nordic company about changing the organization for AI.

**Possible explanations:** (a) Nordic culture doesn't perform transformation publicly -- Janteloven effect. (b) The transformation hasn't progressed far enough to write about. (c) The people doing it are too busy to write. (d) Companies treat it as competitive advantage and keep it private.

**Research implication:** This is a demand gap. If Agents 102 can surface these stories, it fills a genuine void.

### 2. No evidence that consensus culture accelerates or decelerates AI adoption

We found no academic study, practitioner account, or survey that directly tests whether Nordic/Scandinavian consensus management helps or hinders AI adoption. The theoretical case for both directions exists (consensus = slower but more durable vs. consensus = more inclusive adoption), but nobody has tested it. The Brookings finding that 80% of the gap is explained by employer encouragement suggests the bottleneck is management practice, not cultural structure -- but this hasn't been disaggregated by cultural type.

### 3. No documented case of HR-led AI transformation at a non-tech European company

Zapier (US tech) and Spotify (Nordic tech) have HR-led models. F-Secure (in our own experience) has SVP HR leading. But we found **zero documented cases** of a non-tech European company where HR/People function led the AI transformation. This may be the single most important white space.

### 4. No Finnish-specific practitioner accounts

Despite Finland having strong national AI strategy, high digital literacy, and Agentics Helsinki community, we found zero Finnish company practitioner accounts of internal AI culture change. Helsinki Times reports AI use in Finnish workplaces "still limited, mostly by managers."

### 5. Swedish union positions on AI are invisible in English

Unionen and TCO (Sweden's major white-collar unions) presumably have positions on AI in the workplace, but we found nothing in English-language sources. This may be entirely a language barrier problem. Swedish-language research could yield different results.

### 6. No practitioner evidence on whether co-determination is a brake or accelerator

The academic literature documents the legal framework (Germany) and the theory (worker participation could improve AI design). The Deutsche Telekom case suggests it can accelerate by building trust. But there is no comparative study showing co-determination companies adopting AI faster or slower than non-co-determination companies. The EIB productivity study doesn't disaggregate by labor relations model.

## DECIDE (Implications for Agents 102)

### Three Non-SV Pathways Are Emerging

| Pathway | Archetype | Culture lever | Speed | Durability |
|---------|-----------|---------------|-------|------------|
| **Values-first** | IKEA, Spotify | Core values as AI decision criteria, people-function led | Slow | Potentially high |
| **Co-determination** | Deutsche Telekom | Works council co-designs AI governance, veto rights | Very slow | High (legally anchored) |
| **Pragmatic deployment** | Novo Nordisk, Allianz | Champion networks, hands-on workshops, gradual rollout | Medium | Unknown |

### The Nordic Paradox

High tool access (56% of orgs have 40%+ staff with AI tools) + low work redesign (16%) + low job-loss anxiety + 4% achieving strong returns = **tools without transformation.** Nordic companies are providing access without restructuring work. This is the opposite of the SV model (which restructures aggressively) but may produce the same underwhelming results through a different failure mode.

### The Brookings Bombshell

If **80% of the Europe-US gap** is explained by employer encouragement, then the bottleneck is not regulation, not works councils, not consensus culture -- it's whether management actively encourages AI use. This reframes the entire question. The SV memo is one form of encouragement. The IKEA values-first approach is another. The real failure mode is indifference -- neither encouraging nor discouraging.

### The Manager Multiplier

Gallup's 8.7x multiplier on manager support is the most actionable finding. Any AI culture change model -- SV or European -- succeeds or fails at the manager layer. Europe's 13% employee engagement rate (vs. 23% global) suggests the middle-management bottleneck may be *worse* in Europe, regardless of the culture model chosen.

### What This Means for Training

The Agents 102 training program has a differentiated position: it addresses the competence layer that neither the SV mandate model nor the European consensus model provides. The SV model mandates without teaching. The European model negotiates without mandating. Neither builds competence. Competence is the prerequisite for both paths.
