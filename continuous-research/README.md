## Scope: Business Processes, Not Code

**Agentic coding is a red ocean.** Every vendor, bootcamp, and developer community is already there. Claude Code, Codex, Cursor, GitHub Copilot — the coding space is saturated with tools, tutorials, and competition.

Our research focuses on **everything else** — the 90% of a company that isn't engineering. The processes where agents are just now becoming viable but nobody is systematically tracking it:

- **Operations & supply chain** — procurement agents, logistics optimization, vendor management
- **Finance & accounting** — invoice processing, financial reporting, audit preparation, expense management
- **HR & people ops** — onboarding workflows, policy agents, recruitment screening, internal knowledge access
- **Customer service & success** — multi-step resolution agents, escalation handling, proactive outreach
- **Compliance & legal** — regulatory monitoring, contract review, policy enforcement, audit trails
- **Sales & marketing** — lead qualification, proposal generation, competitive intelligence, content workflows
- **Product & strategy** — market research synthesis, customer feedback analysis, roadmap inputs

This is where the blue ocean is. A CTO doesn't need us to tell them about coding agents — their engineers already know. A CTO needs us to tell them what's just now becoming possible for their ops team, their finance team, their HR team. That's the gap nobody is filling.

## The Adoption Curve as Research Framework

Our research model maps directly to Moore's technology adoption lifecycle (Crossing the Chasm). We're not randomly scanning the frontier — we're systematically watching how agentic practices move through the curve:

```
Innovators → Early Adopters → [CHASM] → Early Majority → Late Majority
    ↑              ↑              ↑              ↑
  Tier 1         Tier 2      We track       Our buyers
 (our sources)  (our sources) this crossing   live here
```

**We observe innovators and early adopters.** Then we watch how specific practices cross the chasm — what changes, what gets simplified, what governance wraps around it — and translate that into actionable intelligence for the early majority. That's where our buyers live. They're not innovators. They're the leaders who need to know *when* and *how* something that worked for a solo builder becomes viable for a 5,000-person organization.

The research question is never "what's cool?" It's: **"What just crossed from 'only innovators can do this' to 'your organization can do this in 6 months'?"**

This is the lens that makes the research uniquely valuable. Innovator content (X.com, GitHub) is overwhelming and untranslated. Consultancy content (McKinsey, Deloitte) arrives after the early majority has already started moving. We sit in the gap — watching the chasm crossing in real time and reporting on it before anyone else.

## Evidence Ladder

We don't do science, but we follow the same credibility process. Knowledge accumulates through levels — and our credibility depends on being transparent about which level we're reporting from.

```
Level 4: Cross-domain meta-pattern     ← Premium insight. "Across ALL domains, X holds."
Level 3: Convergence (10-20 independent)← WHERE WE REPORT. This is signal.
Level 2: Single experiment              ← Supporting evidence, clearly labeled.
Level 1: Opinion                        ← Context only. Never a finding.
Level 0: Commercial content             ← OUTSIDE THE LADDER. Not evidence at all.
```

**Three sources CTOs don't trust — and what they DO trust:**

1. **Vendors** oversell to hit quota. A SAP press release is not an experiment. A vendor case study is curated advertising. We label all vendor-sourced claims as "Vendor claim" and track the gap between announcement and deployment. That gap IS the story.

2. **Single early adopter "propeller heads"** are not trusted either. One builder on X.com saying "I built an agent that does X" is a curiosity, not evidence. Could be genius. Could be survivorship bias. CTOs don't act on individual experiments.

3. **Consultancy frameworks** (McKinsey, Deloitte, Gartner) are paid opinion dressed as methodology. Useful for understanding how the early majority thinks, not for seeing what's coming. A Gartner prediction is not a survey.

**What CTOs DO trust: convergence.** When 10-20 independent practitioners are making the same leap — different people, different companies, same outcome — that's signal. Think of agentic coding in early 2025: one person shipping with Claude was a curiosity. When 10-20 developers were independently shipping real things, CTOs started believing. The pattern made it real, not the individual claim.

**Our editorial threshold:** We report at levels 3-4. We cite level 2 as supporting evidence. We acknowledge level 1 as context. We label level 0 as commercial and never present it as a finding.

## Source Tiers

McKinsey's sources are CTOs. Our sources are the people who create what CTOs will use.

### Tier 1: Innovators (where the future lives)

- **X.com / builder community** — the real-time stream of what practitioners are shipping today. We specifically track builders working on non-coding agent workflows: operations automation, document processing, multi-step business processes, knowledge management agents. Individual signals are level 2 (single experiment). We watch for convergence across builders — when 10-20 are independently doing the same thing, that's level 3.
- **Platform and protocol creators** — Anthropic (MCP), OpenAI (Assistants API), Google (A2A), the teams building the infrastructure that lets agents connect to enterprise systems. Their protocol decisions determine which business processes agents can reach. But: platform announcements are level 0 (commercial) until practitioners confirm they work in production.
- **Agentics Foundation** — the emerging standards body. Protocol decisions made here shape what agents can do across platforms and enterprise system boundaries.
- **No-code/low-code agent builders** — n8n, Make, Zapier AI, Microsoft Copilot Studio. The tools that put agent-building in the hands of non-engineers. Their capability roadmaps are leading indicators, but roadmaps are level 0 until deployed.
- **Vertical SaaS with agentic features** — when Salesforce, ServiceNow, or SAP ships agents inside their platforms. **Critical distinction:** the vendor announcement is level 0. Customer evidence of actually using the feature is level 2. Convergence across 10-20 customers is level 3. Our Q1 2026 research found: Salesforce Agentforce = real for narrow cases (level 3), SAP Joule = vaporware (level 0 only), Workday Illuminate = zero practitioner evidence (level 0 only).

### Tier 2: Early Adopters (where edge meets enterprise)

- Enterprise deployment stories with verifiable numbers (Equinor, Gjensidige, Allianz — the ones with specific outcomes)
- Conference talks from practitioners, not vendor keynotes
- Published case studies with enough specificity to extract real patterns
- Source hierarchy: (1) Practitioner at conference > (2) Third-party investigation > (3) Customer testimonial on vendor site > (4) Vendor press release. Only levels 1-2 count as strong evidence.
- **Chasm-crossing signals:** when an early adopter wraps governance around an innovator practice, that's the signal.

### Tier 3: Background Context (the map, not the territory)

- Academic research (lags by 6-18 months, but grounds the patterns)
- Analyst reports (Gartner, BCG — level 0 commercial. Useful for market sizing, never for findings.)
- Consultancy frameworks (McKinsey, Deloitte — level 1 opinion at best. Useful for understanding how the early majority thinks.)

### The Editorial Move

**Not this:** "SAP announced Joule agents for finance." (Level 0. Advertising.)
**Not this:** "One company deployed a finance agent." (Level 2. Single experiment.)
**This:** "15 companies independently found that governance-first deploys faster. Here's the specific mechanism, and here's what it means for your organization in 6 months." (Level 3-4. Convergence and meta-pattern.)

The credibility comes from being transparent about evidence levels. When we say "this is becoming possible," a CTO can see why: not because a vendor announced it, not because one propeller head demoed it, but because 10-20 independent practitioners are doing it and reporting the same results.

## Global vs. Nordic Tagging

**All research is tagged: Global or Nordic.**

The innovator edge is overwhelmingly global — X.com builders, Anthropic, OpenAI, Agentics Foundation. The Nordic sample pool alone may not tell the story. The real breakthroughs in agentic practice are happening in San Francisco, London, Bangalore, and open-source communities that have no geography.

But our buyers are Nordic. They need to know: "This is what's happening globally. Here's what it means for a Nordic organization specifically."

**Why this matters:**
- **Innovators (Tier 1) are almost entirely global.** The builder edge doesn't respect borders. If we only tracked Nordic innovators, we'd miss 95% of the signal.
- **Early adopters (Tier 2) are a mix.** Some of the best enterprise deployment stories are Nordic (Equinor, Novo Nordisk, Scania). Others are global (JPMorgan, Klarna, Walmart). Both matter — Nordic examples have higher trust with our buyers, global examples have higher volume and variety.
- **The chasm-crossing conditions ARE Nordic-specific.** EU AI Act, Nordic labor models, works councils, GDPR interpretation, high-trust business culture, flat organizational structures — these shape how a global innovator practice becomes viable in a Nordic enterprise. A practice that crossed the chasm at a US company may still be pre-chasm in a Nordic context because the governance requirements are different.

### Tagging Discipline

Every research finding gets two tags:
1. **Origin:** Global / Nordic / Both
2. **Nordic applicability:** Direct (works as-is) / Requires adaptation (governance, regulatory, cultural) / Unknown

This creates a distinctive editorial voice: "Globally, 40% of enterprise apps will include agents by end of 2026 (Gartner) (TODO: verify critically). In the Nordics, only 19% of workers use GenAI weekly (BCG) (TODO: verify critically). The capability exists globally. The adoption gap is Nordic-specific. Here's why — and here's what the Nordic early adopters did differently."

The global-vs-Nordic lens is itself a value proposition. No global research firm provides it. No Nordic consultancy tracks the global edge closely enough. We do both.

## Why Nobody Else Is Doing This

McKinsey can talk to 500 CTOs. They can't track what's happening on X.com at the builder level, because that's not their source network and not their competence. This is the structural advantage:

- **Consultancies** (McKinsey, Deloitte) source from CTOs → backward-looking by design. They publish frameworks (strategy layer), not practice intelligence (capability layer). They describe the game; they don't see the next move.
- **Vendors** (Microsoft, Salesforce, Google) source from their own platform → ecosystem-locked. They curate their products, not the frontier. They can't tell you what's happening on a competing platform.
- **Academics** source from peer review → 6-18 months behind. Rigorous but structurally late.
- **Media** (TechCrunch, The Information) source from press releases and leaks → news, not synthesis. "Company X launched Y" without "here's what this means for your agent strategy."
- **Us:** Source from the builders themselves → forward-looking by design. We track innovators and early adopters, then translate chasm-crossing patterns for the early majority. Practitioner credibility (F-Secure, Neste, Posti, 200+ trained) means we can bridge both worlds. The adoption curve is our analytical framework; the builder community is our source network; the quiz is our distribution instrument. The combination is the moat.

### Orchestration Lessons (apply to all future runs)

1. **Quality gates:** Three admission gates per finding: (a) truly agentic? (b) independent evidence? (c) specific outcome? Classify into: Production Agentic / Platform Announcement / Context.
2. **Evidence ladder:** Label every finding with its evidence level (0-4). Only report at levels 3-4 (convergence and meta-patterns). Commercial content (vendor announcements, analyst predictions) is level 0 — outside the ladder.
3. **Sequencing:** Domain tracks in parallel, then cross-cuts after (cross-cuts synthesize across domains). Quality review step before synthesis.
4. **Deduplication:** Each company = primary finding in one domain only.
5. **"What We Did Not Find"** section in every domain file.
6. **Nordic label precision:** Nordic-origin deployment ≠ Nordic-available platform ≠ Nordic-relevant pattern.
7. **Fill the master research log** after each track completes.

## Related: Platform Watch (separate research system)

The **platform-watch** system (`platform-watch/`) tracks agent platform capabilities — what Microsoft, Google, OpenAI, and Anthropic can actually do for business users. It runs on a separate hourly OODA cycle with its own prompt (`platform-watch/cycle-prompt.md`).

**How the two systems connect:**
- **Domain research** (this system) finds practitioners doing agentic work in sales, finance, HR, etc. → Platform watch asks: on which platform? How?
- **Platform watch** finds "platform X can't do Y" → Domain research asks: so how ARE people in that function doing Y?
- **Source roster** (`source-roster.md`) needs domain-specific practitioners, not just tech/AI people. Each domain track should surface innovator practitioners per business function.

**Two personas served:**
1. **CTO planning AI transformation** — served by platform watch (which platform, what's real, what's marketing)
2. **Business user who will USE agents** — served by domain research (what's possible in my function, who's doing it, what works)

The personal→team→company agent framework (defined in `platform-watch/synthesis.md`) applies to domain research too — are the practitioners we find building personal agents, team agents, or company-wide agents?

## Related: AI-Native Teams Research (new track, March 2026)

The **ai-native-teams** track (`platform-watch/ai-native-teams/`) asks a different question from both platform watch and domain research: **"What does the target state look like?"**

Platform watch asks "which platform?" Domain research asks "which processes?" AI-native teams research asks "what does the team that's already good at this actually look like?" — their characteristics, practices, tool stacks, working style, and structure.

**Three-phase expansion:** (1) AI-native teams → (2) AI-native product programs → (3) AI-native companies.

**Platform signal:** People describe tools when describing how they work. Every tool mention feeds back into platform watch — this is a secondary source of platform intelligence from practitioners who are deep users, not evaluators.

**Seed signals:** Compound engineering (Every, Imprint, Autodesk) is one practice, but AI-native is broader than any single methodology.
