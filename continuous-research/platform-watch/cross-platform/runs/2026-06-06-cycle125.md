### Finding 2: Stanford Enterprise AI Playbook — 51 Deployments, 4-Factor Transformation Sequence

**Source:** https://digitaleconomy.stanford.edu/publication/enterprise-ai-playbook/ — [practitioner analysis]
**Date:** April 2, 2026
**Authors:** Elisa Pereira, Alvin W. Graylin, Erik Brynjolfsson (Stanford Digital Economy Lab)
**Agent level:** company
**What:** 51 live production deployments, 41 organizations, 5 months, 5 regions, 1M+ employees. Four factors consistently predicted success: (1) workflow mapping before technology selection (6–8 weeks minimum); (2) governance architecture embedded from day one, not retrofitted; (3) observability established before production launch; (4) executive sponsor continuity through first 18 months. Organizations following this sequence: 40% faster time-to-value, 35% lower transformation cost vs. vendor-driven approaches. Critical finding: 77% of the hardest implementation challenges were invisible — change management, data quality, and process redesign — not technical. 61% of successful projects had a failed prior attempt whose costs were excluded from ROI calculations. Agentic AI delivers superior results but represents only 20% of implementations.
**Evidence level:** Level 3 — 51 independent deployments, academic methodology, named authors, peer-reviewed institution
**Key claims:**
- 77% of hardest challenges are organizational (change management, data quality, process redesign), not technical
- 40% faster time-to-value with 4-factor sequence vs. vendor-first approach
- 61% of successful projects had prior failed attempt with costs invisible in ROI calculations
- Governance-from-day-one outperforms governance retrofitted post-deployment

### Finding 3: Mollick "Secret Cyborg" — Post-Training Suppression Pattern

**Source:** https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/making-ai-work-leadership-lab-and — [practitioner analysis]
**Supporting:** https://knowledge.wharton.upenn.edu/podcast/this-week-in-business/where-artificial-intelligence-stands-heading-into-2026/ — [practitioner direct]
**Date:** May 22, 2025 (framework); 2026 Wharton podcast confirms current position
**Agent level:** company
**What:** Mollick describes a structural failure mode where employees hide AI usage due to fear of job loss or punishment — "Secret Cyborgs." Organizations report modest productivity gains while workers privately claim 3x productivity; the gap is caused by incentive-driven suppression, not capability gaps. This explains why Pattern 12 (competence creates pull) can be suppressed even after training succeeds — training works at the individual level, but organizational incentives kill the pull. His transformation model: Leadership (vision + incentives) → Lab (ambidextrous R&D unit) → Crowd (employees finding practical uses). Counter to "IT should lead transformation": IBM Design (not IT) led their 400,000-person transformation.
**Evidence level:** Level 2–3 — no single named company, but pattern described across multiple organizations with consistent structure
**Key claims:**
- "Secret Cyborg" = employees hiding AI use due to fear; explains gap between private 3x claims and reported modest gains
- Organizational incentives can suppress the competence-creates-pull mechanism
- HR/Design functions, not IT, should lead AI transformation (IBM evidence)
- Better adoption metrics: "Have we built an impossible thing? Have we jettisoned something critical?" (not "touched AI")

### Finding 4: HBR "Last Mile Problem" — Named Post-Deployment Stall Pattern

**Source:** https://hbr.org/2026/03/the-last-mile-problem-slowing-ai-transformation — [practitioner analysis]
**Supporting:** https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/what-we-do/capabilities/applied-artificial-intelligence/content/state-of-ai-in-the-enterprise.html — [practitioner analysis]
**Date:** March 9, 2026
**Authors:** Karim R. Lakhani (Harvard), Jared Spataro (Microsoft), Jen Stave
**Agent level:** company
**What:** Organizations have deployed tools extensively (Copilot, ChatGPT) yet "fundamental changes to operating and business models have not materialized." The "last mile problem" is the specific failure stage AFTER tools are deployed and approved — it's not access (tools exist), not trust (approved), but final behavioral change. Deloitte corroborates: fewer than 60% of employees with approved AI tools use them regularly. Cross-validates Stanford finding that 77% of hardest challenges are organizational.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 — practitioner analysis, no named company deployment data; Deloitte figure is vendor-sourced
**Key claims:**
- "Last mile" = post-deployment behavioral change stage where transformation stalls despite tool access
- <60% of employees with approved AI tools use them regularly (Deloitte)
- The bottleneck is not model quality or data availability

**Key claims:**
- 74% of enterprises that go live with customer-facing AI agents reverse or shut down (N=2,527)
- Governance maturity paradox: 81% rollback rate among most mature governance orgs — discipline surfaces failures
- This is the largest-N enterprise AI deployment failure dataset found in this research program

## Orientation

**New Level 3 pattern (Pattern 48):** Enterprise token economics structurally fail at current pricing. Microsoft + Uber + unnamed ($500M/month) company = convergence. The better the tool, the faster enterprise budgets deplete. This is not a usage problem — it's a unit economics structural problem at current consumption-based pricing. Implication for platform watch: adoption is not constrained only by capability or trust, but by a third axis — sustained economic viability. Pricing pressure will either drive Anthropic to enterprise seat-based pricing (already happening for Team/Enterprise) or force enterprises to ration usage. Both paths reduce the "agent everywhere" vision.

**Transformation methods (Stanford + Mollick + HBR):** Three independent sources converge on the same structure: the hard work is organizational, not technical. Stanford's 4-factor sequence, Mollick's Secret Cyborg, HBR's Last Mile Problem all point to the same gap — Pattern 43 (Training > Tooling) is correct but incomplete. Training creates individual capability; it doesn't automatically create organizational transformation. The full sequence is: Training → Clear incentives (suppressing Secret Cyborgs) → Governance from day one → Last-mile behavioral change. Each step is a gate, not a given.

**Harvey LAB counter-evidence:** The $190M ARR and 100K+ lawyer user figures coexist with a 7.1% end-to-end task completion rate. This is not contradiction — it means lawyers are using Harvey for portions of tasks, not full autonomous completion. The real CTO insight: vertical AI adoption is happening despite the 7.1% ceiling because partial completion + human review still delivers value. The ceiling tells you where NOT to use agents autonomously.

**74% rollback + governance paradox:** The Sinch finding (81% rollback among most mature governance orgs) is striking counter-evidence to "governance solves deployment." Governance doesn't prevent failures — it detects them faster. Organizations with weak governance may show lower rollback rates because they never measure whether their deployment actually works. The paradox should be documented in the patterns file.
