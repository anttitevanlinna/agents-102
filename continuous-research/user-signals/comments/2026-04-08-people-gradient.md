Two-variable adoption model: agent adoption depends on (1) work structure AND (2) people structure.

Work structure gradient (L4 meta-pattern): are the rules codified, is correctness verifiable?
People structure gradient (NEW): are the practitioners in this domain logical/systematic enough to BUILD agents?

Building agents is inherently a logical activity — systematic thinking, structured prompts, evaluation criteria, feedback loops. Every domain has strong logical thinkers, but the DENSITY of systematic practitioners varies. Coding and PM have higher density. Marketing, sales, HR have lower density. This is distribution, not a claim about individuals.

The systematic thinkers in ANY domain are the seed practitioners — the Torres and Rekhi of their field. They get the WOW first, build the first agents, demonstrate to peers. The density of systematic thinkers determines adoption VELOCITY (how fast the peer cascade happens), not adoption POSSIBILITY (whether it can happen at all).

This explains:
- Claude Code PM adoption tracks to the more technical PMs (Torres, Rekhi — both have engineering fluency). They're the seeds. Other PMs follow.
- Marketing has thousands of ChatGPT users but few agent builders — lower density of systematic thinkers means fewer seeds, slower cascade
- HR has zero practitioner signals — very low seed density + unstructured work = slowest cascade
- AI SDR requires 2-3 hrs/day systematic prompt engineering — the systematic salespeople can do it, but they're rare, so it doesn't scale

Prediction matrix (density × work structure):
- High density + structured work (coding, finance) → fast autonomous adoption
- High density + unstructured work (PM strategy, management) → AI assists, doesn't automate
- Low density + structured work (marketing execution, sales ops) → agents arrive via vendor platforms or built by seeds/outsiders, slow cascade
- Low density + unstructured work (HR, stakeholder politics) → slowest adoption, needs external builders

Implication for competence-first thesis: The seed practitioner density determines where competence-building (training) has the highest ROI. Train in high-density domains first — the cascade is faster. In low-density domains, the coding-agent meta-platform matters more — someone else builds the agent, the domain expert provides judgment.
