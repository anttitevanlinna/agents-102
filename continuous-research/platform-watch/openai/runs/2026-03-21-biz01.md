### 6. ChatGPT Enterprise: Fundamental Architecture Limits for Business Agents
**Source:** https://dust.tt/blog/chatgpt-enterprise-alternatives-ai-platforms-for-business — [competitor analysis, but substantive]
**Source:** https://www.eesel.ai/blog/chatgpt-teams — [independent review]
**Date:** 2025-2026
**Agent level:** Team / Company
**What:** Critical structural limitations identified by multiple independent sources:
- **Single-player tool:** Each person gets their own chat interface. No shared agents, no team-level workflows, no compounding organizational knowledge.
- **Read-only connectors:** Google Drive, SharePoint, Notion sync is read-only. No write-back to Salesforce, Zendesk, Snowflake, Jira.
- **EKM kills connectors:** Enabling Enterprise Key Management (for compliance) disables all synced connectors, forcing manual uploads.
- **No workflow orchestration:** Cannot build multi-step workflows, trigger automations, or chain actions across systems.
- **Single-vendor model lock-in:** Only OpenAI models available. Cannot use Claude for specific tasks or route to specialized models.
- **Tab-switching friction:** Lives in its own browser tab, not embedded in business tools. Employees must context-switch constantly.
**Evidence level:** Level 3 (convergence — multiple independent sources identify the same structural gaps)

### 7. Security and Enterprise Readiness: Structural Gaps
**Source:** https://www.itbrew.com/stories/2025/08/07/enterprise-pros-examine-how-chatgpt-s-agent-mode-prompts-can-go-awry — [independent journalism / practitioner quotes]
**Source:** https://brightideasagency.com/blog/are-they-ready-for-the-enterprise — [practitioner analysis]
**Date:** Aug 2025 - 2026
**Agent level:** Company
**What:** Named enterprise professionals identify structural security gaps:
- **Prompt injection via calendar/email:** Bret Kinsella (GM, Telus Digital's Fuel iX) warns that malicious commands can be hidden in calendar entries that agents access. "I don't think I've met a CISO yet who has a clear plan on how to address something like this."
- **No agent identity management:** Unlike Microsoft's Entra-based Agent IDs, ChatGPT agents have no enterprise identity framework. They can't slot into existing security/compliance tooling.
- **Atlas not SOC 2 scoped:** OpenAI's Atlas (enterprise analytics) is not in scope for SOC 2 or ISO attestations and doesn't emit compliance API logs or SIEM feeds.
- **Data accuracy in agent outputs:** "When an agent creates a slide deck, the data can be flat-out wrong" (Kinsella).
- **Sam Altman's own warning:** Called ChatGPT Agent "experimental" and cautioned against "high-stakes uses or with a lot of personal information."
**Evidence level:** Level 2-3 (multiple named practitioners converging on same assessment; no actual deployment failures documented, but structural analysis is consistent)

### 8. Pricing: Business Agent Capabilities Gated by Tier
**Source:** https://intuitionlabs.ai/articles/chatgpt-plans-comparison — [independent comparison]
**Date:** 2026
**Agent level:** All
**What:** Six-tier pricing now:
- Free ($0): Basic chat, no agent mode
- Go ($8/mo): Light usage
- Plus ($20/mo): Agent mode but only 40 agent messages/month — barely enough to test
- Pro ($200/mo): Meaningful agent usage
- Business ($25/user/mo): Team features, shared GPTs, admin controls
- Enterprise (custom): Full governance, SSO, SCIM, unlimited GPT-5.2
The agent capability gap between Plus (40 messages) and Pro ($200) is a cliff. Business users who want to experiment with agent mode must either pay $200/mo personally or wait for their org to upgrade to Enterprise.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (factual pricing, independently verified across multiple sources)

### 9. Rapid Model Retirement Creates Enterprise Instability
**Source:** https://help.openai.com/en/articles/11165333-chatgpt-enterprise-and-edu-models-limits — [vendor documentation]
**Date:** Feb-Mar 2026
**Agent level:** Company
**What:** OpenAI retired GPT-4o, GPT-4.1, GPT-4.1 mini, o4-mini, and GPT-5 (Instant/Thinking) on Feb 13, 2026. Then retired GPT-5.1 models on Mar 11, 2026. Enterprise customers get a few weeks' grace period for Custom GPTs using deprecated models. This velocity of model retirement means any Custom GPT or workflow built on a specific model version can break within months. For enterprises that invested in prompt engineering and testing against a specific model, this is a real operational risk.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (factual, from vendor's own documentation)

## What I Looked For But Did Not Find

1. **No evidence of truly agentic business workflows in production at any named enterprise.** Every enterprise case study (Klarna, Asana, BBVA, PwC) describes chatbot/copilot usage: Q&A, drafting, summarizing, research assistance. Not multi-step autonomous agents completing business processes end-to-end.

2. **No evidence of business teams building shared agentic GPTs.** Custom GPTs are being built, but for Q&A and content generation — not for autonomous workflow execution across business systems.

3. **No evidence of GPT Store being used inside enterprises for business process automation.** The Store exists but is dominated by consumer/prosumer use cases. No enterprise adopter has published results from deploying Store GPTs for business workflows.

4. **No evidence that ChatGPT Agent mode is being used for business tasks beyond research/browsing.** All reviewed tests and user reports describe web research, comparison shopping, and report compilation. Nobody is running business processes (invoice approval, contract review, compliance checks) through ChatGPT Agent.

5. **No evidence of a promotion path from personal GPT to enterprise agent.** A business user who builds a useful Custom GPT cannot promote it to a governed, audited, team-level agent with identity management and access controls.

6. **No Nordic-specific enterprise deployment evidence.** Despite Klarna being Swedish, the published evidence describes global deployment, not Nordic-specific patterns.

## Orientation

### How This Changes the Picture

**The gap between consumer adoption and enterprise agentic capability is the story.** ChatGPT is by far the most widely adopted AI tool (900M weekly users, 5M business seats). But adoption ≠ agentic. The overwhelming usage pattern is still chatbot/copilot: ask a question, get an answer, copy-paste it somewhere.

**ChatGPT Agent mode is impressive but irrelevant for enterprise business processes.** It operates in a sandboxed browser that can't access the user's business systems. A sales person can use it to research a prospect on the web, but cannot use it to update Salesforce, draft a proposal in the company template, and schedule a follow-up — all from one instruction.

**The structural architecture makes team/company agents nearly impossible today:**
- Single-player tool (no shared agents or workflows)
- Read-only data connectors (can't write back to business systems)
- No agent identity management
- No audit trails suitable for compliance
- No workflow orchestration across tools

**For our framework (personal → team → company):**
- **Personal:** Strong. ChatGPT is the best personal AI assistant available. Writing, research, analysis, coding — all work well. But this is copilot, not agent.
- **Team:** Weak. ChatGPT Teams/Business adds admin controls and shared GPTs, but collaboration is superficial (read-only link sharing). No team-level agents.
- **Company:** Non-existent via ChatGPT product. Requires developer path (Agents SDK / Responses API) which is a completely different product. No business user can build a company-level agent.
- **Promotion path:** Broken. A personal GPT cannot become a team agent cannot become a company agent. Each level requires starting over with different tools.

**The pricing cliff is a barrier to experimentation.** 40 agent messages/month on Plus means business users can barely test agent mode before hitting the wall. The jump to Pro ($200/mo) is hard to justify for experimentation. This creates a "demo but can't deploy" dynamic.

**Model retirement velocity creates operational risk.** Two rounds of model retirement in one month (Feb 13 and Mar 11, 2026) means any enterprise investment in prompt engineering or Custom GPT tuning is inherently fragile.

**Implication for our training:** ChatGPT is what every participant already uses. The training opportunity is showing them the gap between "I use ChatGPT for Q&A" and "I need agents that execute business processes." ChatGPT is the familiar starting point — but our training must show why it's not the destination for agentic work.
