# Usage-Based Pricing Churns Your Small Customers. Here's the Data.

_Practitioner essay · "the counter-case"_

Usage-based pricing is the darling of the infrastructure-SaaS world, and for
companies selling to other engineering teams, it works. But there's a survivor-
ship bias in the playbooks: the case studies are Snowflake and Twilio — vendors
whose customers are sophisticated buyers with predictable, high-volume usage.

**For SMB, the story inverts.** Small customers have *volatile* usage and
*loss-averse* budgets. A spiky month produces a bill they didn't plan for, and
"unpredictable software cost" is one of the strongest churn predictors in
small-business SaaS. The mental accounting matters more than the average price:
a customer who would happily pay €1,000/month flat will churn over a €1,400
month they didn't see coming, even if last month was €700.

**The floor doesn't fully save you.** A floor protects *your* revenue, not the
customer's *peace of mind*. The churn driver is variance and surprise, not the
average. Caps help, but a cap that's generous enough to prevent bill shock is
usually low enough to give back most of the upside you moved to usage to capture.

**The honest conclusion:** usage-based is a great fit for high-volume, low-
volatility, sophisticated accounts. It is a *poor* fit for volatile SMB. If your
pilot cohort is SMB-heavy, expect the mid-market to love it and the small,
spiky accounts to revolt — and don't average those two into a single "it works"
verdict. Segment the decision, or you'll ship a model that wins the demo and
loses the cohort.
