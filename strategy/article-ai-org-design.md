# How I'd Approach AI Organisation Design

**Status:** Rough cut v2 — reshuffled around the four-step sequence
**Voice:** First person, Antti. Practitioner, not professor.

---

## The Draft

A company asked me about reorganising for AI. They'd found a framework. Proper intellectual heritage. Topologies, mandates, intelligences. A map of where you are and where you should be.

My answer disappointed them.

The sequence isn't: design the org, then deploy AI. It's the opposite.

### 1. Build competence

Everything starts here. Not strategy. Not structure. Competence.

Put people in front of AI tools. Not a demo. Not a two-hour awareness session. Hands on. Building real things for real work. Every person I've trained who actually built something with an agent came out thinking differently about their job. That shift doesn't happen in workshops. It doesn't happen in org design sprints. It happens when someone builds an agent that does in twenty minutes what used to take them a week.

At one company, after two training modules, people started building — dashboards, agents, applications. Nobody assigned them. Nobody told them which processes to automate. The competence itself created pull. Domain experts found better candidates for AI in a week than any consultancy assessment would find in three months. Because they know the work.

This is what every org design framework skips. You can't design an AI-ready organisation when nobody in the room has built an agent. Previous transformations — digital, agile, cloud — were vision-led. A CEO didn't need to write code to envision a digital company. AI agents are different. "An agent that autonomously processes invoices across three systems with human escalation at decision points" means nothing to someone who hasn't built one. They hear "automation" and think RPA.

Without hands-on experience, your org design is based on what you imagine AI can do. Which is shaped by whichever vendor presented last.

Even Shopify — the company famous for the top-down AI mandate — did competence first. Farhan Thawar, their VP Engineering, revealed that Shopify had 80% Copilot adoption before Tobi Lütke's famous memo. The culture changed before the mandate. The memo accelerated something already built. Competence created the pull. The memo gave it permission.

Klarna did the opposite. CEO mandate, no infrastructure underneath. Cost-first, not competence-first. Quality collapsed. "The Klarna Effect" became the cautionary tale. Even their CEO admitted cost "seems to have been a too predominant evaluation factor."

### 2. Discover what makes you win

This is the part no framework can give you. What makes your company win with AI is not the same as what makes the company next door win. It depends on your data, your processes, your people, your customers. It is ruthlessly specific.

And you can't discover it by thinking about it. You discover it by building.

The experiments reveal the possibilities. The possibilities reveal the strategy. The real strategy — not the workshop output — emerges from action.

Most companies reaching for org redesign actually have a strategy problem. They think they have an OKR problem — the cascade doesn't work, teams aren't aligned, objectives are vague. But OKRs can't cascade choices that haven't been made. Roger Martin is right: strategy is choosing where to play and how to win. If those choices are fuzzy, no structure fixes the fuzz. No org topology fixes it either.

The hard truth: you cannot know where to play and how to win in AI until your people have built enough to show you what's possible. The choices emerge from the competence. Not the other way around.

Mike Fisher — CTO of Etsy, now CEO of MyFitnessPal — put it well: financial considerations should function as guardrails early, not goals. The Wright brothers didn't model the economics of aviation before solving controlled flight. They solved the problem, and the economics followed.

Same with org design. Solve the competence problem first. The strategy reveals itself. The org design follows.

### 3. Get insanely good at that specific thing

Now it gets serious. You've built competence. Your people have discovered which processes benefit, what data matters, where AI changes the game. You've found the thing that makes you win.

Pour everything into it. This is where learning rate becomes the moat.

Tools and connectors commoditise. Models improve every quarter. The durable advantage is how fast your organisation learns to do the thing that matters — for your specific company, your specific customers, your specific competitive position.

A company that wins through AI-powered customer service needs completely different capabilities than one that wins through AI-powered product discovery. The org shape that serves one would cripple the other. Designing the shape before knowing the thing is buying furniture before seeing the flat.

This is also where org design finally earns its place. Not as a prerequisite. As an accelerator for something you've already proven. You know what you're getting good at. Now you can ask: what structure makes us get good at this faster? That's a question worth answering. "What structure makes us ready for AI?" is not. It's too abstract to be useful.

### 4. The org shape adapts

The org design implications emerge from the building. They don't need to be imposed from above.

When people build, they hit walls. Data access. Deployment platforms. Governance. Security. These are real, specific, grounded needs. Not theoretical topology shifts. Real people saying "I need access to Salesforce data for my agent" or "where do we run these apps we've built?" or "who approves this before it touches a customer?"

Those questions are solvable, scoped, and reversible. They don't require board-level reorganisation. They require practical leadership that unblocks practical people.

The org adapts to what competent people need. Not to what a framework says it should become.

### Why the old playbook doesn't work here

Every org design framework makes the same assumption: your strategy is right, your structure is wrong. Fix the boxes and the strategy executes better.

It's a flattering assumption. Leadership loves hearing "your thinking is fine, your org chart is the problem." It means the hard work is structural, not intellectual. Hire the consultants, run the workshops, redraw the topology.

This is what happened with agile at scale. SAFe became the bureaucracy it replaced. LeSS stayed niche. The Spotify model didn't work at Spotify. Thirty years of "design the structure and behavior follows." The results are in.

Now the same playbook is being sold for AI. "First design, then AI." The map looks different. The logic is identical. And the logic has a thirty-year track record of producing new org charts with the same people thinking the same way.

### The culture question

There's a temptation to copy Silicon Valley. Founder memos. "Everyone codes." Builder-hero culture.

Try that at a 5,000-person company with consensus culture and works councils.

But the substance underneath the swagger is real and universal: people who've touched the technology make better decisions about it. That's not cultural. That's epistemological.

At one company, the AI transformation is led by SVP HR. Not a builder-CEO. A people leader who built a pathway that works with the existing culture. Training, not mandates. Pull, not push. Co-creation, not edicts. It produced the same result: competence created pull, people built things, the org started adapting.

You don't need Silicon Valley's style. You need the mechanism. Experience changes minds. Changed minds change organisations.

### The plain answer

Nobody can tell you what your AI-ready organisation looks like. Not a framework. Not a consultancy. Not a book with a 2x2 matrix. Not me.

The diagnostic tools are useful. "Where are we today?" is a good question. But the theory of change — that you can design your way to a better organisation — isn't supported by evidence. Not for agile. Not for digital. Not for AI.

What is supported by evidence: build competence, discover what makes you win, get insanely good at it, let the shape follow.

It's less comfortable than a redesign programme. There's no Gantt chart. No target operating model.

But it produces organisations that actually know what they're doing with AI. Because they learned it by doing, not by diagramming.

The best strategies emerge from action, not analysis.
