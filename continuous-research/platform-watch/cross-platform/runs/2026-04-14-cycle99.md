## Findings

### Finding 1 — The 33-Point Gap: Quantified Survey Evidence
**Source:** https://www.productfocus.com/product-management-amplified-part-1-the-33-point-gap/ — [domain trade publication / practitioner survey]
**Date:** 2026
**What:** The 2026 Survey of the Product Management Profession (N=677, 40 countries, conducted by Product Focus UK) found 97% of PMs report AI improved personal productivity, but only 64% say it improved product outcomes — a 33-point gap. PMs with all five unspecified "organizational conditions" in place were over 5x more likely to report significant outcome improvement (44% vs. 9%). Only 8% of respondents had all five conditions in place.
**Evidence level:** Level 3 — convergence (large independent survey, not vendor-sourced, 677 practitioners across 40 countries)
**Note:** The five conditions are published in a follow-on article ("It's Not Just the AI. It's the Organisation.") — follow-up fetch needed next cycle.

### Finding 2 — John Cutler: "AI Does Not Fix the Funnel. It Reveals It."
**Source:** https://cutlefish.substack.com/p/tbm-415-demand-mix-shaping-and-ai — [practitioner direct]
**Date:** 2026 (TBM 415)
**What:** Cutler argues directly that AI amplifies existing organizational dysfunction rather than correcting it: "If you keep the same mental model, AI will not clean anything up. It will accelerate the dynamics you already have." On demand mix specifically: if the system is built around managing scarcity and controlling access to engineering capacity, AI intensifies that dysfunction. "The key point is that AI does not fix the funnel. It reveals and amplifies whatever version of the funnel you are already operating." He diagnoses the root cause as demand mix and intake structure, not tooling.
**Evidence level:** Level 1 — practitioner opinion (Cutler is a product systems thinker with cross-company visibility, but this is his analysis, not a controlled study)
**Significance:** This is the clearest direct practitioner statement yet on the mechanism. Closest we've found to naming the root cause explicitly.

### Finding 3 — Teresa Torres: Discovery Habits Don't Scale When Skipped
**Source:** https://www.producttalk.org/my-2026-roadmap/ — [practitioner direct]
**Date:** 2026
**What:** Torres acknowledges the organizational reality: "Many teams won't make time for [discovery] and AI can still play a role in helping them learn about their customers." But she also warns: "When we let AI do this work for us, we are giving something up. Some of the value of the work is the actual doing of the work." Her 2026 focus is splitting between AI tools that teach discovery habits and AI tools that automate discovery — implying she sees both paths in use, with the automation path carrying risk.
**Evidence level:** Level 1 — practitioner direct (Torres coaches product teams at scale; her observation reflects patterns across many teams, but no data cited)

### Finding 4 — Feature Factory Problem: Mechanism Named, No Named Companies
**Source:** https://blog.suryas.org/p/feature-factory-problem-ai-amplifies — [practitioner analysis]
**Date:** 2026
**What:** Argues AI accelerates *building velocity* faster than *validation velocity*, creating dangerous mismatch. "You can ship faster. But are you learning faster?" Notes that in B2B patterns, users don't churn immediately — features go unused and customer success teams field confusion about feature purpose. Prescribes measuring "validated learning per sprint" rather than features shipped. No named companies cited.
**Evidence level:** Level 1 — practitioner opinion, no primary data
**Significance:** Useful framing (building velocity vs. validation velocity) but no case evidence. Gap in practitioner record confirmed.

### Finding 5 — Shreyas Doshi: Product Sense as the Upstream Filter
**Source:** https://shreyasdoshi.substack.com/p/why-product-sense-is-the-only-product — [practitioner direct]
**Date:** 2026
**What:** Doshi argues product sense is "the only product skill that will matter in the AI age" — the judgment layer that determines what gets built. He does not address what happens when that judgment layer is weak or missing. The essay assumes the filter works; it doesn't examine the failure mode (building the wrong things faster). Useful as the positive case for what closes the gap, not as evidence that the gap exists.
**Evidence level:** Level 1 — practitioner opinion

### Finding 6 — PwC 2026: 56% of CEOs Report No Financial Return from AI
**Source:** https://www.pwc.com/us/en/tech-effect/ai-analytics/ai-predictions.html — [general press / analyst survey — bare facts only]
**Date:** 2026 (PwC Global CEO Survey)
**What:** 56% of CEOs achieved no financial return from AI. Only 12% reported both revenue and cost benefits. This is consistent with the output-outcome gap pattern but is a CEO survey — it measures perception of ROI, not causal analysis of why.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 supporting — large-sample survey, but from a consultancy (PwC), not independent research. Confirms the gap exists at scale; does not diagnose root cause.

## What I Looked For But Did Not Find

- **Named company accounts of "we were building the wrong things faster and here's what we changed."** No practitioner has published this story with specifics. The gap in the practitioner record is real — confirmed by this cycle. Cutler comes closest but is writing systemically, not from a named deployment.
- **Teresa Torres or Shreyas Doshi directly naming discovery/intent quality as the root cause of outcome stagnation.** Torres addresses the risk but not the failure pattern. Doshi doesn't address the failure mode at all.
- **Counter-evidence of AI genuinely improving product outcomes at the discovery/intent level.** All counter-evidence found (Walmart logistics, BMW quality inspection, JPMorgan document processing) is operational AI — process automation with measurable outputs — not product discovery/development. This is the operational vs. strategic AI gap in one search result set.
- **The five organizational conditions** from the Product Focus 33-point gap study. Promised in a follow-up article — fetch next cycle.

## Orientation

The 33-point gap now has quantified survey support (Level 3: 677 practitioners, 40 countries, independent research firm). The mechanism is named by Cutler at Level 1: AI reveals and amplifies existing organizational dysfunction, particularly in demand mix and intake structure. The specific root cause — "we are building the wrong things" — is being circled by practitioners but not yet stated as a named case study. The operational/strategic split in counter-evidence is itself a signal: AI is demonstrably improving outcomes in operational domains (logistics, QA, document processing) but the product development discovery layer — where intent, customer understanding, and demand shaping happen — shows the gap. The next move is finding the five conditions from the Product Focus follow-up article and searching for teams that explicitly changed their discovery practice in response to AI-accelerated output. That is the practitioner story still missing.
