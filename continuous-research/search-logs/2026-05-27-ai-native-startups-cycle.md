### Harvey: First Commissioned-but-Independent Outcome Study Published (Nov 2025)
**Source:** https://legaltechnology.com/2025/12/02/the-impact-of-legal-ai-a-deeper-dive-into-the-rsgi-harvey-adoption-report/ — [domain trade publication]
**Date:** 2025-12-02
**Agent level:** company
**What:** RSGI (the UK research firm behind FT Innovative Lawyers) surveyed 40 Harvey customers — 29 law firms, 11 corporate legal teams across 5 regions — commissioned by Harvey but structured to be independent. Power users save 36.9 hours/month; standard users save 15.7 hours. In-house power users save 28.3 hours, standard users 11.8 hours.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single study, Harvey-commissioned, methodology partially disclosed, named participants including A&O Shearman, Orrick, Paul Weiss, Mayer Brown — credible list)
**Key claims:**
- 36.9 hrs/month savings for law firm power users (self-reported via survey, not independently audited)
- 40 customers in study; named firms include Am Law 100 members
- Study commissioned by Harvey but RSGI claims editorial independence — structure resembles Level 2, not 3
- Time savings are productivity-input claims, not outcome claims (no billing data, client satisfaction, error rates)
**Caveat:** "Commissioned by Harvey on the basis it would be entirely independent" is not the same as independent. No third-party audit of methodology. Treat as strong Level 2.

### Harvey Launches Open-Source Legal AI Benchmark (LAB) — May 2026
**Source:** https://www.lawnext.com/2026/05/some-thoughts-on-harveys-launch-of-lab-an-open-source-long-horizon-benchmark-for-legal-ai-agents.html — [domain trade publication]
**Date:** 2026-05-06 (Artificial Lawyer), commentary ~2026-05-19 (LawNext)
**Agent level:** company
**What:** Harvey released LAB (Legal Agent Benchmark), an open-source eval framework with 1,200+ tasks across 24 practice areas graded against 75,000+ expert-written rubric criteria. Framed as a tool for law firms to evaluate vendors rather than rely on demos. Contributed to by Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Nvidia, Stanford LIFTLab.
**Evidence level:** Level 0 on outcomes (vendor initiative, not deployment data). Strategically significant: Harvey is attempting to own the evaluation standard.
**Key claims:**
- 1,200+ benchmark tasks, 75,000+ rubric criteria
- Multi-lab collaboration signals Harvey is trying to become evaluation infrastructure, not just a product
- LawNext commentary (Bob Ambrogi, practitioner-analyst) notes this is a strategic move to lock in evaluation framework before competitors can define it
**Implication:** If law firms adopt LAB for procurement decisions, Harvey controls the terms of competition. Legora and Luminance would have to optimize to Harvey's rubric.

### Salesforce Agentforce: 12% Customer Penetration, 31% Six-Month Active Rate
**Source:** https://www.salesforceben.com/salesforce-says-agentforce-is-booming-the-community-isnt-so-sure/ — [practitioner analysis — Salesforce Ben is independent Salesforce community analyst]
**Source 2:** https://techhq.com/news/salesforce-agentforce-enterprise-agentic-ai/ — [general press]
**Date:** 2026 Q1-Q2 (Salesforce Ben community analysis); Q4 FY2026 earnings (Feb 2026)
**Agent level:** company
**What:** Salesforce reports 29,000 Agentforce deals closed, $800M ARR (+169% YoY), 50% QoQ growth in deals. But independent analysts estimate only ~12% of Salesforce's 150,000+ customers have Agentforce deals, and Valoir research found only 31% of deployments remain active after 6 months. Community skepticism on Salesforce Ben contrasts with official bullishness.
**Evidence level:** Level 2. Valoir is an independent analyst firm (not Salesforce). The 31% six-month active rate is a specific claim with a named source — key finding.
**Key claims:**
- 29,000 Agentforce deals (Salesforce Q4 FY2026 earnings — vendor, Level 0)
- $800M ARR (vendor, Level 0)
- 12% penetration of customer base (Stifel Research — analyst, Level 1-2)
- **31% of deployments active after 6 months (Valoir Research — independent analyst, Level 2)**
- This 31% active rate directly parallels the AI SDR churn pattern and Klarna reversal
**Implication:** Incumbent platforms are scaling deals faster than Harvey or Sierra, but the 31% active rate suggests significant "shelfware" risk. Salesforce has distribution advantage; activity rates may improve as the product matures.

### AI SDR Category: 50-70% Churn Confirmed, Clay Anomaly — Enrichment vs. Outreach Distinction Holds
**Source:** https://www.saastr.com/the-wave-of-ai-agent-churn-to-come-prompts-are-portable/ — [practitioner analysis — SaaStr is practitioner-focused publication]
**Source 2:** https://www.clay.com/blog/100m-arr — [vendor press release]
**Date:** 2026 (SaaStr); 2026 (Clay blog)
**Agent level:** company
**What:** SaaStr article diagnoses AI agent churn as structural: buyers refuse multi-year contracts because underlying models improve quarterly — what's state-of-the-art today is commoditized in six months. AI SDR tools churn 50-70% annually. Clay, however, reports never having churned an enterprise customer with >200% net revenue retention. Clay's model is enrichment + data, not autonomous outreach — this distinction explains the anomaly.
**Evidence level:** Level 2-3 converging. The 50-70% churn is cited across multiple independent sources. Clay NRR >200% is vendor-disclosed but specific and verifiable.
**Key claims:**
- 50-70% AI SDR annual churn (multiple sources, approaching Level 3)
- Only 2% of AI SDR implementations survive past year 1 (single source, unverified methodology — treat as Level 1)
- Clay: 0 enterprise customer churn, >200% NRR (vendor, but specific and checkable)
- Clay reached $100M ARR in ~2 years (vendor-confirmed)
- 41% of enterprise B2B teams have at least one AI SDR in production (Q1 2026, source unclear — [UNVERIFIED — NO URL])
**Implication:** Clay's survival and growth in a high-churn category confirms the enrichment/outreach distinction from previous cycles. Enrichment (data + context) retains; autonomous outreach (action without human review) churns. This is a durable pattern.

### Klarna: CEO Admits AI Overreach, Rehiring Now Underway
**Source:** https://fortune.com/2025/05/09/klarna-ai-humans-return-on-investment/ — [general press]
**Source 2:** https://www.entrepreneur.com/business-news/klarna-ceo-reverses-course-by-hiring-more-humans-not-ai/491396 — [general press]
**Date:** 2025-05-09 (Fortune), updated coverage into 2026
**Agent level:** company
**What:** Klarna CEO Sebastian Siemiatkowski publicly admitted "we went too far" in replacing ~700 CS workers with AI. Customer satisfaction deteriorated on complex interactions; projected cost savings did not fully materialize. Klarna is now piloting an Uber-style blended model with flexible remote human agents. Rehiring already underway by early 2026.
**Evidence level:** Level 2. CEO admission is a primary source. No independent audit of CSAT or cost data, but the admission itself and rehiring action are observable facts.
**Key claims:**
- ~700 positions eliminated 2022-2024 replaced by AI
- CSAT deteriorated on complex/emotional service interactions (CEO admission, unquantified)
- Blended model (Uber-style flexible humans + AI) now being piloted
- This represents a documented public reversal — the most credible signal of AI CS limits from a named enterprise
**Implication:** Klarna is the most-cited case of AI CS overcorrection. The new model (AI for tier-1 volume, humans for escalation/emotional complexity) is becoming the industry template. Level 3 when combined with the broader pattern across CS deployments.

Consolidation is structured as talent deals and technology licenses, not traditional press-release M&A.
**Evidence level:** Level 1. Warning from named executive is qualitative signal. Acquisition activity is factual.

- Two startup models flagged: pure LLM wrappers and AI aggregators

- Acqui-hire / tech-license structure means most consolidation is invisible in press
**Implication:** The AI-native startups most at risk are those without (a) proprietary training data, (b) deep workflow integration creating switching costs, or (c) network effects. Harvey (legal-specific training data + workflow depth), Clay (data moat), Decagon/Sierra (enterprise workflow integration) are safer than pure-prompt-wrapper plays.

## Orientation

The legal AI category has genuinely bifurcated: Harvey owns the US enterprise tier with Am Law 100 depth, while Legora (Swedish-born, now global) is executing a credible parallel at $5.55B and >$100M ARR — a two-horse race is forming faster than the previous cycle suggested. The most structurally important finding this cycle is the Salesforce Agentforce 31% six-month active rate: even the best-resourced incumbent is generating massive deal counts but not sustained deployment, which mirrors the AI SDR churn pattern and the Klarna reversal — the category-level problem is not adoption, it is *activation and sustained use*, and no player (startup or incumbent) has solved this yet except Clay in the narrow enrichment-only lane.
