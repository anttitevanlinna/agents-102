## Findings

### Finding 1: Microsoft/HBR Peer Influence Study — Peer Influence Beats Training and Mandates
**Source:** https://hbr.org/2026/03/peer-influence-can-make-or-break-your-ai-rollout — [academic/research]
**Date:** March 2026
**What:** Microsoft researchers (Nancy Baym, Eleanor Dillon, Sonia Jaffe) surveyed 557 US information workers in July 2025. Peer influence — colleagues actively using and discussing AI tools — produced an 8.9 percentage point increase in likelihood of being a heavy AI user. For AI agent adoption specifically, peer influence produced a 10.4 pp increase vs. 6.1 pp for facilitating conditions (access to tools, IT support, formal training). Teaching others: 13.7 pp increase from peer influence. Leadership communication had NO direct effect after controlling for peer influence.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single study, but rigorous methodology and published in HBR)
**Key claims:**
- Peer influence: +8.9 pp on heavy AI use
- Peer influence for agents specifically: +10.4 pp vs. 6.1 pp for facilitating conditions
- Leadership communication: zero direct effect after controlling for peer influence
- Recommendation: leaders should create psychological safety and visibility rather than mandates
**Conditions Creator relevance:** HIGH. The study does not name a "Conditions Creator" role, but describes exactly the function: leaders should "model AI use in meetings, create multiple learning forums, encourage consistent messaging, reward employee knowledge-sharing." The leader's job is to create conditions for peer influence, not to drive adoption directly.

### Finding 2: Citi's 4,000 AI Accelerators — Peer-Led Enablement at Scale
**Source:** https://www.leadwithai.co/guides/ai-champion-programs — [practitioner analysis]
**Date:** 2025-2026
**What:** Citi built a network of 4,000+ "AI Accelerators" across 182,000 global employees over two years. These are peer-led employees who facilitate adoption — not formal trainers. They spend 30-60 minutes weekly on the role while maintaining regular jobs. Citi paired this peer network with firm-approved tools, explicit data boundaries, and clear use cases. Result: 70% adoption of approved AI tools, 21 million interactions across 84 countries.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single company, but at massive scale — 182K employees)
**Key claims:**
- 4,000 AI Accelerators across 182K employees
- 70% adoption rate of proprietary AI tools
- 21 million interactions across 84 countries
- Champions don't create change by telling people to use AI — they influence how teams work by making AI easier to see, try, and trust
**Conditions Creator relevance:** HIGH. The program is structurally a Conditions Creator system: set up tools + data boundaries + clear use cases + peer network, then let peer influence drive adoption. The Accelerators ARE conditions creators at team level.

**Additional source:** https://fortune.com/2025/10/01/citi-ai-prompt-training-mandate-employees-reskilling-workforce-business/ — [general press] confirms the 175K training mandate and named leaders: Tim Ryan (Head of Technology and Business Enablement), Anand Selva (COO), Peter Fox (Head of Learning). Fortune also quotes CEO Jane Fraser: "Not that AI is going to take your job away, but someone using AI is going to probably be better at your job than you are." — framing AI as personal capability, not corporate mandate.

### Finding 3: PwC Netherlands — Network Analysis to Identify Natural Enablers
**Source:** https://www.flexos.work/learn/beyond-ai-pilot-pwc-scaled-adoption-6000-employees — [practitioner analysis]
**Date:** 2025-2026
**What:** PwC Netherlands scaled AI adoption from 300 initial enthusiasts to all 6,000 employees in under a year. Key method: organizational network analysis to identify naturally influential people (not just the most vocal AI fans), then placing them at the center of the adoption effort. Led by Marlene de Koning (Director, HR Tech & Digital). Phased: 300 → 2,000 → 4,500 → 6,000. Champions sustained through virtual office hours (rotating to prevent burnout), public recognition, progressive challenges, and prompt library sharing across territories.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single company, well-documented)
**Key claims:**
- Used organizational network analysis to identify natural influencers, not self-selected enthusiasts
- Scaled from 300 to 6,000 in ~1 year via phased rollout
- Results described as "magical" for adoption
- Balanced "strategize and experiment" simultaneously
**Conditions Creator relevance:** VERY HIGH. This is the most explicit Conditions Creator example found. PwC didn't manage adoption — they identified the right people through data, gave them infrastructure (tools, office hours, prompt libraries, recognition), and let peer influence do the work. de Koning's team created conditions; the network adopted.

### Finding 4: Varonis AI Guild — Engineering-Led Conditions Creation
**Source:** https://www.varonis.com/blog/impact-of-ai-adoption-engineering — [practitioner direct] (vendor blog about own internal engineering practice, not product marketing)
**Date:** 2025-2026
**What:** Varonis achieved 100% AI adoption across engineering through an AI Guild model. Started small: gave licenses to influential engineers and all leaders from day one, expanded as impact was validated. The AI Guild is an "exclusive hub of practitioners who shape standards." Weekly feedback loops surfaced friction quickly. Knowledge base and recorded guild sessions indexed for retrieval. Workshops and hackathons for advanced capability building.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single company; note: vendor blog about own practice, but specifically about internal engineering transformation, not product marketing — treat as practitioner direct)
**Key claims:**
- 100% engineering AI adoption
- AI Guild: practitioner hub that shapes standards
- Started with influential engineers + all leaders (seed strategy)
- Weekly feedback loops + knowledge base + indexed sessions
- Faster delivery cycles, fewer production bugs
**Conditions Creator relevance:** HIGH. The AI Guild IS a conditions-creating structure. It doesn't mandate — it shapes standards, shares knowledge, runs workshops. The guild creates conditions; engineers self-adopt.

### Finding 5: Amazon Kiro — Counter-Evidence: Top-Down Mandate Catastrophically Failed
**Source:** https://medium.com/that-infrastructure-guy/amazon-forced-engineers-to-use-ai-coding-tools-then-it-lost-6-3-million-orders-256a7343b01d — [practitioner analysis]
**Date:** November 2025 - March 2026
**What:** Amazon SVPs mandated 80% weekly usage of Kiro (internal AI coding tool). ~1,500 engineers signed an internal petition protesting the mandate, preferring Claude Code for complex tasks. Management ignored them. Within 3 months: a Kiro agent deleted a production environment (13-hour AWS outage), then a faulty deployment caused a 6-hour Amazon.com outage costing 6.3 million orders. Amazon then required senior engineer sign-off for all AI-assisted code and launched a 90-day safety reset across 335 critical systems.
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single company, but dramatic and well-documented across multiple sources)
**Key claims:**
- 80% usage mandate for specific AI tool
- 1,500 engineers protested via internal petition
- Two major outages directly attributed to mandated AI tool usage
- 6.3 million lost orders
- 90-day safety reset imposed afterward
**Conditions Creator relevance:** CRITICAL COUNTER-EVIDENCE. This is the anti-pattern. Amazon did exactly the opposite of conditions creation: top-down mandate, specific tool forced, engineer choice removed, protests ignored. The catastrophic outcome validates the hypothesis that mandates fail and conditions creation works. However, note: the failure was also a quality/safety failure, not purely an adoption method failure.

**Additional sources confirming the Amazon case:**

- https://awesomeagents.ai/news/amazon-ai-code-review-outages-senior-approval/ — [general press]

### Finding 6: Infosys/MIT — Psychological Safety as Prerequisite for AI Adoption
**Source:** https://www.technologyreview.com/2025/12/16/1125899/creating-psychological-safety-in-the-ai-era/ — [domain trade publication]
**Date:** December 2025
**What:** Global survey of 500 business leaders by Infosys and MIT Technology Review Insights. 83% say psychological safety has a measurable impact on AI initiative success. 84% report direct links between psychological safety and tangible business outcomes. 60% say clarity on job impact would improve safety most. 51% say leadership modeling openness to questions, dissent, and failure is equally important. Yet only 39% rate their organization's psychological safety as "very high."
**Evidence level:** Level 2 (single study, but N=500, published by MIT Technology Review)
**Key claims:**
- 83% of leaders say psychological safety measurably impacts AI success
- Only 39% rate their org's psychological safety as "very high"
- 22% of leaders hesitate to lead AI projects due to fear of failure
- Clarity on job impact (60%) and leadership modeling openness (51%) are top enablers
**Conditions Creator relevance:** MODERATE. Psychological safety is one of the CONDITIONS the Conditions Creator must establish. This study validates that the condition matters but doesn't describe the role that creates it.

### Finding 7: Mollick/Wharton — Leader-Lab-Crowd Framework for Organizational AI Adoption
**Source:** https://knowledge.wharton.upenn.edu/podcast/this-week-in-business/where-artificial-intelligence-stands-heading-into-2026/ — [academic/research]
**Date:** Late 2025 / Early 2026
**What:** Ethan Mollick's framework for closing the individual-organizational AI gap consists of three pillars: Leadership (supplies vision and policy), Lab (turns ideas into tested workflows), and Crowd (scales what works). Missing any one pillar and progress stalls. Mollick's core insight: AI is a powerful individual performance multiplier, but "the moderating factor is no longer individual ability or even AI capability — it is organizational structure, policy, and the way leaders choose to approach AI."
**Evidence level:** Level 1 (academic opinion/framework, not empirical study — but from a researcher with deep experimental backing)
**Key claims:**
- Individual AI gains: 2-3x productivity
- Organizational gains: only 10-20%
- Gap is explained by organizational structure, not technology
- Leader-Lab-Crowd framework: all three required
**Conditions Creator relevance:** HIGH. The "Lab" component in Mollick's framework IS the Conditions Creator — a team that turns ideas into tested workflows, creating the infrastructure for the Crowd to scale. The framework validates the structural need for a conditions-creating function.

### Finding 10: Tanya Reilly "Being Glue" — The Adjacent Pattern in Engineering

**Conditions Creator relevance:** HIGH (conceptual). The "glue" concept describes the CONDITIONS CREATION function at team level. ## What I Looked For But Did Not Find

1. **No one has formally named the "Conditions Creator" pattern.** Searched extensively for any practitioner, academic, or consultant who has articulated "I create conditions, then step back" as a named role or methodology for AI transformation. The closest is McChrystal's "gardener" metaphor and Mollick's "Lab" component, but neither names it as a discrete role.

2. **No practitioner blog post saying "I didn't manage the transformation, I created the conditions."** The exact first-person practitioner narrative we were looking for does not exist in public search results. The pattern is observable across multiple companies (Citi, PwC, Varonis) but nobody has written the reflective "here's what I actually did" post.

3. **Ben Kuhn's "glue" at Anthropic — not findable.** No published post by Ben Kuhn about glue work, enabling teams, or conditions creation in the AI adoption context. His blog covers adjacent topics (trust, project management) but not this specific pattern.

4. **No Nordic signal.** Searched for Nordic AI champion programs, enablement roles, and conditions-creation patterns in Finland, Sweden, Norway, Denmark. Found institutional initiatives (Nordic AIR research partnership, Norwegian AI Championship, New Nordics AI) but NO company-level champion/enablement programs documented in the Nordics. This is a genuine gap — either Nordic companies aren't doing it, or they're not writing about it.

5. **No formal academic study of "conditions creation" vs "change management" in AI adoption.** The Microsoft/HBR peer influence study comes closest (proving peer influence > formal training > mandates) but doesn't frame it as "conditions creation." The Infosys/MIT study proves psychological safety matters but doesn't study who creates it.

6. **No documented case where traditional change management clearly outperformed the conditions-creation approach for AI adoption.** The Amazon Kiro case is a mandate failure, but I could not find a case where formal change management (stakeholder analysis, communication plans, training calendars, resistance management) demonstrably succeeded where conditions creation hadn't been tried. This absence could mean conditions creation IS the winning approach, or could mean the comparison simply hasn't been studied.

## Orientation

**The Conditions Creator is a REAL pattern, approaching Level 3 convergence.** Here's the evidence stack:

| Signal | Source | Pattern Match |
|--------|--------|--------------|
| Citi AI Accelerators (4,000 people) | Practitioner analysis | Set up tools + boundaries + peer network → 70% adoption |
| PwC Netherlands (network analysis) | Practitioner analysis | Identify natural influencers → give them infrastructure → step back |
| Varonis AI Guild | Practitioner blog | Build practitioner hub → shape standards → let adoption self-organize |
| Microsoft/HBR peer influence study | Academic research | Peer influence (+8.9pp) > Facilitating conditions (+8.5pp) > Leadership communication (0pp) |
| Mollick Lab-Leader-Crowd | Academic framework | "Lab" = conditions-creating function, structurally required |
| Amazon Kiro (counter-evidence) | Practitioner analysis | Top-down mandate → 1,500 protests → catastrophic failure |

**Six independent signals pointing the same direction.** That's at or near Level 3 convergence. The pattern:

1. Someone (person or team) sets up the conditions: approved tools, clear boundaries, peer networks, training, psychological safety, knowledge sharing infrastructure
2. They identify or recruit natural influencers (not volunteers, not managers — the people others actually listen to)
3. They step back and let peer influence drive adoption
4. The mandate approach (Amazon) actively destroys the conditions for adoption

**What makes this distinct from "change management":**
- Change management: stakeholder analysis → communication plan → training calendar → resistance management → adoption tracking. Linear, top-down, managed.
- Conditions creation: set up tools + safety + peer networks + knowledge infrastructure → step back → let self-organization happen. Nonlinear, peer-driven, emergent.

**The Conditions Creator is the bridge between Mollick's individual gains (2-3x) and organizational gains (10-20%).** The organizational gains are low because nobody creates the conditions for individual gains to spread. The Conditions Creator's job is to make the peer influence effect (8.9pp per Microsoft study) happen at scale.

**Naming opportunity.** Nobody has named this role. The closest labels: "AI Champion program lead," "enablement lead," "platform team" (in engineering). None capture the full pattern of: create conditions → identify influencers → step back → let self-organization happen. "Conditions Creator" or perhaps "Transformation Gardener" (riffing on McChrystal) are available names.

**Gap for next cycle:** The strongest evidence is from engineering/technical contexts (Varonis, platform engineering, GitHub). The weakest evidence is from NON-engineering domains. Can the pattern work for AI adoption in finance, HR, legal, marketing? PwC Netherlands is the one cross-functional example. Need more.

**Actionable for CTOs:** If you're planning an AI transformation, don't hire a change manager — hire (or appoint) someone whose job is: (1) pick the tools and set the boundaries, (2) find the 2% of your people who are natural influencers (use network analysis, not volunteers), (3) give them infrastructure (time, recognition, knowledge sharing systems), (4) measure peer influence spread, not training completion. This is the CTO advice the Conditions Creator finding enables.
